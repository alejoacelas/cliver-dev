# Measure 14 — Identity Evidence Match: Product Prioritization

## Selected stack

### 1. Jumio Identity Verification (`m14-jumio`)

Selected as the primary vendor IDV. Jumio offers the broadest document coverage (5,000+ subtypes across 200+ countries), claims Kantara-listed IAL2 certification under NIST SP 800-63-3, and provides the full evidence-match pipeline: document capture, OCR, template authentication, NFC chip read on ePassports, biometric face match, and active+passive liveness detection. Its Liveness Premium product explicitly defends against injection attacks (camera emulators, deepfake video feeds), which the vendor reports have surged 9x in 2024. The interface contract is clean: a single `scanReference` per transaction, structured JSON callback with pass/fail verdict, extracted identity fields, face-match similarity, liveness result, and coded rejection reasons. At $1.50-$3.50 per verification, the cost is competitive. The manual review handoff is well-specified via the Jumio Customer Portal. Jumio must be configured with Liveness Premium mandatory for SOC orders, and the provider must implement a transliteration-aware name-matching layer to control the 8-15% non-Latin-script false-positive rate.

### 2. NFC ePassport chip read (`m14-nfc-epassport`)

Selected as a hardening layer within the Jumio IDV flow. NFC chip reading is the only check in the set that defeats face morphing on genuine document substrates — the chip portrait is CSCA-signed and cannot be altered without invalidating Passive Authentication. This directly closes the morph-attack detection gap that all three vendor IDV ideas (Jumio, Onfido, Persona) flag as AMBIGUOUS. The marginal cost is near-zero ($0-$0.50 incremental, bundled with the Jumio Document report), and setup is limited to enabling NFC in the Jumio SDK configuration. Coverage is incomplete (10-20% of customers lack an ePassport, 5-10% lack NFC devices), so the provider must define an explicit enforcement policy: hard-require NFC for SOC orders where an ePassport is available, accept OCR-only with compensating controls otherwise. The chip portrait also provides a cryptographic ground-truth reference for the biometric face match, strengthening the selfie comparison.

### 3. FIDO2/WebAuthn order-time step-up (`m14-fido2-stepup`)

Selected as the order-time binding layer. FIDO2 step-up closes the single most important bypass shared by all vendor IDV ideas: ATO-inherits-prior-IAL2. Even if an attacker has the victim's password and session, they cannot produce a valid FIDO2 assertion without the physical authenticator registered at onboarding. This makes it the cheapest possible re-proofing mechanism ($0 marginal cost, no third-party API), phishing-resistant by protocol design, and it creates a durable audit trail linking customer, order, credential, and timestamp across all orders. It catches IDV-session handoff exploits and blocks email-only ordering. The main deployment challenge is the enrollment campaign (20-40% of customers will need onboarding) and shared-lab accounts (10-25% of academic orders), which require per-user account mandates or accepted degradation. The provider should accept synced passkeys (platform authenticators) for coverage while tracking the `credentialBackedUp` flag for risk assessment.

## Dropped ideas

- **Onfido (Entrust Identity Verification):** Functionally equivalent to Jumio with a slightly narrower document coverage (2,500 vs. 5,000 subtypes), higher Motion capture failure rate on low-end devices (5-10% vs. 3-7%), and no meaningful differentiation that justifies running two vendor IDV integrations in parallel. The `compromised_document` check is a modest differentiator but does not justify the second integration's complexity.
- **Persona:** Strong configurability and a useful `selfie_unique` within-tenant dedup check, but lacks Kantara-listed IAL2 certification — a compliance gap that increases misconfiguration risk and regulatory exposure. Database verification coverage degrades outside OECD (15-30% of international customers lack cross-reference). Better suited as an alternative if Jumio contract terms are unacceptable.
- **Stripe Identity:** PAD/liveness assessment is opaque (no ISO 30107-3 conformance level published), making it inadequate as the primary IDV for SOC orders. The synthesis itself recommends dual-vendor escalation to Jumio/Onfido for SOC orders, which means Stripe adds a $1.50 pre-screen layer without reducing the need for a certified vendor. Adds cost and complexity without incremental assurance.
- **Federated IAL2 (Login.gov / ID.me):** 40-60% of global customers have zero coverage (non-US/UK structural exclusion). Login.gov is likely unavailable to private-sector providers. Collapses to ID.me only, which is more expensive ($2-$5/check), black-box (provider cannot inspect failures), and carries FTC consent action reputational risk. Cannot serve as a primary path; marginal value as an optional convenience lane does not justify the integration cost.
- **Cross-tenant biometric dedup:** Most expensive idea in the set ($2-$8/check vendor-hosted, $100K-$300K in-house setup) with the narrowest incremental value: catches only same-person multi-identity enrollment at the same provider. The Critical surviving finding — that the cro-identity-rotation attacker story explicitly defeats within-tenant dedup by routing across providers — means the check fails against its own primary use case. Cold-start problem (1,000+ templates needed), BIPA/GDPR consent blockers (20-40% of customers), and demographic bias risk further reduce practical value. Persona's `selfie_unique` check provides a lighter-weight partial substitute within the selected Jumio flow if the provider later adds Persona as a fallback.

## Composition note

The three selected ideas form a layered stack addressing document authenticity, biometric binding, and order-time re-authentication:

- **Onboarding flow:** Customer completes Jumio Identity Verification (document + selfie + Liveness Premium). If the customer presents an ePassport and has an NFC-capable device, the Jumio SDK performs the NFC chip read automatically — Passive Authentication validates the CSCA chain, and the chip portrait (DG2) becomes the ground-truth reference for the face match. After successful IAL2 verification, the customer enrolls a FIDO2 authenticator (platform authenticator or hardware key) bound to their account. The provider stores the Jumio `scanReference`, structured verification result, and the WebAuthn credential public key.
- **SOC order flow:** At order submission, the portal demands a fresh FIDO2 assertion (`userVerification: required`). This proves the same human who completed onboarding IDV is placing this order — no re-running the full Jumio flow on every order. If the FIDO2 assertion fails or the customer has no enrolled credential, the order is blocked and the customer must complete fresh IAL2 re-proofing plus authenticator enrollment.
- **Flag aggregation:** Flags from all three layers (`jumio_*`, `nfc_*`, `webauthn_*`) route to a single reviewer queue. The reviewer sees the Jumio verification record (document images, selfie, rejection codes, NFC PA/AA/CA results) alongside the FIDO2 assertion status. A single review resolves the measure per order.
- **Key policy requirements:** (1) Liveness Premium is mandatory for all SOC-order IDV sessions. (2) NFC is hard-required when the customer presents an ePassport on an NFC-capable device; OCR-only is accepted with a downgraded-assurance flag otherwise. (3) FIDO2 re-enrollment after authenticator loss requires fresh IAL2 re-proofing plus dual-approval from support — never self-service via email/SMS. (4) The provider must implement transliteration-aware name matching between Jumio-extracted fields and the account record to control non-Latin-script false positives.
