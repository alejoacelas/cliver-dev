# Product Prioritization — Measure 15: SOC Self-Declaration

## Selected stack

### 1. Structured SOC declaration form (m15-structured-form)

The structured form is the foundation of the entire M15 stack and a precondition for every other selected idea. Without it, m15-llm-extraction has no structured fields to cross-reference against free text, m15-screening-reconciliation has no typed organism or use-category to compare against vendor outputs, and m15-drift-detector has no feature vector to track over time. The form itself is not primarily a detection layer — it is a data-capture layer that forces machine-checkable records into existence. Its pluggability is strong: it is an internal HTML form with no external vendor dependency beyond the free NCBI Taxonomy API for organism validation. Interface clarity is high — controlled vocabularies with enumerated values, auto-resolved taxon IDs, and deterministic validation rules produce unambiguous flags (`bsl_inconsistent`, `select_agent_no_ibc`, `taxid_invalid`). Marginal cost per order is $0. The form's standalone detection value is limited to catching uninformed attackers who cannot fill domain-specific fields correctly (unrelated-dept-student, inbox-compromise), but its value as infrastructure for the rest of the stack is irreplaceable. The main operational risk is escape-hatch abuse (10–25% of submissions using `other/unknown`), which should be mitigated by adding per-field triggers on load-bearing fields (intended-use category and source organism) rather than relying solely on the 3+ field `excessive_other` threshold.

### 2. Sequence-screening / declaration reconciliation (m15-screening-reconciliation)

The reconciliation engine is the highest-leverage detection layer in the M15 stack because it compares customer claims against an independent physical signal — what the sequences actually are. When a customer writes "molecular biology research" while ordering a select-agent gene, the vendor screening output identifies the SOC and the reconciler surfaces the disagreement. This is the core M15 cross-check described in the measure definition ("verify customer declaration aligns with output of sequence screening"). It addresses five attacker stories directly and its false positives, while meaningful (7–9% benign select-agent gene matches), produce flags that are substantively reviewable by a biosafety-trained reviewer. Multi-vendor reconciliation (SecureDNA at $0 plus one or two commercial vendors) adds coverage-gap detection that no single vendor provides. Composability is excellent: reconciliation consumes the structured form's typed fields and can incorporate the LLM extraction's inferred fields as a third signal. The dominant cost is reviewer time on the 7–9% benign-gene FP rate, not per-check API fees (SecureDNA is free; commercial vendors are $0.10–$2/sequence at enterprise tier). Setup cost of 4–8 engineer-weeks reflects the three-vendor integration. The short-sequence gap (<200 bp, potentially 20–40% of order lines) needs a minimum-length enforcement policy.

### 3. LLM extraction + deterministic cross-reference (m15-llm-extraction)

The LLM extraction layer closes the gap between the structured form and the free text customers actually write. Customers routinely provide project descriptions, biosafety summaries, and end-use notes in unstructured prose; the LLM extracts a canonical SOC declaration object from this text and a deterministic comparator diffs it against both the structured form and the screening output. This three-way comparison (form vs. LLM extraction vs. screening) is materially stronger than any two-way check: it catches vague declarations that satisfy the form's enum selection but contradict the project description, and it catches exemption claims that the form cannot challenge but that the free text reveals. Marginal cost is very low ($0.005–$0.02 per order). The `extracted_quotes` field gives reviewers a fast fidelity check against hallucination. Composability is the key strength: the LLM extraction adds almost no cost but materially increases the signal surface for reconciliation and drift detection. The main risk is the terse-submission gap (15–30% of orders where extraction yields nothing useful), which limits the check to a manual-review funnel for those orders. An extraction-accuracy evaluation benchmark is needed before deployment.

### 4. Cross-order SOC drift detector (m15-drift-detector)

The drift detector is the only idea in the M15 stack that operates across orders rather than within a single order, making it the sole defense against the gradual-legitimacy-accumulation attacker story. Its composability score is high: it consumes the structured form's feature vector, the LLM extraction's inferred fields, and the screening reconciliation's flag history to build a per-customer trajectory. Marginal cost is $0 (internal stream processor). The Critical structural blind spot — inability to detect attackers who are consistent from the start (8 of 14 stories) — is acknowledged and acceptable because those stories are covered by the per-order checks above. The cold-start gap (30–50% of accounts with <10 orders) means the detector adds no value for new customers, but new customers are already covered by the per-order stack. The dominant operational challenge is the false-positive rate from legitimate research pivots (10–20% of PI accounts per 2-year window); positioning the detector as a contributor to a composite risk score rather than a standalone trigger keeps reviewer burden manageable. Setup cost of 4–8 engineer-weeks is reasonable given that it depends on infrastructure from the structured form and reconciliation engine already being in place.

## Dropped ideas

- **IBC / sponsor-PI attestation upload (m15-ibc-attestation):** Dropped from the default recommendation due to disproportionate false-positive burden and narrow coverage. Section III-F exempt researchers (30–60% of orders) have no IBC document to upload, creating a hard block on legitimate customers or requiring an undefined exemption path. Foreign institutions (~40–50% of global market) have no equivalent registry, reducing the check to unverified document-on-file. The $5–25 per-check human review cost is the highest in the M15 stack. The idea is sound for providers with a predominantly US academic customer base and existing biosafety review staff, and should be reconsidered as a conditional add-on for SOC orders involving select agents or BSL-3+ organisms, where the IBC gate is already a regulatory expectation rather than a new burden.

## Composition note

The recommended stack executes in two phases. **Phase 1 (per-order, synchronous):** The structured form captures typed fields at order time; sequence-screening vendors run in parallel; the LLM extraction processes any free text. **Phase 2 (per-order, post-submission):** The reconciliation engine diffs the three inputs (form, LLM extraction, screening output) and generates flags (`soc_screening_disagreement`, `llm_form_mismatch`, `llm_sequence_disagreement`). Any high-severity flag routes to the scientific reviewer queue. **Phase 3 (cross-order, asynchronous):** The drift detector updates the per-customer feature vector and runs change-point detection; `concerning_trajectory_match` flags route to the priority queue. The structured form is the shared data contract: its JSON schema defines the canonical SOC declaration object that all three downstream ideas consume. The LLM extraction and screening reconciliation are independently valuable but strongest in combination — the three-way diff catches inconsistencies that any two-way comparison misses. The drift detector layers on top as a longitudinal signal that compounds in value as order history accumulates. IBC attestation can be added later as a conditional gate for high-risk SOC categories without disrupting the stack's architecture.
