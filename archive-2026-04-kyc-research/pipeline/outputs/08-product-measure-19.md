# Measure 19 — Individual Legitimacy (SOC): Product Prioritization

## Selected stack

### 1. OpenAlex author + affiliation history lookup (`m19-openalex-author`)

Selected as the primary bibliometric signal. OpenAlex is the broadest open bibliometric source (114M authors, CC0 license, free API with $1/day credit sufficient for screening volumes) and provides the richest structured data: affiliation history with ROR IDs, h-index, topic tags, and citation metrics. It subsumes most of what PubMed provides while adding author disambiguation, institutional linkage, and research-area classification that PubMed lacks. The `openalex_topic_mismatch` flag feeds directly into the role-vs-scope SOP. The `openalex_affiliation_mismatch` flag is the most actionable negative signal among the bibliometric checks. Coverage gaps are real — non-publishing staff (~15-25%), industry researchers (~40-60%), early-career researchers (~10-20%) — but these are shared by every publication-based check and are handled by treating absence as non-denial. The preprint-seeding bypass ($0 cost, one bioRxiv preprint creates a valid record) is a genuine weakness, but it requires the attacker to produce a traceable artifact, and it is partially mitigated by a minimum-footprint threshold (works_count >= 3) that should be calibrated against false-positive rates on early-career researchers. Setup cost is 2-4 engineer-weeks, the largest among selected ideas, but this is a one-time investment for the single highest-coverage bibliometric check.

### 2. ORCID employment + education record lookup (`m19-orcid-employments`)

Selected for the institution-verified affiliation signal — the strongest single-field individual-level evidence available when it fires. An institution-asserted employment (where the institution's HR system pushed the assertion via its ORCID member integration, identifiable by `source-client-id`) confirms the person is currently employed at the claimed institution. This is extremely difficult to fabricate externally. The critical limitation is that only ~2% of ORCID records carry institution-verified affiliations; for the other 98%, the check degrades to a self-asserted corroboration no stronger than the order form itself. The check is still worth including because: (a) when the strong signal fires, it is near-conclusive and eliminates the need for further individual-level corroboration; (b) the `orcid_recent` flag catches impatient manufactured personas; (c) the ORCID iD is a stable, citable identifier with strong audit value; and (d) the marginal cost is $0 with ~1 engineer-day setup. The check composes naturally with OpenAlex — an ORCID iD dramatically improves OpenAlex disambiguation, and OpenAlex provides the corroboration needed when ORCID produces only a self-asserted signal.

### 3. NIH / NSF / Wellcome / ERC / UKRI PI lookup (`m19-nih-nsf-pi`)

Selected for the highest-trust positive signal in the stack. A confirmed PI record on a major government funder is created through competitive grant-award processes with institutional verification — it is the hardest individual-level credential to fabricate. The check's value is not coverage breadth (60-75% of order-placers are not PIs) but signal strength: when `pi_record_present` fires, it is near-conclusive evidence of a legitimate research role at a verified institution. The check's most valuable negative use is claim contradiction — a customer who explicitly claims PI status but has no funder record across five databases is substantively suspicious. All five sources are free, require no authentication, and the setup cost is 1-2 engineer-days. The check composes well: it provides the strongest positive signal for senior researchers, while OpenAlex and ORCID cover the broader population. The ORCID-based disambiguator should be required (not optional) to defend against the dormant-domain name-collision attack.

### 4. Role-vs-scope / seniority alignment SOP (`m19-role-vs-scope`)

Selected as the human-judgment integration layer that consumes signals from all upstream automated checks. This SOP is the point where the reviewer synthesizes OpenAlex topics, ORCID affiliations, PI records, and sequence-screening flags into a decision about whether the customer's claimed role is plausible for the order's scope. It directly targets the role-scope mismatch and junior-as-front attacker patterns that no automated check can catch independently. The load-bearing step — contacting the claimed PI via independently verified contact information — provides real friction for fabricated supervisory relationships. The SOP triggers only on the ~5% of orders flagged by sequence screening, keeping the cost manageable ($50-$300 per flagged order, ~$0.50-$15 amortized). The critical implementation requirement is that PI contact information must be independently sourced (institutional directory, not customer-provided). Setup cost is ~$5k-$15k for playbook authoring and reviewer training. The SOP cannot catch role-authentic insiders (lab managers, recruited insiders), but this is a structural boundary of individual-legitimacy checks, not a gap that another M19 idea could fill.

### 5. Faculty / lab page + institutional directory (`m19-faculty-page`)

Selected as the non-bibliometric web-presence check that covers a different evidence channel from the publication-based checks. While OpenAlex, ORCID, and PI lookups all operate on structured academic databases, the faculty-page check searches the institution's own website for the customer's name in a research role. This provides independent corroboration that does not share the same data pipeline as the other checks, making cross-source agreement more meaningful. The `faculty_page_recent_only` flag (Wayback-based freshness check) provides a targeted signal against the dormant-domain story. The `faculty_page_directory_mismatch` flag catches IT-manufactured personas who appear in directories but not on lab pages. Cost is low (~$0.01-$0.015/check, ~1 engineer-day setup). The Google PSE sunset (Jan 2027) is a hard dependency requiring migration to Bing Web Search or a self-hosted alternative — this should be planned immediately. The Wayback pre-aging bypass is a known weakness; cross-referencing Wayback snapshot timestamps against domain WHOIS data (from m18) partially mitigates it.

## Dropped ideas

- **PubMed + Scopus cross-source lookup (`m19-pubmed-scopus`):** PubMed adds no discriminating power beyond OpenAlex for individual-legitimacy purposes — it shares all structural limitations (non-publishing populations invisible, preprint seeding defeats it) while offering weaker disambiguation (no native author IDs) and narrower scope (biomedical only, ~70.9% of publications). Scopus requires a vendor-gated commercial license (likely low-to-mid five figures USD/year) for near-zero incremental bypass detection over the free sources. The synthesis document itself recommends evaluating whether PubMed should be "a separate implementation or folded into the OpenAlex check as a secondary query." PubMed's MeSH headings could add value for topic-mismatch detection, but OpenAlex `topics[]` and `x_concepts[]` already provide this. If a second bibliometric cross-validation source is desired, PubMed can be added later as a lightweight secondary query within the OpenAlex check flow at ~1 engineer-day cost.

- **ClinicalTrials.gov + FDA BIMO investigator (`m19-clinicaltrials-investigator`):** Structurally null for 70-85% of synthesis customers (basic-science researchers), plus trainees, industry scientists, and non-US researchers. Coverage is too narrow to justify a standalone implementation. The check's positive signal for clinical/translational investigators is genuine but affects a small minority of orders. If a customer claims clinical-trial work, the reviewer can perform an ad-hoc ClinicalTrials.gov search within the role-vs-scope SOP rather than building a dedicated automated pipeline. Can be reconsidered if the provider's customer base skews heavily toward clinical researchers.

## Composition note

The five selected ideas form a layered evidence model with clear roles:

- **Layer 1 — Broad bibliometric signal (OpenAlex):** The primary automated check, applied to every SOC order. Produces affiliation match, topic distribution, and publication metrics. Absence is non-denial; presence is strong positive. Approximately 40-60% of academic customers will produce a usable match.
- **Layer 2 — Identity anchor (ORCID):** Provides a stable identifier and, for the ~2% with institution-verified affiliations, near-conclusive positive evidence. For the other 98%, it improves OpenAlex disambiguation via ORCID-linked author resolution and produces a self-asserted signal that requires Layer 1 corroboration.
- **Layer 3 — High-trust positive signal (PI lookup):** Fires for ~25-40% of senior researchers. When present, it is the strongest positive evidence in the stack. The claim-contradiction mode (customer claims PI status, no record found) is the strongest negative signal.
- **Layer 4 — Independent web-presence check (faculty page):** Searches a different evidence channel (institutional websites) from the bibliometric databases. Cross-source agreement between Layers 1-3 and Layer 4 is more meaningful than agreement among bibliometric sources alone. Also provides freshness detection via Wayback.
- **Layer 5 — Human synthesis (role-vs-scope SOP):** Consumes all upstream signals and applies human judgment to the role-scope plausibility question. Triggers only on sequence-screening-flagged orders (~5%). The PI-verification step (contacting the PI via independently sourced contact information) is the single most adversary-resistant mechanism in the entire stack.

**Decision logic:** A customer who produces a strong positive from any two of Layers 1-4 proceeds without Layer 5 review (unless sequence screening flags the order). A customer who produces only weak or null signals across Layers 1-4 and whose order is sequence-screening-flagged enters the Layer 5 SOP. A customer who claims PI status and contradicts all funder databases (Layer 3) is escalated regardless of other signals.

**Shared coverage gaps:** All five ideas are structurally silent on role-authentic insiders, authentication-layer attacks, and industry researchers without academic footprints. These are not M19 gaps to fill — they require behavioral analytics (outside M19 scope), authentication controls (M16), and institutional-level verification (M18/M20) respectively. The ~20-40% of customers with no ORCID and no publication record represent the irreducible population where M19 individual-legitimacy checks provide weak signal and institutional-level checks (M18, M20) must carry the burden.
