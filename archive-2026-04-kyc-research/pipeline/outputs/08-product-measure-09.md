# Measure 09 — Institution Real Life Sciences: Product Prioritization

## Selected stack

### 1. Corporate registry stack (`m09-corp-registry-stack`)

Selected as the baseline. This is the foundational check for M09: it answers the primary question — does the institution legally exist? — by querying Companies House (UK), SEC EDGAR (US public), and OpenCorporates (all other jurisdictions). The input contract is clean (legal name + jurisdiction in, incorporation status + SIC/NAICS + registered address + officers out), and its four flags (`registry_no_record`, `registry_dissolved`, `sic_not_life_sciences`, `registry_recent_incorp`) map directly to the measure's purpose. The `registry_dissolved` flag is the only auto-block in the M09 suite and catches the direct-reuse branch of the dormant-domain story. Cost is $0–$1.50 per check depending on jurisdiction routing. The check has structural blind spots — government labs and universities (~39% of customers) are absent from corporate registries — but these are covered by companion checks. Every other M09 idea either depends on corp-registry output (registered-agent-denylist consumes the registered address and agent name) or complements it (PubMed covers the life-sciences relevance question that SIC codes answer poorly). This must be built first.

### 2. PubMed affiliation search (`m09-pubmed-affiliation`)

Selected for life-sciences relevance coverage. While the corp-registry stack answers "does this entity legally exist?", it answers "is this entity in life sciences?" only weakly (SIC/NAICS codes are self-reported and frequently misclassified). PubMed affiliation search directly addresses the second half of the measure's purpose: a multi-year publication record affiliated with the institution is expensive and time-consuming to fabricate, making it one of the strongest positive signals in the suite. The check is free ($0 marginal via NCBI E-utilities and bioRxiv API), composable with every other idea, and covers the academic segment (~39% of market) where corp-registry produces no signal at all. Its main weakness — uninformative for CROs and industry customers who do not publish — is exactly the population where the corp-registry stack is strongest. The two checks are complementary by design: corp-registry covers commercial entities, PubMed covers academic and research institutions, and the intersection (established biotech with publications) provides the highest-confidence pass signal. The `no_pubmed_affiliation_5yr` flag must be treated as a soft signal requiring corroboration, not a standalone gate, given that 30–50% of legitimate commercial customers never publish.

### 3. Domain authentication stack (`m09-domain-auth-stack`)

Selected for dormant-domain coverage and shell-company friction. The baseline DNS + RDAP check is free and adds a time-dimension signal (`domain_recent`, `domain_reanimated`) that neither corp-registry nor PubMed provides. Its strongest contribution is against the dormant-domain story: `domain_reanimated` detects post-gap re-registration or recent transfers, catching attackers who acquire the lapsed domain of a defunct research entity — a scenario where the corp-registry might show dissolved (caught) but could also show a newly re-filed entity (only partially caught). For shell-company and CRO-framing stories, `domain_recent` provides early friction during the first 90 days. The check composes cleanly with corp-registry: a cluster of `registry_recent_incorp` + `domain_recent` + `no_pubmed_affiliation_5yr` is the canonical shell-company signature. The `domain_no_mail_auth` flag has an ~80% base rate on small domains and must be heavily down-weighted or used only in combination. DomainTools enrichment ($15.75/query) should be reserved for escalation cases where `domain_reanimated` fires or other M09 signals cluster. Setup cost is only ~2 engineering days.

## Dropped ideas

- **ClinicalTrials.gov + FDA registration (`m09-clinicaltrials-fda`):** Provides high-confidence positive corroboration when it fires, but structurally produces no signal for 60–80% of academic customers, 70–85% of small biotech, and 100% of community labs. The `no_ctgov_no_fda_registration` flag is non-diagnostic. At $0 cost and 1–2 days setup it is harmless to add later, but its narrow coverage (clinical/translational pipeline only) means it adds negligible marginal value over the selected stack for the initial build.

- **Registered-agent / virtual-office denylist (`m09-registered-agent-denylist`):** Highest false-positive rate of any M09 idea — the mass-formation agent flag fires on 30–50% of legitimate new US small businesses (LegalZoom has served 4M+ businesses). Near-zero standalone discriminative power. Its only useful signal (CMRA + mass-formation agent combination) is dominated by the corp-registry + domain-auth cluster, which catches the same shell pattern with lower noise. Ongoing denylist curation (1–2 days/quarter) is not justified by the marginal signal. US-only coverage (30–50% of market excluded) further limits value.

- **IRS Form 990 cross-check (`m09-irs-990`):** Applicable to ~10–20% of synthesis customers at best (US 501(c)(3) nonprofits with 990 filings). Seven of nine attacker stories use for-profit structures, making this check structurally inapplicable to the dominant threat surface. Within its narrow scope, the `990_program_not_life_sciences` flag is genuinely informative, but the population it covers is small enough that it should be deferred until after the core stack is operational. Free to run, so worth adding as a conditional check in a later phase.

## Composition note

The three selected ideas share a common input (customer-stated institution name, jurisdiction, and domain) and compose into a layered assessment:

- **Execution order:** Corp-registry runs first to establish legal existence, status, and registered address. PubMed affiliation and domain-auth run in parallel, as neither depends on the other's output. All three complete before the measure-level result is computed.
- **Signal combination:** The measure produces a composite assessment from three signal classes: legal existence (corp-registry), research activity (PubMed), and domain legitimacy (domain-auth). A pass on all three is the strongest positive signal. The canonical shell-company signature is `registry_recent_incorp` + `domain_recent` + `no_pubmed_affiliation_5yr` — any two of three should trigger escalation to manual review. The `registry_dissolved` flag remains the only auto-block.
- **Population coverage:** Corp-registry covers commercial entities (for-profit companies, CROs). PubMed covers academic and research institutions. Domain-auth provides a universal time-dimension signal across all customer types. Together, the three checks produce at least one meaningful signal for an estimated 80–90% of the customer base. The remaining gap (government labs not in registries, not publishing in PubMed, using .gov domains that trivially pass domain-auth) requires separate handling.
- **Reviewer handoff:** The reviewer receives a single measure-level summary: registry match (status, SIC, incorporation date), PubMed hit count and sample papers, and domain signals (age, mail-auth, transfer history). Disposition follows a decision tree: dissolved → auto-block; all-clear across all three → auto-pass; any flag cluster (2+ flags from different checks) → manual review with the five-case playbook from corp-registry as the anchor, supplemented by PubMed and domain context.
- **Marginal cost:** $0.25–$1.50 per customer (dominated by OpenCorporates), with DomainTools escalation adding $15.75 for flagged cases. PubMed and baseline domain-auth are free. Total setup: ~8–12 engineering days across all three checks.
