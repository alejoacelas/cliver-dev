# Product Prioritization — Measure 08: Institution Denied Parties

## Selected stack

### 1. BIS Entity List + Consolidated Screening List (m08-bis-entity-csl)

The CSL is the foundation layer and a non-negotiable inclusion. It is free, pluggable (a single REST endpoint with no vendor negotiation), and covers the 13 US restricted-party lists that any US-jurisdiction synthesis provider is already legally obligated to screen against. Its interface is clean and publicly documented: fuzzy name search, structured JSON response with score, source list, address, and Federal Register notice. The marginal cost is $0 per query with ~$5-10k one-time setup. False-positive burden is manageable through score thresholds and address-country cross-checks. Coverage gaps — non-US sanctions, subsidiary ownership graphs, transliteration misses — are real but well-characterized, and the first of these is addressed by the commercial layer below. The CSL also provides a bulk-download option for airgapped operation, which strengthens resilience. Every other M08 idea layers on top of this one; it cannot be dropped.

### 2. Internal institution denylist (m08-internal-denylist)

The internal denylist is the only M08 idea that compounds in value over time and is the only one that captures provider-specific intelligence (prior denials, bypass attempts, compliance referrals). It scores high on composability: its beneficial-owner cross-referencing closes a structural gap in both the CSL and commercial vendors, and its multi-identifier matching schema (name + registry ID + domain + payment-instrument hash + BO) provides a richer signal surface than any external list. Pluggability is strong — it is an internal system with no vendor dependency. Marginal cost per check is effectively $0 (index lookup). The $25-75k setup cost is a one-time investment. The cold-start problem (Gap 1) is real but unavoidable for any retrospective mechanism, and the denylist becomes more valuable precisely as a provider accumulates screening history from the CSL and other measures. Cross-provider federation via IGSC would be transformative but is legally blocked and should be tracked as a future opportunity, not a launch dependency.

## Dropped ideas

- **Commercial PEP / entity watchlist (m08-commercial-pep-watchlist):** Dropped as a default recommendation due to disproportionate cost ($5k-$300k+/yr subscription) and false-positive burden (~30-60% of alerts) relative to incremental coverage over the free CSL. No wg attacker stories engage this measure, so the added multi-jurisdiction sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media intelligence are regulatory hardening, not threat-model coverage. The idea is sound for large providers with globally diverse customer bases and dedicated compliance teams, but the cost barrier makes it prohibitive for an estimated 50-70% of providers by count. Providers who need non-US sanctions coverage at lower cost should consider OpenSanctions as a lighter-weight supplement to the CSL rather than a full commercial AML subscription.

## Composition note

The recommended stack is CSL-first, denylist-second, run in parallel on every order. The CSL screen executes as a synchronous API call (or bulk-download lookup) and produces flags (`csl_entity_hit_high_confidence`, `csl_entity_hit_low_confidence`, `csl_alt_name_hit`, `csl_uvl_hit`). The internal denylist executes as a local index lookup and produces flags (`internal_denylist_hit_high_confidence`, `internal_denylist_hit_medium_confidence`, `internal_denylist_name_only_collision`, `internal_denylist_beneficial_owner_match`). Any high-confidence flag from either source is a hard block routed to compliance review. The denylist's write path is fed by denial decisions from the CSL screen and from other measures across the pipeline — every denial from any measure should result in a denylist entry, making the denylist a cross-measure memory layer. For providers seeking to close the non-US sanctions gap (CSL Gap 1) without a commercial subscription, OpenSanctions (open-source, CC-BY-SA, multi-jurisdiction) can be added as a third parallel screen at minimal cost, though its data quality and update cadence are less rigorous than commercial vendors.
