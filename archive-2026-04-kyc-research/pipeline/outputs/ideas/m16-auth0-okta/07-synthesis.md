## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check uses a hosted identity provider — Auth0 (Okta Customer Identity Cloud), Okta Workforce, or AWS Cognito — to enforce multi-factor authentication with step-up for SOC orders. When the synthesis provider's sequence-screening pipeline identifies a Sequence of Concern in an order, the order pipeline triggers a step-up authentication flow: the customer is redirected to the IdP-hosted login and must complete a fresh FIDO2/WebAuthn passkey challenge within a tight time window (e.g., 5 minutes). The step-up produces an `auth_time` timestamp and an `amr` claim (authentication methods reference) that the provider verifies before allowing the order to proceed. Adaptive risk scoring evaluates signals such as geo-velocity, device fingerprint, and IP reputation. The entire MFA lifecycle — enrollment, factor changes, authentication events — is captured in the IdP's audit log, providing a complete record of who authenticated, when, with what factor, and from what device.

### What it catches

The check defeats the most common and cheapest account-takeover methods. AitM phishing relays (Tycoon 2FA, EvilProxy) are blocked because FIDO2 passkeys bind the challenge to the relying party's origin — a proxy site cannot complete the WebAuthn handshake. Infostealer-exfiltrated TOTP seeds are useless when the policy enforces passkey-only for SOC step-up. SIM-jacking and SS7 interception are irrelevant when SMS is not accepted as a factor. Email-channel MFA reset attacks are blocked because the recovery flow requires IDV re-proofing (via m14-stripe-identity), not just an email link. FIDO2 downgrade attacks (spoofing browser UA to force TOTP/SMS fallback) fail because the policy blocks fallback factors entirely. Grace-period timing exploits fail because step-up is per-order with a tight `auth_time` window. Dormant-account takeover via federated IdP re-enrollment or MFA policy relaxation is blocked by the requirement for a provider-side passkey independent of federation. In total, the check catches 8 of 12 mapped bypass methods.

### What it misses

The primary remaining attack is cloud account compromise enabling synced passkey injection. If a customer uses Apple iCloud Keychain or Google Password Manager to sync their passkey, an attacker who compromises the customer's cloud account can add a new device to the sync group and receive the passkey — the provider sees a valid WebAuthn assertion with no distinguishing signal. The implementation does not distinguish synced from hardware-bound passkeys. Social engineering of provider support is the second gap: the IDV-required playbook exists, but voice cloning, PII from compromised inboxes, and institutional authority can fool a support agent who deviates from procedure. The implementation does not specify mandatory delays, dual-agent approval, or callback verification for support-initiated MFA resets. Adaptive risk signal specificity is undefined — which specific signals Auth0/Okta/Cognito check and whether they can detect anti-detect browsers with residential proxies is not documented.

### What it costs

Marginal cost per SOC step-up is near-zero with passkey or TOTP (no per-authentication charge). SMS-based step-up adds $0.01–$0.04 per attempt via AWS SNS. The platform cost depends on monthly active users and the pricing tier: Auth0 B2C starts at ~$35/month for essentials and ~$240/month for professional, with FIDO2 enforcement likely requiring Professional or Enterprise. AWS Cognito's three-tier model charges by feature level rather than per-MFA event. Setup cost is 4–8 engineer-weeks for OIDC integration, step-up flow wiring, FIDO2 enrollment UX, and order-pipeline hook. The highest-cost operational item is passkey enrollment support for existing customers at rollout — with 30–60% of customers potentially lacking passkeys, the enrollment support burden could be significant during the transition period.

### Operational realism

The reviewer's role is to handle lockouts and recovery. When a customer fails the step-up (3 attempts) or has no passkey enrolled, the reviewer initiates an out-of-band enrollment ceremony — typically a video call confirming identity followed by an enrollment link. For lost-device recovery, the reviewer follows a playbook requiring IDV re-verification via the provider's existing identity proofing vendor. The audit trail is comprehensive: the IdP logs every enrollment, factor change, and authentication event with device fingerprint and risk score; the provider logs which order triggered which step-up and the outcome. The idea's leverage is entirely policy-dependent — the IdP is the delivery mechanism, and coverage depends on what the provider mandates (passkey-only vs. TOTP fallback, tight `auth_time` windows, IDV-gated recovery). The policy choices determine whether this is a genuine barrier or a checkbox.

### Open questions

The synced-vs-hardware passkey policy is the most consequential unresolved question. Hardware security keys provide the strongest security (resistant to cloud account compromise) but impose the highest friction (customers must carry a physical device). Syncable passkeys are more convenient but introduce the cloud-account-compromise attack vector. The anti-social-engineering controls for support-initiated MFA resets need specification: mandatory delay (48–72h), dual-agent approval, out-of-band callback, and notification to registered backup contact. The adaptive risk signal set and threshold need enumeration. The `mfa_recent_enroll` 48h cooling period is a heuristic and its tuning rationale is undocumented. The scope of provider-side passkey enforcement (SOC-only or all orders) is not fully specified for federated-identity customers.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** All findings were Moderate or Minor.
- **Moderate finding M1 — Synced passkeys vulnerable to cloud account compromise.** Synced passkeys (iCloud Keychain, Google Password Manager) can be injected by an attacker who compromises the customer's cloud account. Requires a policy decision: hardware-only (highest security, highest friction), synced-accepted (with compensating IDV control), or documented risk acceptance.
- **Moderate finding M2 — Social engineering of provider support bypasses procedural controls.** IDV-required playbook exists but lacks anti-SE hardening: no mandatory delay, no dual-agent approval, no callback verification. Voice cloning and institutional authority create plausible impersonation scenarios.
- **Moderate finding M3 — Adaptive MFA risk-signal specificity not defined.** Which signals are checked and whether anti-detect browsers (GoLogin/Multilogin with residential proxies) are detectable is undocumented.
- **`[vendor-gated]` fields requiring sales conversations:** Auth0 Enterprise tier for FIDO2 enforcement and exact pricing; Okta Workforce MFA add-on pricing.
