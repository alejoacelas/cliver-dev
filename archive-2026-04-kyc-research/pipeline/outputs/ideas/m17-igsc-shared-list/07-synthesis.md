## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check queries two information sources. First, the IGSC member-to-member information-sharing channel: a mechanism, described by multiple secondary sources (RAND 2024, Council on Strategic Risks 2024, PMC Screening State of Play 2024) as existing but "rarely used," through which IGSC member companies can alert each other about suspicious customers. The mechanism has no public API or structured database — it is best understood as an ad-hoc human-to-human channel (email, phone, IGSC officer relay). Second, the provider's own internal CRM, which stores the customer's prior order history, screening verdicts, and any prior law-enforcement reports. When a customer orders, the provider checks both sources: the IGSC channel for external flags and the CRM for internal history. Matches generate flags (`igsc_flagged`, `internal_prior_red_flag`) that route to the biosecurity officer for adjudication.

### What it catches

The check is designed for a specific, narrow scenario: a customer who was previously flagged as suspicious by another IGSC member, whose flag was actually shared through the channel, and who returns to a different provider with sufficiently similar identity fields to trigger a match. This is the cross-vendor-shopping and industry-known-bad use case. The internal CRM component catches repeat offenders at the same provider — customers with prior denials or law-enforcement reports whose circumstances have not changed. When the check fires, it is a strong signal: a bilateral confirmation between the biosecurity officer and the originating IGSC member liaison provides high-confidence intelligence.

### What it misses

Stage 5 classified all 14 bypass methods across all seven mapped attacker stories as MISSED. The check provides zero discriminatory signal against any of them. This is structural, not an implementation deficiency. The seven attacker stories are all designed to either manufacture a clean history (gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, shell-company, biotech-incubator-tenant), inherit a clean history (account-hijack, credential-compromise, dormant-account-takeover), or operate from within a pre-approved institutional account (bulk-order-noise-cover). In all cases, the IGSC channel returns null (no prior flag exists for a first-time attacker) and the internal CRM shows a clean or inherited-clean record. Stage 6 confirmed that >99% of new customers receive no signal from this check, the channel's under-utilization means >80% of warranted alerts may never be filed, and identity drift defeats 30–50% of deliberate cross-vendor evaders.

### What it costs

The IGSC channel is $0 incremental beyond membership dues (which are unknown but estimated at $5–50K/year for an industry consortium). The internal CRM lookup is <$0.01 per check. The real cost is in the human-to-human operational workflow: when an IGSC flag fires, the biosecurity officer must contact the originating member liaison, confirm the flag is still active, learn the originating concern, and make a disposition decision. This workflow is appropriate for the rare cases where it fires but is not scalable if adoption increases significantly.

### Operational realism

The check's operational reality is dominated by null results. For the rare case where a flag fires, the 6-step SOP is concrete and defensible: pull the IGSC alert and CRM record side-by-side, determine exact vs. fuzzy match, escalate to the biosecurity officer for bilateral confirmation with the originating member, and log the decision. The audit trail (IGSC liaison contact log, biosecurity-officer memo, CRM diff) satisfies third-party audit requirements. The false-positive concerns are standard name-matching problems (common-name collisions, romanization variants, shared institutions).

### Open questions

Whether the IGSC channel can be operationalized more effectively is the central open question. Multiple secondary sources identify antitrust and GDPR concerns as the primary barriers to sharing customer PII between competitors. A structured database with legal safe harbor, standard data format, and anonymized identifiers would substantially increase the check's value — but this is a governance and legal problem outside any single provider's control. The IGSC membership dues amount is unknown. Whether IGSC's coverage of ~80% of synthesis capacity is sufficient, or whether parallel sharing mechanisms are needed for non-IGSC providers, is unresolved.

## Section 3: Open Issues for Human Review

- **Critical finding (stage 5):** The check provides zero discriminatory signal against any of the seven mapped attacker stories. All seven branches either manufacture or inherit clean histories. This is structural: the "flag bad actors after the fact" architecture cannot detect first-time attackers with clean records. The idea is correctly scoped for post-hoc intelligence sharing about known-bad actors; the limitation should be understood as a scope constraint, not a deficiency.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** Channel under-utilization amplifies the structural gap. The IGSC channel is "rarely used" per multiple secondary sources. Even for the narrow repeat-offender use case, most warranted alerts are likely never filed.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** Identity drift defeats fuzzy matching for serial offenders. An attacker flagged under Entity A who returns as Entity B with a different name/LLC/domain defeats the match. No concrete mitigation is available within the current field set.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** Antitrust/GDPR redaction may render IGSC payloads too thin to match on. The degree of PII sharing is a governance decision not specified in the implementation.
- **[unknown] IGSC channel interface:** No public API, portal, or documented data format. The mechanism is ad-hoc and human-to-human.
- **[unknown] IGSC channel fields returned:** No documentation of what data is exchanged between members.
- **[unknown — searched for: "IGSC membership cost", "International Gene Synthesis Consortium dues"]:** Membership dues amount unknown.
- **[vendor-gated] IGSC operational details:** IGSC publishes only the Harmonized Screening Protocol PDF and member roster — no operational sharing-channel documentation is public. Would require IGSC liaison contact to confirm actual handoff format.
- **4C flag (minor):** "Rarely used" attribution — most cleanly attributable to Council on Strategic Risks 2024, not RAND/PMC as primary source. The document attributes indirectly.
- **Stage 6 form-check flag:** Gap 3's >80% alert loss rate is a strong claim with no supporting derivation beyond the "rarely used" characterization.
- **Stage 6 form-check flag:** Gap 5 (antitrust/GDPR) overlaps significantly with Gap 3 (under-utilization) — the two could be consolidated or cross-referenced more cleanly.
- **Structural note for stage 8:** This idea is best understood as a weak supplementary signal, not a primary screening mechanism. Its value is concentrated in a tiny population (known-bad actors returning with the same identity). The internal CRM rollup is more reliable than the IGSC channel and should be evaluated independently. The idea's value would increase significantly if the IGSC channel were operationalized with a structured database, legal safe harbor for sharing, and standard data format — but this is a governance/legal problem, not an implementation problem.
