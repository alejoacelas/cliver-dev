## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The voucher IAL2 IDV check requires the voucher to complete a government-ID verification flow before being granted any vouching authority. The provider integrates with one of several IDV vendors (Jumio, Onfido/Entrust, Stripe Identity, Persona, or Login.gov/ID.me for federal partners). The voucher uploads a photo of a government-issued ID document (passport, driver's license, national ID card) and takes a live selfie. The vendor's ML models verify document authenticity, extract identity fields (name, date of birth, address, document number), perform a face-match between the selfie and the document photo, and check for passive liveness (ensuring the selfie is of a live person, not a photo of a photo). The vendor returns a structured result: PASS, REFER (requires human review), or FAIL. On PASS, the voucher's identity is bound to a real person with biometric commitment.

### What it catches

IDV is the strongest available check for binding a voucher to a real person. It eliminates fabricated voucher personas (no real person can produce a matching gov ID + live selfie), catches credential/session/inbox compromise (the attacker cannot replicate the voucher's biometrics), and blocks dormant-account takeover (the account taker lacks the original voucher's face). For shell entities, CRO rotations, and gig-platform recruitments, IDV creates a biometric attribution trail — the accomplice is now photographically identified and committed to the conspiracy, which materially raises the personal risk of participation. The per-rotation cost ($1.50–$5 plus time friction) also adds meaningful cost amplification for CRO identity rotation schemes.

### What it misses

The hardening stage identified one Critical finding: rubber-stamping by real, IDV-verified vouchers is systematically missed. IDV verifies that the voucher is a real person with a real government ID — it does not verify that the person has reviewed the SOC order, is qualified to assess its risk, or has any connection to biosafety. Every attacker branch that uses a real, inattentive voucher (PI rubber-stamping, recruited co-conspirator, gradual-legitimacy cultivation, bulk-order noise cover) passes trivially. Self-vouching (orderer = voucher) is not blocked because the implementation does not compare orderer and voucher biometrics. Cross-session biometric deduplication (same voucher appearing for multiple unrelated entities) is not specified.

### What it costs

Per-check cost is $0 for the first 50/month on Stripe Identity, then ~$1.50/check; Jumio/Onfido/Persona charge $1.50–$5/check at moderate volume (all vendor-gated for exact rates). Setup cost is 1–4 engineering weeks depending on vendor complexity, plus 1–2 legal weeks for KYC/DPA review. Reviewer time is zero for clean passes, ~10 minutes for REFER cases (estimated at 5–15% of sessions). The dominant cost is not monetary but operational: the coverage research estimates 20–40% abandonment/refusal among senior academic vouchers — the highest-credibility population — who find consumer-grade selfie verification insulting or unnecessary.

### Operational realism

The IDV flow is a standard vendor-hosted integration that users increasingly encounter in financial services. The audit artifact is strong: biometric + gov ID creates a durable investigative trail that deters rational accomplices. The critical operational tension is between identity assurance and voucher friction. Senior PIs with h-indices of 80 will balk at taking a selfie; non-OECD vouchers face 2–5x higher document rejection rates; demographic facial-recognition bias creates systematically unequal barriers (NIST FRVT documents 3x false-reject differentials). The check is best deployed as a complement to ORCID OAuth and DKIM: IDV proves the voucher is a real person, ORCID proves they control a researcher identity, DKIM proves they control an institutional email.

### Open questions

The Critical finding C1 (rubber-stamping by real vouchers) is structural to IDV as an identity check — no vendor or implementation tweak addresses per-order scrutiny. The hardening stage's Moderate findings (M1: self-vouching not blocked; M2: no cross-session biometric dedup; M3: re-verification cadence unspecified) are all implementable refinements. The 4C claim check noted that Stripe Identity's "$1.50/check" is a rough public estimate — the exact post-free rate is vendor-gated. Persona's IAL2 Kantara certification status remains unknown. The coverage research's Gap 2 false-reject differential ("2–5x" for non-OECD documents) is uncited and needs vendor-specific FRVT data.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Critical hardening finding C1 (structural, surviving):** Rubber-stamping by real, IDV-verified vouchers is systematically missed. IDV is an identity check, not an order-scrutiny check. Every real-but-inattentive-voucher pattern passes. Requires complementary per-order attestation mechanism.
- **Moderate hardening finding M1 (surviving):** Self-vouching (orderer = voucher) not blocked. Recommend comparing IDV-verified names and face embeddings between orderer and voucher; flag `voucher_orderer_same_person`.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2 (surviving):** No cross-session biometric deduplication. Same voucher can IDV for multiple unrelated entities without detection. Recommend face-match across all voucher sessions; flag `voucher_face_reuse`.
- **Moderate hardening finding M3 (surviving):** Re-verification cadence unspecified. If one-time-at-onboarding, post-onboarding account takeover bypasses IDV. Recommend annual re-IDV minimum + per-action for high-risk SOC orders.
- **Moderate hardening finding M4 (context):** Attribution/deterrence value is real but not scored as a preventive signal. Shell accomplices and gig workers are biometrically committed, which deters rational actors but does not prevent willing conspirators.
- **[vendor-gated] fields:** Stripe Identity exact post-free per-check rate; Jumio/Onfido/Persona per-check pricing (all custom-quote); Login.gov availability to non-federal partners.
- **[unknown] field:** Persona IAL2 Kantara certification status — searched but not found.
- **[unknown] field:** IDV vendor rate limits — searched for Stripe Identity and Jumio; no explicit RPS published.
- **4C claim flag (OVERSTATED — mild):** Stripe "$1.50/check" should be weakened to "post-free pricing per the Stripe pricing page."
- **Coverage Gap 1 (friction):** 20–40% senior-academic refusal rate is the dominant operational barrier. This is a design tension between identity assurance and voucher willingness.
- **Coverage Gap 2 (documents):** "2–5x higher false-reject" for non-OECD documents is uncited; needs vendor-specific FRVT accuracy data.
- **Coverage Gap 3 (demographic bias):** NIST FRVT documents 3x false-reject differentials. Vendor selection should prefer vendors with smaller demographic gaps. Documented reviewer-override policy required.
