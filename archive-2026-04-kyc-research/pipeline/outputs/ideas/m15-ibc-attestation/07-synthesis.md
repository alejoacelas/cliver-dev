## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check requires customers placing Sequences of Concern (SOC) orders to upload an Institutional Biosafety Committee (IBC) approval letter — or a signed sponsor-PI attestation — proving that an IBC has reviewed and approved the specific work. The uploaded document is processed through OCR to extract structured fields (institution name, IBC registration number, chair and Biosafety Officer names, approval scope, expiration date). The system then cross-checks the institution and named officers against the NIH IBC Registration Management System (IBC-RMS), a public roster that became available in June 2025 under NOT-OD-25-082. If the institution has no active IBC in the roster, or the chair/BSO names don't match, flags are raised for human review. A biosafety-trained reviewer examines the document for plausibility, checks scope against the order, and — if forgery is suspected — contacts the IBC chair directly using the email on the NIH roster (not the email on the document itself).

### What it catches

The check is a hard gate for attackers who lack institutional biosafety infrastructure. An unrelated-department student attempting to order SOCs under generic framing is blocked by `ibc_doc_missing` or exposed by `ibc_doc_unverified` when their forged document fails the roster cross-check. Inbox-compromise attacks that rely on exemption claims are blocked because the implementation requires a document rather than accepting bare assertions. Fabricated visiting-researcher sponsorships are exposed when the IBC chair (contacted via the roster) confirms the visitor is unknown. Dormant-account takeovers are caught via expired-document flags and PI-name mismatches. These represent meaningful coverage of the naive-exploitation and fake-affiliation branches of the attacker taxonomy.

### What it misses

Four attacker stories — cro-framing, cro-identity-rotation, shell-company, and biotech-incubator-tenant — route around the check entirely because private-sector entities often have no NIH-registered IBC. The implementation must define an "alternative evidence" path for these customers, which an attacker can satisfy with a self-authored biosafety plan. Genuine insiders (insider-recruitment) upload real IBC documents with matching scope — the malicious intent is invisible to document verification. The lift-and-modify attack (changing PI name on a real IBC letter) passes all automated checks and is caught only if the resource-intensive chair-contact step is triggered. Foreign institutions (~40–50% of the global market) have no equivalent registry, reducing the check to unverified document-on-file. Section III-F exempt researchers (potentially 30–60% of orders) have no IBC document to upload at all.

### What it costs

Marginal software cost is near-zero (cloud storage + OCR at ~$0.001/page). The dominant cost is human review: 5–15 minutes per SOC order from a biosafety-qualified reviewer, translating to $5–25 per check at $50/hr loaded. Setup cost is estimated at 3–6 engineer-weeks for the upload UI, OCR pipeline, hash store, reviewer queue, and IBC-RMS scraper/cache. The NIH roster itself is free and public.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is the core of this check's value — automated checks (hash, name-match, expiration) filter out obvious problems, but the reviewer's judgment on scope match, signature plausibility, and when to escalate to an IBC chair contact is what catches sophisticated forgeries. The chair-contact step is the strongest verification but is expensive and cannot be triggered on every order; a triage policy is needed. The reviewer leaves an auditable record: stored PDF with hash, IBC-RMS snapshot at time of review, disposition notes, and copies of any chair-contact correspondence. For deny/escalate paths, the audit trail includes the outbound email to the IBC chair.

### Open questions

The implementation does not specify whether a previously-uploaded IBC letter is accepted for subsequent orders or whether a fresh upload is required each time (cached documents favor account-takeover attackers). The scope-match heuristic is described as "fuzzy" without defining the matching granularity. The PI-name-on-document vs. account-holder cross-check is not explicitly required. The alternative-evidence path for non-NIH-funded entities is undefined. The Section III-F exemption citation (NIH Guidelines) was flagged as missing by the claim check. The size estimate for the Section III-F exempt gap (30–60%) has no citation and was flagged by the form check as load-bearing and under-sourced.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** All findings were Moderate or Minor.
- **Moderate finding M1 — Private-sector entities without NIH IBC registration bypass the gate entirely (4 attacker stories).** The alternative-evidence path for non-NIH-funded entities is undefined. Resolution requires a policy decision on what evidence substitutes for an IBC letter.
- **Moderate finding M2 — Foreign institutions have no equivalent registry (~40–50% of market).** Structural to the US-centric registry approach. Requires a different evidence standard for non-US customers.
- **Moderate finding M3 — Genuine insider attestation is undetectable.** Structural to document-based verification; not addressable within this idea alone.
- **Moderate finding M4 — Lift-and-modify attack passes automated checks.** Only the manual chair-contact step catches this; that step's trigger policy is unspecified.
- **`[unknown — searched for]` field:** NIH IBC-RMS has no documented public REST API (3 queries searched). Automation requires scraping or manual lookup. This affects scalability.
- **`[best guess]` fields requiring validation:** Section III-F exempt fraction of orders (30–60% — no citation; potentially the largest false-positive category); IBC chair rotation rate (5–10%); IBC-RMS scraping rate limits and ToS.
- **4C claim check unresolved item:** NIH Guidelines Section III-F exemption reference needs a direct citation (suggested: NIH Guidelines PDF).
- **6F form check flags:** Gap 3 (Section III-F exempt) size estimate needs stronger sourcing or explicit `[unknown]` admission; Gap 4 (chair rotation) estimate is a bare guess.
- **Minor finding N1 — Per-order vs. cached IBC upload policy unspecified** (affects account-hijack, dormant-account-takeover stories).
- **Minor finding N2 — PI-name-on-document vs. account-holder cross-check not specified.**
- **Minor finding N3 — Scope-match granularity ("fuzzy") undefined** (affects lab-manager-voucher, bulk-order-noise-cover stories).
