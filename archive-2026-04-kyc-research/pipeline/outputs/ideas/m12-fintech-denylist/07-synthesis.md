## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check maintains an internal denylist of BIN ranges belonging to known consumer-fintech and neobank issuers — Mercury (BIN sponsor: Choice Financial Group / Evolve Bank & Trust), Brex (Emigrant Bank / Sutton Bank), Relay (Thread Bank), Wise (Community Federal Savings Bank / Evolve), Revolut Business, and Bluevine. When a customer pays with a card whose BIN matches the denylist and the customer claims institutional affiliation, the order is soft-flagged for manual review. The BIN can be checked via Stripe Radar's `:card_bin:` rule attribute (if the provider uses Stripe), via a commercial BIN database like BinDB, or via the PSP's native issuer metadata. The check does not hard-block — it routes to a reviewer who asks the customer to confirm their procurement channel.

### What it catches

The check directly targets the shell-company and related LLC-based attacker branches. The attacker-story corpus explicitly names Mercury, Relay, and Brex as the standard LLC-bank-account rails for shell companies, shell nonprofits, CRO-framing entities, and similar purpose-built organizations. All seven LLC-based branches in the m12 mapping trigger this denylist. The check also catches dormant-domain Bypass B (LLC + fintech bank account) and inbox-compromise Method 3 (small-LLC business card). In each case, the check fires and creates friction: the customer must explain why a claimed research institution pays through a neobank rather than an institutional procurement system.

### What it misses

The check has two classes of limitations. First, it is structurally irrelevant to attacker branches that use traditional-bank instruments: inherited institutional billing (dormant-account-takeover, account-hijack, credential-compromise), personal credit cards from traditional banks (inbox-compromise Method 1), and cloned cards. These branches do not trigger the fintech denylist because they use non-fintech BINs. Second and more importantly, even when the check fires on LLC-based branches, the reviewer faces a fundamental signal-to-noise problem: the attacker stories use the exact same banks that legitimate small biotechs use. Mercury has over 300,000 customers; 40% of new startups have a Mercury account. An estimated 10-25% of commercial DNA synthesis customers may pay through fintech/neobank cards. The check cannot distinguish a real 5-person biotech from a shell — it can only add friction. Stage 5 classified this as a Moderate finding: the check adds real cost and delay to the attacker but does not reliably block them.

### What it costs

Runtime cost is $0-$0.07 per check, depending on whether the provider uses Stripe Radar inline rules (no marginal cost on standard pricing) or a separate BIN lookup service. Denylist maintenance is the primary ongoing cost: approximately $500-$2,000 per year in analyst time to curate and update BIN-to-fintech mappings as neobanks change BIN sponsors, plus approximately one engineer-day for initial curation. Reviewer cost depends on the false-positive rate: if 10-25% of commercial customers trigger the flag, the reviewer burden could be substantial for providers with a large small-biotech customer base. The soft-flag architecture (not a hard block) means the cost falls on reviewer labor, not on lost customers.

### Operational realism

When the fintech flag fires, the reviewer checks the customer's claimed institution type. For known universities, government labs, or large pharma, fintech billing is anomalous and the reviewer requests a purchase order or procurement-office confirmation. For small biotechs and startups, fintech billing is expected — the reviewer checks whether the company name on the fintech account matches the claimed institution. If it matches (which it will for both legitimate biotechs and shell companies), the reviewer has limited additional signal unless they consult m09/m18 institution-legitimacy outputs. The escalation path (customer cannot explain the billing mismatch) is the strongest action, but sophisticated shell operators will always have a plausible explanation. The audit trail includes the BIN lookup result, denylist match details, reviewer adjudication memo, and the denylist version at the time of the check.

### Open questions

The claim check flagged three issues. First, the binlist.net rate limit was mis-cited as 10 requests/minute when the actual limit is 5 requests/hour — making it unsuitable even for moderate-volume production use (curation only). Second, Stripe Radar pricing was overstated: on standard pricing, basic Radar is free and Fraud Teams is $0.02/transaction (not $0.05/$0.07, which apply only to custom-pricing accounts). Third, the BIN-sponsor mappings (Mercury to Choice Financial Group, Brex to Sutton Bank, etc.) are presented without citations — widely discussed in fintech forums but should carry a [best guess] marker since issuers do not officially publish all mappings. Stage 5 also identified a cross-entity correlation gap: when the same individual opens multiple LLC bank accounts at Mercury and places orders from each, the implementation processes each order in isolation rather than correlating the pattern.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** Two Moderate findings:
  - Finding 1: The fintech denylist fires on all 7 LLC-based branches but the reviewer cannot distinguish real small biotechs from shells. This is a signal-to-noise limitation, not a fixable gap. The check should be framed as "friction + composite signal" rather than standalone block.
  - Finding 2: No cross-order or cross-entity correlation — if the same beneficial owner appears behind multiple fintech-flagged entities (the cro-identity-rotation pattern), the implementation does not detect the pattern. Cross-entity linkage is a complementary control not covered by this idea.
- **Claim-check flags requiring correction (04C REVISE verdict):**
  - binlist.net rate limit: actual is 5/hour, not 10/minute (medium severity — affects feasibility assessment)
  - Stripe Radar pricing: omits standard-pricing rates ($0 basic, $0.02 Fraud Teams); document shows only custom-pricing rates
  - BIN-sponsor mappings: widely reported in fintech forums but presented without citations; should carry [best guess] marker
- **[unknown -- searched for] fields affecting policy implications:**
  - Neobank virtual card BIN ranges (searched without result; drives Gap 4 severity)
  - International fintech BIN sponsor names (acknowledged as not searched in this round; drives Gap 3 severity)
- **[vendor-gated] fields:**
  - BinDB pricing beyond free tier (requires account creation)
- **06F minor flags:**
  - Gap 1 estimate (10-25% of commercial customers) is the most consequential number and is a [best guess] with wide range
  - Gap 3 international fintech search was acknowledged as incomplete
- **Fundamental tension for policymakers:** This check targets the exact same banking rails used by legitimate small biotechs. It adds friction to shell-company operations but imposes an equivalent friction on the startup biotech ecosystem. Whether this tradeoff is worthwhile depends on how much the provider values small-biotech customers relative to shell-company risk.
