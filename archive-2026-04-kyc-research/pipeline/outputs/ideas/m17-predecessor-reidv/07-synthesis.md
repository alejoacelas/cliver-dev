## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

When a SOC order arrives from a pre-approved entity, the submitting individual must complete a fresh identity-proofing event conformant with NIST 800-63A IAL2: they photograph a government-issued identity document, take a selfie, and pass a liveness check via a third-party IDV vendor (Stripe Identity, Persona, Onfido, ID.me, or similar). The resulting verification artifact — including the verified name, document details, biometric comparison verdict, and liveness result — is cryptographically bound to the specific order record, not to the account. This per-order binding is the key innovation: unlike onboarding-time IAL2 (which proves identity once and then allows any session to ride on it indefinitely), re-IAL2 at order time proves that the person submitting *this specific order* is the same person who was originally approved — or, if not, surfaces the discrepancy immediately via a `predecessor_rebind_name_mismatch` flag.

### What it catches

The check's primary value is closing identity-inheritance attacks. When an attacker takes over a hijacked, compromised, or dormant account (account-hijack, credential-compromise, dormant-account-takeover stories), the re-IAL2 forces them to prove they are the person originally approved. If they present their own identity, the name mismatch flag fires and the order is held. If they attempt to impersonate the predecessor, they must defeat document verification plus liveness detection — a high-cost, high-expertise attack. The check also catches the acquisition variants of the shell-company and biotech-incubator-tenant stories (new principal's name does not match the predecessor's) and surfaces unauthorized users of shared core-facility accounts. For attackers who use their own real identity throughout (gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, build-from-scratch shell company), the check provides attribution rather than prevention: each SOC order carries a biometric commitment tied to the attacker's real face and document, strengthening the post-incident investigative trail.

### What it misses

The check cannot prevent orders from real-identity attackers — a patient operator who builds a purpose-built entity over 12 months using their genuine identity passes re-IAL2 because their identity is real. Prevention for these branches comes from m18/m19/m20 (institutional and individual legitimacy checks), not from identity re-binding. Non-SOC orders are not covered, so an attacker using a dormant account can build activity with non-SOC orders before hitting the SOC trigger. The deepfake/document-forgery arms race defines the check's ceiling: an attacker with access to the predecessor's HR documents and a high-quality deepfake could potentially defeat liveness detection, though major vendors invest heavily in presentation-attack detection. Stage 4C flagged that Stripe Identity, Persona, and Onfido do not have documented NIST IAL2 conformance attestations — only ID.me does — meaning the "IAL2-equivalent" framing should be understood as a vendor marketing claim rather than a certified conformance for most options.

### What it costs

Per-verification cost is $1.50 at Stripe Identity (the cheapest documented option) or $1–$5 at other vendors at moderate volume. For a provider placing 10,000 SOC orders per year, this is $15K–$50K annually in verification costs alone. The dominant cost is friction: industry data from retail banking shows 38–80% abandonment for IDV flows, though synthesis customers are likely more motivated. Stage 6 estimates 10–25% drop rate on first encounter, declining with familiarity. Legitimate handoffs (personnel transitions) in an academic-weighted customer base are estimated at 10–20% of accounts per year, each requiring manual review, institutional confirmation, and potentially full re-screening through m18/m19/m20. Setup cost is estimated at $20K–$80K one-time.

### Operational realism

The review queue receives two classes of flags: verification failures (declined, document tampering, liveness fail — standard IDV failure handling, relatively rare) and name mismatches (the predecessor's name does not match the verified individual — the primary catching mechanism). For name mismatches, the reviewer follows a three-way fork: same person re-verifying (clean signals → release), different person with plausible handoff (verify with institution, require letter, re-screen fully), different person with no handoff (suspend, escalate). The handoff path is the high-volume case in academic settings and the main operational burden. The audit artifact — a verification ID hashed to a specific order record, with the verification report downloadable — is significantly stronger than onboarding-time IAL2 because it demonstrates per-order identity confirmation, not per-account.

### Open questions

The friction drop rate for re-IAL2 at order time (not onboarding) in a synthesis-customer context is not empirically validated — the 10–25% estimate is a downward adjustment from retail banking data. The PAD (presentation-attack-detection) level required is not specified; major vendors offer PAD Level 2 but the implementation does not mandate it. Whether a lighter-weight identity check (e.g., MFA step-up without full IAL2) should fire on non-SOC orders from dormant accounts would close the ramp-up gap but increase friction for legitimate reactivations. The NIST IAL2 conformance gap (only ID.me has documented conformance) should be resolved: either mandate ID.me, or accept "IAL2-equivalent" from other vendors with documented justification.

## Section 3: Open Issues for Human Review

- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** Re-IAL2 provides attribution but not prevention for own-identity purpose-built-organization branches (gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, shell-company build-from-scratch, biotech-incubator-tenant own-identity). The check creates per-order biometric commitment but does not block the order. Prevention for these branches comes from m18/m19/m20, not identity re-binding. This is by design, not a deficiency.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** Non-SOC orders are not covered by re-IAL2. An attacker using a dormant or shared account for non-SOC orders first builds activity without triggering the check. Recommendation: consider lighter-weight identity check on any order from accounts dormant > N months.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** Liveness detection rigor is the load-bearing assumption for defeating dormant-account-takeover with HR document access. The implementation does not specify which PAD level is required. Recommend mandating PAD Level 2 minimum.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** Legitimate-handoff friction may be high in academic settings — 10–20% of accounts per year may experience personnel change, each triggering full re-screening. No streamlined path for institutions with verified HR integrations is proposed.
- **4C flag (OVERSTATED, minor):** Stripe Identity, Persona, and Onfido are treated as IAL2-conformant by implication, but only ID.me has documented NIST 800-63A IAL2 conformance. The document should explicitly note this distinction and recommend tightening in v2.
