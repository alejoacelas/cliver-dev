## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check inspects the DNS configuration and registration history of a customer's claimed institutional domain. When an order arrives, the system performs live DNS lookups for MX, SPF, DMARC, and A/AAAA records, then queries RDAP (the IANA-maintained successor to WHOIS, canonical since January 2025) for the domain's registration date, registrar, and event history. Optionally, DomainTools Iris Investigate can be used to pull up to 100 historical WHOIS records for a domain, revealing ownership-change events and registration gaps. The system computes four flags: `domain_recent` (registered <90 days ago), `domain_no_mail_auth` (missing MX, SPF, or DMARC), `domain_reanimated` (recent transfer or post-gap re-registration visible in RDAP/DomainTools), and `dns_unresolvable` (no A record). The baseline check (DNS + RDAP) costs $0; DomainTools enrichment costs ~$15.75/query and is reserved for escalation.

### What it catches

The check's strongest contribution is against the **dormant-domain** attacker story, where someone acquires the lapsed canonical domain of a defunct research entity. The `domain_reanimated` flag is designed exactly for this scenario — RDAP shows a registration gap or recent transfer, and DomainTools history reveals the ownership change. For other stories (**shell-company**, **cro-framing**, **biotech-incubator-tenant**), the `domain_recent` flag provides friction if the attacker uses a freshly registered domain and places orders within 90 days. During the initial 4–6 weeks of domain warming, the `domain_no_mail_auth` flag may also fire before the attacker finishes mail-authentication setup. These are contributing signals, not standalone gates.

### What it misses

The check has significant structural limitations. The 90-day `domain_recent` threshold is shorter than most attacker lead-up times: the shell-company branch plans 6–12 months, shell-nonprofit plans 2–6 months of pre-aging, and the gradual-legitimacy-accumulation branch is explicitly designed to wait out time-based gates. Aged-domain purchase ($50–$5,000) is a routine bypass listed in multiple attacker branches and completely defeats all time-based signals. Mail-authentication signals are trivially satisfied — Google Workspace automatically configures MX, SPF, DKIM, and DMARC in under an hour. For the **foreign-institution** story, the check validates the real institution's domain (which passes all checks) but cannot determine whether the individual actually works there. For academic customers (~39% of market), the check falls back to the parent-university domain, which trivially passes but provides no discriminating signal about the specific lab.

### What it costs

The baseline check (DNS + RDAP) is free. Setup cost is approximately 2 engineering days for the DNS-parsing and RDAP-normalization layer. DomainTools enrichment adds $15.75/query at the Tier 0 Starter plan ($15,750/month for 250 queries), making it viable only as an escalation path for high-suspicion cases. Post-GDPR WHOIS redaction significantly degrades DomainTools' value: when both old and new registrant information is redacted (est. 60–80% of gTLD registrations), ownership-transfer detection is invisible, weakening the cost-benefit proposition for the $15+/query enrichment.

### Operational realism

The `domain_recent` and `dns_unresolvable` flags are straightforward for reviewers: cross-check domain age against incorporation date, or confirm whether the domain resolves. The `domain_no_mail_auth` flag is operationally problematic because of its extremely high base rate — approximately 80% of domains globally lack DMARC, and 39% lack even SPF. For small legitimate customers, this flag will fire far more often than not, creating substantial review noise. It must be heavily down-weighted or used only in combination with other flags. The `domain_reanimated` flag requires the most judgment: the reviewer must evaluate whether a recent RDAP `last-changed` event reflects a genuine ownership transfer or a routine DNS update. DomainTools history, when available, provides clearer evidence but at $15.75/query. The audit trail is a JSON snapshot of DNS and RDAP responses with query timestamps.

### Open questions

The 04C claim check flagged two items: the ICANN WHOIS sunset date should be cited from an ICANN-hosted source rather than Wikipedia (UPGRADE-SUGGESTED), and the MxToolbox API `[unknown]` could benefit from one more search query targeting "MxToolbox Delivery Center pricing." The 06F form check noted that the ccTLD RDAP coverage estimate (30–50% without functioning federation) is a `[best guess]` with no derivation method despite citing the RDAP Deployment Dashboard. The hardening report raised the question of whether DomainTools enrichment should be the default rather than the escalation path, given that the `domain_reanimated` flag — the check's strongest contribution — depends on it. Post-GDPR redaction makes this cost-benefit calculation uncertain.

  - gTLD privacy redaction rate (60–80%) — reasonable post-GDPR but no single authoritative source.
- **`[vendor-gated]` items:** DomainTools Enterprise pricing and exact rate limits require sales conversation. MxToolbox API pricing is effectively vendor-gated behind a subscription inquiry.
- **04C claim check unresolved flags:**
  - UPGRADE-SUGGESTED: ICANN WHOIS sunset date should cite ICANN directly, not Wikipedia.
  - THIN-SEARCH (mild): MxToolbox `[unknown]` could try "MxToolbox Delivery Center pricing."
- **Hardening suggestions not implemented:**
  - Finding 2 (Moderate): Consider making DomainTools (or cheaper WHOIS-history service) the default for new customers rather than escalation-only, or flag any domain whose RDAP `last-changed` is within 6 months regardless of registration date.
  - Finding 1 (Moderate): Graduated domain-age tiers (90/180/365 days with decreasing suspicion) rather than a single 90-day cutoff.
  - Finding 4 (Minor): Trigger DomainTools enrichment for any domain with RDAP `last-changed` within 12 months, regardless of other signals.
- **Structural limitation acknowledged:** This check is a corroborating signal, not a standalone screen. The `domain_no_mail_auth` flag has an ~80% base rate on small domains and must be down-weighted. For academic customers (~39% of market), the check provides near-zero marginal signal.
