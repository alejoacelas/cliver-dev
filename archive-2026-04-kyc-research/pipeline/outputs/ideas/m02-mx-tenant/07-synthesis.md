## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check examines the DNS-level mail infrastructure behind a customer's email domain. It resolves MX records (which mail servers handle delivery), SPF records (which servers are authorised to send on behalf of the domain), and DMARC records (what policy governs unauthenticated messages). It then probes Microsoft's unauthenticated GetUserRealm endpoint to determine whether the domain has an Entra ID / M365 tenant, and if so, whether it is cloud-managed or federated and what brand name is registered. A classifier maps the MX hostnames to a provider category (Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, Proofpoint, self-hosted, generic shared hosting, etc.). The check fires when the signals are inconsistent with the customer's claimed affiliation — for example, a claimed university whose domain has no DMARC, or an M365 tenant whose brand name does not match the claimed institution. All data sources are free and unauthenticated.

### What it catches

The check reliably catches typosquat / lookalike-domain attacks: a freshly registered domain mimicking a real institution will typically lack DMARC, have no M365 tenant, or have a tenant whose brand name does not match the impersonated institution. It also catches the self-hosted variant of lapsed-domain acquisition, where an attacker re-registers a defunct institution's domain and stands up their own mail server — the `mx_self_hosted_unverified` flag fires. These correspond to the `dormant-domain` attacker story (Bypasses A self-hosted and C typosquat).

### What it misses

The check misses two structurally distinct categories. First, all purpose-built-organisation attacks where the attacker registers a fresh domain and sets up Google Workspace or M365 with proper SPF/DMARC — the check passes them identically to a legitimate small biotech. This covers at least 10 attacker stories (cro-identity-rotation, cro-framing, shell-company, biotech-incubator-tenant, gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, community-bio-lab-network, shell-nonprofit Workspace variant, and others). Second, all credential-compromise and insider attacks where the attacker operates from within genuine institutional infrastructure — 11 stories including inbox-compromise, credential-compromise, account-hijack, dormant-account-takeover, visiting-researcher, insider-recruitment, and others. No DNS-level check can distinguish an authorised from an unauthorised user of an institutional mailbox. The M365 bare-brand heuristic (flagging FederationBrandName matching only the bare domain) is trivially defeatable since the attacker can set any brand string via the M365 admin portal.

### What it costs

Marginal cost per check is effectively $0 — DNS queries and the Microsoft GetUserRealm endpoint are free and unauthenticated. Setup cost is approximately 1-2 engineer-days to build and maintain the MX-fingerprint regex table that classifies providers. Ongoing maintenance is needed as new hosted-mail providers appear. No vendor contract or API key is required.

### Operational realism

When a flag fires, the reviewer receives a card showing the customer's claimed institution, email domain, MX provider classification, M365 tenant type, DMARC policy, and the raw DNS records. The SOP has four decision points: cross-check federation brand against institution name, verify self-hosted MX against the institution's published infrastructure, escalate missing/weak DMARC on large institutions, and flag bare-domain M365 tenants. The main operational concern is queue volume: approximately 50% of .edu domains have `dmarc_p=none`, which means the `dmarc_p_none` flag will fire on a substantial fraction of legitimate US academic customers unless tuned to fire only in combination with another risk signal. Self-hosted academic institutions (15-25% of non-US equivalents) will also generate false positives requiring manual resolution. The audit trail consists of timestamped raw DNS records, GetUserRealm XML responses, computed classifications, and all flags fired with the fingerprint-table version used.

### Open questions

The GetUserRealm endpoint's rate limits and terms of service are undocumented — Microsoft does not publish either. The endpoint is widely used by AAD reconnaissance tools and appears tolerant at low volumes, but a formal assessment of permissible use for KYC purposes has not been conducted. The 06-coverage stage flagged that the free-mail customer prevalence estimate (5-15% of orders) is entirely unsupported by synthesis-industry data. The small-commercial-segment size estimate (30-50% of commercial customers being small companies) relies on a wide range without a direct citation.

- **Weakly bounded estimate:** The "30-50% of commercial segment are small companies" figure in coverage gap 2 lacks a direct citation (06F flagged this as MINOR). A tighter proxy (e.g., SBA firm-size distribution in NAICS 5417) would strengthen the coverage gap sizing.
- **Stage 5 Moderate finding M1:** Self-owned commercial domain with Google Workspace passes all checks across 10 attacker stories. This is structural and cannot be resolved within this idea — pairing with m02-rdap-age, m02-ror-domain-match, or m02-wayback is recommended.
- **Stage 5 Moderate finding M2:** Genuine institutional email bypasses are structurally invisible to all DNS-based M02 checks across 11 attacker stories. Not addressable by this idea; requires measures 14, 16, 19.
- **Stage 5 Minor finding m3:** M365 bare-brand heuristic is trivially defeatable — attacker can set any FederationBrandName string. Consider downweighting or removing from flag logic.
- **06-coverage note:** ~50% of .edu domains at `dmarc_p=none` creates high false-positive volume on `dmarc_p_none` flag. Consider tuning to fire only in combination with another risk signal.
