## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The lookalike domain detector compares a customer's email domain against a corpus of approximately 84,000 research institution domains from the Research Organization Registry (ROR). Four detection methods run in sequence: the UTS #39 confusables skeleton function catches homoglyph substitutions (e.g., Cyrillic "a" for Latin "a"), Levenshtein distance catches typographic near-misses within edit distance 2, dnstwist generates permutations across 11 fuzzer classes (addition, bitsquatting, homoglyph, hyphenation, insertion, omission, repetition, replacement, subdomain, transposition, vowel-swap), and crt.sh certificate transparency provides the first-seen cert date to distinguish aged legitimate domains from freshly set-up impersonation domains. All four components are free and self-hostable: dnstwist is open-source Python, ROR is a CC0 data dump refreshed monthly from Zenodo, crt.sh is a free CT log search operated by Sectigo, and the confusables library is a pip-installable Unicode reference. When a near-match fires combined with a recently issued first cert (under 90 days), the system produces a hard-hold flag and routes to a human reviewer for callback verification with the real institution.

### What it catches

The check catches a narrow but high-signal class of impersonation attacks: domain homoglyphs and typosquats of known research institutions. This directly addresses the typosquat/lookalike fallback variant of the dormant-domain story and the lookalike-inbox-creation variant of the inbox-compromise story. When an attacker registers `harvrad.edu` or uses a Cyrillic homoglyph of `stanford.edu`, the detector flags it. The combination of string-similarity match plus recently-issued certificate is particularly strong — a fresh domain that closely resembles a known institution is almost certainly malicious. The check forces attackers who would otherwise use cheap homoglyph domains to instead invest in more expensive and time-consuming strategies (exact domain acquisition, building original identities).

### What it misses

The check's scope is structurally narrow: it detects domain lookalikes of known institutions, nothing else. It provides zero signal against attackers who use original domains that do not resemble any known institution — which is the strategy for shell companies, CRO facades, incubator tenants, community labs, and gradual-legitimacy accumulators. Entity-name collisions that do not translate to domain-string similarity are missed entirely (an attacker can choose a confusingly similar institution name while registering a completely different domain). The dormant-domain branch's primary strategy — acquiring the exact lapsed domain of a defunct institution — evades the check because it is an exact match, not a near-match. Pre-aged domains (the shell-nonprofit branch explicitly recommends 2–6 months of pre-aging) defeat the 90-day cert threshold. Identity-layer attacks using real institutional domains (inbox compromise, credential compromise, IT persona manufacturing) are outside scope. ROR coverage limits the check to ~84,000 institutions with populated domain fields; commercial entities and ROR records without domains are invisible.

### What it costs

The marginal cost per check is effectively $0: all components are free and self-hosted, with total wall time of approximately 3 seconds per check. Setup cost is roughly one engineer-day for building the in-memory ROR domain index, skeleton precomputation, dnstwist wrapper, crt.sh client with rate-limit politeness, and a monthly ROR dump refresh job. This makes the lookalike detector one of the cheapest ideas in the m18 stack. The only ongoing cost is the human review time when flags fire, which should be infrequent given the narrow match criteria.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is structured as a three-case triage: outright impersonation (reject and denylist), legitimate spinout/subsidiary (callback to the real institution's known number), and aged domain matching a defunct institution (Wayback Machine cross-check, fraud escalation). The callback hygiene rule — use only the real institution's verified contact number, never a number from the suspect domain — is a critical operational detail. In practice, the false-positive rate is driven by legitimate spinouts, hyphenation variants, and transliterated foreign domains. The check produces a detailed audit record: the lookalike report JSON containing the input domain, all matched ROR records, permutation type, crt.sh data, and the reviewer's disposition with callback notes. The check pairs naturally with m02 measures (domain age, RDAP registrant checks) and m18-companies-house-charity (legal existence of the entity behind the domain).

### Open questions

The claim check flagged two issues: the ROR REST API rate limit ("~2000 req/5min") may have changed across API versions and should be softened, and the "~30% of ROR records lack domains" figure is a best guess with a computable answer from the dump itself. The hardening analysis found four Moderate findings, all reflecting structural scope limitations rather than implementation bugs: entity-name collision not addressed by domain-string comparison (M1), exact-domain acquisition evading all detection methods (M2), pre-aged domains defeating the 90-day cert threshold (M3), and ROR self-listing converting the check into a self-match (M4). None of these are addressable within this idea's scope — they require separate ideas (entity-name similarity detector, WHOIS history/registrant-change detector, ROR listing-age cross-check). The minor finding on bidirectional dnstwist comparison should be resolved in the implementation specification.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.**
- **Moderate hardening finding M1:** Entity-name collision not addressed by domain-string comparison. The shell-nonprofit branch's primary strategy (confusingly similar institution name with a different domain) is outside this check's scope. A separate entity-name-similarity idea against ROR names would address this.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2:** Exact-domain acquisition (dormant-domain primary strategy) evades all four detection methods. A WHOIS-history / registrant-change detector would address this.
- **Moderate hardening finding M3:** Pre-aged domains (2–12 months) defeat the 90-day cert-age threshold. Parameter extension to 12–18 months would help only for the subset where string similarity also fires.
- **Moderate hardening finding M4:** ROR self-listing converts the check into a self-match. Cross-checking ROR record established year and listing date against WHOIS creation date could mitigate, but requires ROR to expose listing-date metadata.
- **Claim check REVISE (minor):** ROR REST API rate-limit claim ("~2000 req/5min") should be softened to "rate-limited, low thousands per 5-minute window." The "~30% of ROR records lack domains" figure should be verified from the dump.
- **`[best guess]` fields needing tighter anchoring:**
  - ROR domain coverage (~30% without domains) — computable from the dump itself.
  - Institutions outside ROR (40–60% of customers) — needs synthesis market customer-type data.
  - crt.sh reliability (5–10% incomplete lookups) — no proxy cited.
- **Minor finding m1:** Bidirectional dnstwist comparison direction is unspecified. Should generate permutations from both ROR domains and customer domain to catch subdomain variants.
- **Minor finding m2:** ROR domain-absent fallback to name fuzzy match has no specified algorithm or thresholds.
- **crt.sh ToS:** No formal terms of service found. Commercial screening use at scale may need to fall back to SSLMate ($1,000/month) or a self-hosted CT log mirror.
