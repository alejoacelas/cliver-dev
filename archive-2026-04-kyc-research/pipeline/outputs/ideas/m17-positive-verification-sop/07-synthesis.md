## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check is a documented standard operating procedure (SOP) for periodically re-verifying entities on a provider's pre-approved customer list. On an annual cadence (or sooner if triggered by a material event such as a sanctions hit or personnel change), the SOP requires an analyst to send a positive-confirmation request to a contact at the pre-approved institution. The critical design element in v2 is the independent-contact mandate: the analyst must obtain the contact through a channel the customer does not control. The priority order is (1) the NIH IBC Registration Management System, which publicly posts BSO and IBC chair contacts for NIH-funded institutions; (2) the institution's main switchboard, looked up from the official website; (3) the institution's public faculty/staff directory; (4) corporate registry officer records (e.g., Companies House for UK entities). The analyst is explicitly prohibited from using any contact information provided by the customer being re-verified. When the confirmation reply arrives by email, the SOP requires DMARC alignment verification against the institutional domain. For non-established entities (commercial, new, no IBC-RMS record), two independently-sourced contacts must confirm.

### What it catches

The SOP catches two primary threat patterns. First, stale pre-approvals: an entity was legitimately approved years ago but the authorizing personnel have departed, the IBC status has lapsed, or the entity's sanctions posture has changed. The annual cadence forces fresh confirmation that the entity remains active and authorized. Second, account-takeover exploitation: in the account-hijack and credential-compromise attacker stories, the adversary controls the PI's inbox and would self-confirm under the v1 SOP. The v2 independent-contact mandate routes the confirmation to the BSO or department contact via switchboard/IBC-RMS — a person the attacker does not control. The DMARC check prevents the attacker from spoofing a reply from the institutional domain (where DMARC is enforced).

### What it misses

The SOP has a fundamental structural limitation against purpose-built organizations. When the institution itself is a shell or front controlled by the attacker, every "independent" channel — the switchboard, the website, the domain, the corporate registry officers — leads back to the attacker. The two-contact requirement for non-established entities raises the cost (the attacker must staff two independently-reachable personas) but does not constitute a hard block. The implementation doc acknowledges this honestly and defers to complementary ideas (m18 institution-legitimacy checks, m09 entity-verification) for coverage. Additionally, the coverage research revealed that DMARC enforcement among universities is far lower than the implementation doc's initial estimate: cited data shows only ~23% of US top-500 universities and ~25% of European top-500 universities enforce DMARC at quarantine or reject levels. This means Layer 2 (email authentication) provides no protection for roughly 70–80% of academic institutions, making the phone-callback supplement essential rather than optional. The SOP also provides weak signal for small labs where the independent contact and the entity principal are the same person.

### What it costs

The marginal cost is $75–$150 per entity per year, estimated at 45–90 minutes of analyst time at a fully loaded rate of $100/hour. The independent-contact lookup adds approximately 15–30 minutes over v1's simpler outreach. The two-contact requirement for non-established entities approximately doubles the contact-verification portion. These estimates likely understate costs for non-English institutions (Gap 5), where switchboard lookups involve translation tools, multilingual staff, and timezone coordination. Setup cost is $15K–$35K, covering SOP documentation, workflow tool configuration, DMARC validation integration, and analyst training. IBC-RMS lookup and DMARC validation have zero marginal cost.

### Operational realism

The analyst workflow is structured around a 60-day window: the ticket opens 30 days before the due date, allowing time for independent-contact identification and outreach. If the first attempt fails, a second attempt via a different channel (e.g., phone if email failed) follows. After 30 days past due with no confirmation, the entity is auto-suspended — a strong default that prevents stale approvals from persisting indefinitely. The DMARC check is performed at the provider's email gateway, requiring no external API call but depending on the provider's existing email infrastructure supporting DMARC reporting. For institutions where DMARC returns `none` (the majority, per coverage research), the SOP treats this as a weaker confirmation and supplements with a phone callback, which adds time but maintains a second verification channel. The record left includes full documentation of the independent contact source, the confirmation artifact, DMARC results, sanctions delta, and reviewer decision — providing a comprehensive audit trail for each re-verification cycle.

### Open questions

The DMARC enforcement rate among academic institutions is materially lower than the implementation doc's >70% estimate. Cited data shows ~20–30% enforcement globally among top universities. This does not invalidate Layer 2 but reframes it as a bonus signal rather than a reliable control — the phone-callback channel becomes the primary supplementary mechanism for most institutions. The fraction of academic labs small enough that the independent contact is the same person as the entity principal is unquantified. The cost estimates for non-English-institution switchboard lookups likely need upward revision. IBC-RMS coverage is limited to US NIH-funded institutions, leaving the majority of global academic customers without access to the highest-confidence independent-contact source.

- **No surviving Critical findings.** C1 (self-confirmation by attacker-controlled contacts) downgraded to Moderate after v2's independent-contact mandate. Minor finding m1 (out-of-band confirmation channels) resolved.
- **Surviving Moderate findings:** M1 — IBC-RMS coverage gap for non-US and non-academic institutions (unchanged; switchboard/directory fallback is less reliable). M2 — order-pattern monitoring is outside SOP scope (by design).
