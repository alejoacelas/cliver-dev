# Coverage research: Annual positive-verification SOP (with independent-contact mandate and DMARC verification)

## Coverage gaps

### Gap 1: Purpose-built organizations where attacker controls all independent channels
- **Category:** Entities that are shell organizations or purpose-built fronts where the attacker controls the institution's switchboard, website, domain, corporate registry officers, and all personnel. For these entities, every "independent" contact source leads back to the attacker — the independent-contact mandate collapses.
- **Estimated size:** [unknown — searched for: "percentage of DNA synthesis orders from shell companies", "shell entity detection rate KYC", "purpose-built organization frequency biosecurity"]. The implementation doc explicitly acknowledges this as a structural limitation. [best guess: among the attacker population (not the general customer population), purpose-built organizations are a major category in the attacker stories. Among the legitimate customer population, this gap does not generate false positives — it is a false-negative gap (attacker passes the check). The relevant question is how many attacker-class entities exist among customers; this is unknowable a priori but is the core threat that m18 institution-legitimacy and m09 entity-verification are designed to address.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-negative — the SOP produces an affirmative confirmation from what appears to be an independent contact, but the contact is attacker-controlled.
- **Reasoning:** This is the fundamental structural limitation of any contact-based verification for purpose-built entities. The v2 two-contact requirement for non-established entities provides a speed bump but not a hard block. The SOP documents this gap explicitly and defers to other ideas (m18, m09) for coverage.

### Gap 2: Non-US academic institutions not in IBC-RMS
- **Category:** Legitimate academic researchers at institutions outside the US (or at US institutions without NIH funding) that are not registered in the NIH IBC Registration Management System. For these entities, the highest-priority independent contact source (IBC-RMS) is unavailable, and the SOP falls back to switchboard/directory lookup, which is less standardized and more labor-intensive.
- **Estimated size:** IBC-RMS covers institutions that receive NIH funding for recombinant/synthetic nucleic acid research [source](https://osp.od.nih.gov/policies/biosafety-and-biosecurity-policy/faqs-on-institutional-biosafety-committee-ibc-administration-april-2024/). [best guess: there are ~400-600 US institutions with active IBC registrations in IBC-RMS, representing the major US research universities and medical centers. Non-US institutions (which represent the majority of global academic synthesis customers, given that ~63% of synthesis market revenue is non-North-American [source](https://www.towardshealthcare.com/insights/dna-synthesis-market-sizing)) are entirely outside IBC-RMS. Additionally, US institutions without NIH funding (primarily teaching colleges, small liberal-arts schools with limited research) are also outside IBC-RMS. Net: IBC-RMS covers perhaps 20-30% of academic synthesis-customer institutions globally.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the SOP still works (switchboard callback, faculty directory) but the independent-contact source is less reliable and more labor-intensive.
- **Reasoning:** IBC-RMS provides a high-confidence independent contact (the BSO or IBC chair, maintained by institutional administration). Without IBC-RMS, the analyst must find an independent contact via web lookup, which is feasible for well-known universities but unreliable for smaller or non-English-language institutions.

### Gap 3: Institutions without DMARC enforcement
- **Category:** Legitimate entities whose email domains do not enforce DMARC (p=none or no DMARC record), meaning the SOP's Layer 2 (DMARC alignment check on confirmation replies) produces no signal or a weak signal.
- **Estimated size:** DMARC enforcement rates in higher education are substantially lower than the implementation doc's [best guess: >70%] estimate. Actual data shows: US top 500 universities — only 23% at enforcement (p=quarantine or p=reject) [source](https://dmarcian.com/dmarc-adoption-among-us-and-canada-higher-education/); European top 500 — ~25% at enforcement [source](https://dmarcian.com/dmarc-adoption-european-higher-education/); APAC — Australia is relatively strong (~50% enforcement among top 50) but broader APAC is lower [source](https://dmarcian.com/dmarc-adoption-apac-higher-education/). [best guess: across the global academic customer base, only ~20-30% of institutional domains enforce DMARC at quarantine or reject levels. The remaining 70-80% either have no DMARC record or use p=none (monitoring only), meaning the DMARC check provides no protection against spoofed replies.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the SOP treats DMARC-none as a "weaker but not failed" confirmation and supplements with phone callback, but the Layer 2 email-authentication control is effectively absent for the majority of institutions.
- **Reasoning:** This is a significant finding that contradicts the implementation doc's optimistic DMARC estimate. The 70% enforcement assumption appears to be based on general DMARC adoption trends rather than higher-education-specific data. The actual enforcement rate among universities is much lower, substantially weakening Layer 2 for most academic customers.

### Gap 4: Small commercial entities with few employees (two-contact requirement infeasible)
- **Category:** Legitimate small biotech startups, CROs, and contract manufacturers with fewer than 5 employees, where the SOP's two-contact requirement for non-established entities is impossible to satisfy (there aren't two independent contacts to reach).
- **Estimated size:** Small to mid-size biopharmaceutical companies account for 99% of businesses in the field [source](https://www.biospace.com/how-small-to-medium-biotech-companies-are-growing-in-uncertain-times). Average startup establishments have ~4 employees [source](https://www.bls.gov/spotlight/2022/business-employment-dynamics-by-age-and-size/home.htm). [best guess: among non-established commercial entities ordering synthesis, perhaps 30-50% have fewer than 5 employees. These entities cannot satisfy the two-contact requirement. The SOP's fallback (one contact plus corroborating signal from m18/m09/m20) is reasonable but adds complexity and may delay orders.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-positive — the entity fails the two-contact gate and must use a fallback path, adding friction even for legitimate entities.
- **Reasoning:** The implementation doc acknowledges this for entities with <5 employees and specifies a fallback. The coverage gap is the fraction of the commercial customer base that hits this fallback path, which is non-trivial given the prevalence of small biotech companies.

### Gap 5: Institutions where switchboard/directory lookup fails (non-English, no public directory)
- **Category:** Legitimate academic or government institutions, primarily in non-English-speaking countries, where: (a) the institution's website is not in English, making switchboard lookup difficult for English-speaking analysts; (b) the institution does not maintain a publicly accessible faculty/staff directory; (c) the switchboard number listed online is outdated or does not route to biosafety/sponsored-programs offices.
- **Estimated size:** [best guess: among non-US, non-UK academic institutions, perhaps 30-40% present significant barriers to independent-contact lookup for English-speaking analysts. This includes most institutions in China, Japan, South Korea, and continental Europe where institutional websites are primarily in the local language. Chinese institutions alone may represent 15-25% of academic synthesis customers globally, based on China's dominant position in synthetic biology research [source](https://merics.org/en/report/lab-leader-market-ascender-chinas-rise-biotechnology).]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the SOP can technically be executed (via multilingual staff or translation tools) but the practical barriers increase analyst time, error rates, and the risk of reaching the wrong contact.
- **Reasoning:** The SOP's independent-contact mandate assumes the analyst can navigate the institution's public-facing infrastructure to find a contact. For institutions in non-English-language contexts, this is substantially harder. The implementation doc mentions "timezone coordination and potentially language barriers" under failure modes but does not quantify the affected population.

### Gap 6: Entities where the customer-provided contact and the independent contact are the same person
- **Category:** Legitimate small labs or departments where the PI, the orderer, and the institutional contact who would respond to a positive-verification request are all the same person. The independent-contact mandate finds this person via an independent channel, but the confirmation is still from the same individual whose authorization is being verified.
- **Estimated size:** [best guess: common for small academic labs (1 PI + 1-3 students/postdocs), where the PI is both the orderer's supervisor and the person the switchboard routes to. Perhaps 20-30% of academic re-verification cases involve a lab small enough that the independent contact is functionally the same as or closely aligned with the entity being verified.] [unknown — searched for: "average academic lab size biology", "single PI lab percentage academic research"]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the contact is independently sourced (passed the independence test) but the confirmation is from a person with an interest in the outcome.
- **Reasoning:** The SOP notes that matching independent and customer-provided contacts is "expected for legitimate entities but notable." This is correct but undersells the coverage limitation: for these entities, independent-contact verification does not add meaningful assurance beyond what v1 provided.

## Refined false-positive qualitative

1. **Small entities failing two-contact requirement** (Gap 4): Legitimate startups with <5 employees cannot provide two independent contacts. The fallback path adds friction and delay. This is the most common false-positive trigger for commercial customers.
2. **Institutions without DMARC enforcement** (Gap 3): DMARC-none results create a "weaker confirmation" status that may trigger supplementary phone callbacks, adding friction for ~70-80% of academic institutions globally.
3. **Non-English institutions causing switchboard-lookup failure** (Gap 5): Analyst cannot reach an independent contact, triggering `reverification_contact_not_independent` flag and possible suspension for a legitimate entity. Disproportionately affects Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and continental European institutions.
4. **Legitimate entities flagged for having same customer-provided and independent contact** (Gap 6): The SOP flags this as notable, potentially generating unnecessary reviewer attention for small labs.

## Notes for stage 7 synthesis

- The most significant finding is that DMARC enforcement rates in higher education are far lower than the implementation doc's >70% estimate. Actual enforcement is ~20-30% globally among universities. Layer 2 (DMARC alignment check) is effectively absent for the majority of academic customers. The SOP should treat DMARC as a bonus signal, not a reliable control.
- Gap 1 (purpose-built organizations) is the acknowledged structural limitation — this SOP cannot catch attackers who control the entire institutional facade. This is by design (deferred to m18, m09) but the stage 7 synthesis should emphasize that this SOP's coverage is limited to the "stale approval / post-approval drift" threat model, not the "purpose-built entity" threat model.
- The independent-contact mandate is a genuine improvement over v1 for the "account hijack / credential compromise" attacker class. For institutions in IBC-RMS, it provides strong assurance. For non-IBC-RMS institutions, assurance degrades to switchboard/directory lookup quality, which varies widely by region.
- Operational cost estimates in the implementation doc ($75-$150/entity/year) should be revisited given the switchboard-lookup difficulties for non-English institutions (Gap 5), which likely push actual per-entity costs higher for the international customer base.
