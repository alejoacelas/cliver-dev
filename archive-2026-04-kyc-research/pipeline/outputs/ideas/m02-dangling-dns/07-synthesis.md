## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The dangling-DNS / drop-catch detector scans a customer's affiliation domain for two specific attack patterns: (a) DNS records pointing to abandoned cloud resources (S3 buckets, Heroku apps, Azure endpoints) that an attacker could claim to control, and (b) domains that were recently drop-caught — purchased after their original registration lapsed. It combines three free, open-source data sources: dnsReaper (an MIT-licensed subdomain takeover scanner), crt.sh (a public Certificate Transparency log), and the Tranco top-1M domain ranking list. The combined signal looks for the hallmarks of domain repurposing: dangling DNS records, gaps in certificate history, and disappearance-then-reappearance in domain rankings. Any findings route to a manual reviewer who correlates with WHOIS history and archive.org snapshots.

### What it catches

The check is highly effective against its designed target: the dormant-domain attacker story. Specifically, it catches Bypass A (acquiring a lapsed canonical domain of a defunct research entity — the CT-log gap and Tranco rank delta expose the ownership change) and Bypass B (subdomain takeover via dangling DNS on a live institution — dnsReaper's signature-based detection directly targets this). Stage 5 confirmed both bypasses as CAUGHT. Several aged-domain purchase scenarios across other attacker stories (cro-identity-rotation, biotech-incubator-tenant, gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, shell-company) produce AMBIGUOUS results — the signal depends on whether the aged domain had a certificate gap during its parking period.

### What it misses

The check has no leverage on the large majority of modeled attacker stories. Attackers who use self-owned newly-registered domains (cro-framing, shell-nonprofit, community-bio-lab-network, and others) produce no dangling-DNS findings, no CT gaps, and no Tranco anomalies. All institutional-inbox-compromise attacks (inbox-compromise, credential-compromise, account-hijack, dormant-account-takeover, it-persona-manufacturing, visiting-researcher, insider attacks) operate on genuine institutional domains with clean DNS — the check cannot detect who controls the inbox. Typosquat domains (dormant-domain Bypass C) also slip through because they are fresh domains that do not claim long history. The Tranco rank-delta signal is structurally absent for small institutional domains outside the top 1M, which may be 30-50% of customers. Customers using personal email (Gmail, Outlook) — an estimated 15-25% — bypass the check entirely.

### What it costs

Total marginal cost is effectively $0: dnsReaper is open-source with negligible compute cost, crt.sh is free, and Tranco data is free. Setup requires an estimated 1-2 engineer-weeks to build the orchestration pipeline and reviewer queue. For higher-SLA deployments, a paid aggregator (SecurityTrails, DomainTools) could replace the free tools but costs four-to-five-figure annual subscriptions. The real cost is manual review time: university DNS hygiene is empirically poor (researchers found over 1,000 organizations with vulnerable subdomains, and major US universities like UCLA and Stanford were demonstrated to have exploitable dangling DNS), meaning the false-positive rate against legitimate institutions may be significant.

### Operational realism

In practice, findings enter a reviewer queue showing the customer domain, any dangling records with their claim signatures, the CT first-seen date, and the Tranco rank delta over 30 days. The reviewer pulls WHOIS history (via DomainTools or SecurityTrails) and archive.org snapshots to assess whether the domain was genuinely repurposed. Dangling records on the primary mail subdomain trigger high-suspicion handling (block + escalate); peripheral subdomains are logged as advisory only. Clear drop-catch evidence (large CT gap, content discontinuity) requires additional affiliation evidence before approving. The reviewer must distinguish "sloppy DNS hygiene" from "active takeover" — a judgment call that requires training and calibration. Wildcard DNS configurations at large institutions can generate dozens of findings per domain, each requiring triage.

### Open questions

Two Moderate findings from stage 5 remain unresolved: (1) typosquat domains are not detected — a string-similarity check against a known-institution domain list may be needed, though this is arguably a separate M02 idea; (2) aged-domain purchases with certificate continuity are invisible because the archive.org content-comparison step only triggers if another flag fires first — adding a WHOIS registrant-change signal as a primary trigger (independent of CT/Tranco) would close this gap but may overlap with m02-rdap-age. Stage 8 synthesis should assess whether these two ideas are complementary on the aged-domain dimension.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No Critical findings from stage 5.** The check is effective at its designed target (dormant-domain Bypasses A and B) but narrowly scoped.
- **Moderate: typosquat domains slip through (dormant-domain Bypass C).** A string-similarity check against known institutions is needed but may belong in a separate M02 idea rather than this DNS-focused check. Requires scoping decision.
- **Moderate: aged-domain purchases with certificate continuity are invisible.** The archive.org content-comparison step only triggers if another flag fires first. Adding WHOIS registrant-change as a primary trigger would close this gap but may overlap with m02-rdap-age. Requires cross-idea coordination at stage 8.
- **High false-positive base rate against legitimate universities.** Empirical evidence shows major universities routinely have exploitable dangling DNS. Reviewer SOP must distinguish "sloppy DNS hygiene" from "active takeover" — this requires calibration data and training that does not yet exist.
- **Tranco signal absent for small institutions.** The rank-delta signal — the most distinctive and hardest-to-fake component — is unavailable for domains outside the top 1M, which includes many of the institutions most relevant to biosecurity screening.
- **No signal for customers without institutional domains (15-25% of customer base).** This check must be paired with other M02 ideas for comprehensive coverage.
