## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check verifies that a customer's claimed institution is a legally registered, active corporate entity with a plausible connection to life sciences. When an order arrives, the system routes the customer's stated legal name and jurisdiction through a three-tier registry stack: UK Companies House for UK entities (free API, 600 req/5min), SEC EDGAR for US public companies (free, 10 req/sec), and OpenCorporates for US private companies and all other jurisdictions (commercial API, ~$0.25/call on the Basic plan). The system captures the entity's incorporation date, legal status (active vs. dissolved), registered office address, officers, and SIC/NAICS industry codes. It then evaluates four flags: no record found, dissolved status, non-life-sciences industry code, and recent incorporation (<12 months). Dissolved status triggers auto-block; the other flags route to manual review with a five-case disposition playbook.

### What it catches

The check provides meaningful friction against several attacker stories. For the **dormant-domain** scenario — where an attacker acquires the lapsed domain of a defunct research entity — the `registry_dissolved` flag catches the direct-reuse path, since the defunct entity's corporate record shows dissolved/struck-off status (auto-block). For **shell-company**, **shell-nonprofit**, **cro-framing**, **cro-identity-rotation**, **biotech-incubator-tenant**, and **community-bio-lab-network** stories, the `registry_recent_incorp` flag fires during the entity's first 12 months, providing a contributing (though non-blocking) signal. This is most useful in combination with other M09 checks: the cluster of recent-incorp + new domain + no PubMed footprint is the shell-company signature.

### What it misses

The check has several structural blind spots. First, all time-based signals expire after 12 months — the **gradual-legitimacy-accumulation** branch is explicitly designed to wait out this window. Second, **entity acquisition** (buying a dormant micro-CRO or going-concern) inherits an aged incorporation date, active status, and potentially life-sciences SIC codes, producing a fully clean pass. The implementation does not detect officer turnover, which could signal a recent acquisition (this data exists in Companies House and OpenCorporates but is not used). Third, SIC/NAICS codes are **attacker-controlled at filing** — an attacker can self-select "Scientific Research and Development" (NAICS 5417) with zero verification, defeating the `sic_not_life_sciences` flag entirely. Fourth, the **foreign-institution** story is completely outside scope: the check validates that a real foreign institution exists in registries, but the exploit is at the individual-affiliation layer (does this person work there?), not the entity-existence layer. Coverage gaps are also structural: government research institutes (~30–50% of ~4,200 globally) and public universities (~60–70% of ~26,000 worldwide) are not in corporate registries, meaning the check produces no signal for roughly half the synthesis customer base (academic ~39% + government).

### What it costs

Marginal cost per check: $0 for Companies House and SEC EDGAR; ~$0.25 for OpenCorporates. Combined per-customer estimate is $0.25–$1.50, dominated by OpenCorporates when the customer is outside UK/US-public-company jurisdiction. Annual OpenCorporates licensing starts at £2,250/yr (Essentials) and scales to £12,000/yr (Basic) or enterprise pricing. Setup cost is estimated at 3–5 engineering days for the routing layer (jurisdiction detection, registry selection, fallback chain, response normalization). OpenCorporates is request-metered, meaning even no-result searches (coverage-gap jurisdictions) consume budget.

### Operational realism

When a flag fires, the reviewer receives the customer's stated legal name, registered address, and jurisdiction alongside the raw registry response and matched record. The `registry_dissolved` flag is the simplest: auto-block, reviewer notified for audit trail. The `sic_not_life_sciences` flag requires the reviewer to read the company name and activities description — many legitimate small biotechs file under generic codes, so this is not auto-deny but adds ~2–5 minutes of reviewer time. The `registry_recent_incorp` flag is explicitly non-blocking and requires the reviewer to check other M09 signals (domain age, PubMed presence, ROR match). The `registry_no_record` flag is the most labor-intensive: the reviewer must manually check the relevant Secretary of State portal or national registry web search to rule out coverage gaps. Each check produces a JSON snapshot of the registry response plus a structured "registry verification record" for audit.

### Open questions

The 04C claim check flagged two missing citations: the UK SIC 2007 code list (codes 72110/72190/21100/21200/86900 claimed as life-sciences) should cite the Companies House SIC list, and the NAICS codes (5417/3254/6215/6113) should cite census.gov/naics. The OpenCorporates pricing figures are sourced from a third-party analysis (zephira.ai) rather than the vendor's own pricing page — an upgrade-suggested flag. The 06F form check flagged three weaknesses in the coverage research: the university count derivation is thin, the biotech formation rate extrapolation from a 2021-boom article to steady-state is weakly grounded, and the "15–25% of global biotech activity" figure lacks a derivation pathway (no clarity on whether the proxy is revenue, company count, or publications).

- **04C claim check unresolved flags:**
  - MISSING-CITATION: UK SIC 2007 codes for life-sciences — should cite https://resources.companieshouse.gov.uk/sic/.
  - MISSING-CITATION: NAICS codes — should cite https://www.census.gov/naics/.
  - UPGRADE-SUGGESTED: OpenCorporates pricing cross-cite against opencorporates.com/pricing directly.
- **Hardening suggestions not implemented:**
  - Finding 3 (Moderate): officer-change detection via Companies House `/company/{number}/officers` endpoint (appointed_on/resigned_on) could flag recent wholesale officer turnover on aged entities, partially addressing the entity-acquisition bypass. Data not available for US LLCs.
  - Finding 1 (Moderate): extending the `registry_recent_incorp` window to 24 months and using graduated scoring (0–6, 6–18, 18–36 months) would extend friction but increase false positives on real new biotechs.
  - Finding 2 (Moderate): SIC/NAICS should be treated asymmetrically — non-life-sciences codes add suspicion, but life-sciences codes should not be treated as positive evidence of actual life-sciences work.
