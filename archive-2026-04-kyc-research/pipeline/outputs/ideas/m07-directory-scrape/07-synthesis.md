## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check scrapes the public people-search directory of the customer's claimed institution to verify that the customer appears as a listed member. For each institution, an HTML scrape adapter specifies the search URL template, CSS selectors for name/email/department/title fields, and a robots-compliant crawl delay. At order intake, the system looks up the adapter, issues a search request, parses the response, and returns a structured record indicating match status (exact, fuzzy, none, gated, or error) along with matched fields. The HTML response is cached as an audit artifact. Where no adapter exists — which covers the vast majority of the world's ~25,000+ institutions — the system falls back to the sister idea m07-google-site-search or to manual reviewer verification. Building and maintaining adapters for the ~200–400 top US, UK, Canadian, and Australian research universities is the realistic deployment scope.

### What it catches

The check's highest-value output is negative: when the scrape runs successfully and finds no record for the customer, the `directory_no_match` flag signals that the claimed affiliation cannot be confirmed from the institution's own public data. This catches inbox-compromise scenarios where a departed adjunct's account is taken over but the directory entry has been removed, dormant-account-takeover cases where the departed researcher's entry is gone, and fabricated personas created without IT access to the directory system. The `directory_role_mismatch` flag is also high-information: it fires when a customer claims to be a PI but the directory lists them as a graduate student, revealing a misrepresentation of role. Stage 5 found that these negative-signal cases are the only scenarios where the check adds genuine adversarial value.

### What it misses

Stage 5 walked 15+ attacker branches and classified the vast majority as MISSED. The check has zero leverage against two large categories: (1) genuine-but-malicious affiliates (visiting researchers, students, lab managers, insiders, account hijackers, credential-compromised PIs) whose real directory entries confirm their real affiliation — the check cannot discriminate intent; and (2) purpose-built organizations (shell companies, shell nonprofits, CROs, dormant domains, community bio labs) where the attacker controls the "institution" and therefore controls the directory the scrape reads. IT-persona-manufacturing defeats the check by definition, since the attacker creates the directory entry the scrape finds. Non-Anglophone institutional directories lack adapter coverage, so the scrape produces no signal for most foreign institutions. This is consistent with M07's "low-scrutiny" scope — the measure is not designed as a hard gate.

### What it costs

Per-check cost is near-zero when an adapter exists (~$0.001 compute + cache). Without an adapter, the manual reviewer fallback costs $15–$30 per check (10–20 minutes of effort). The dominant cost is adapter engineering: $200–$800 per institution to build, with the top ~200 US R1+R2 costing $40K–$160K one-time. Institutional websites redesign 1–3 times per year on average, creating ongoing maintenance. The cost structure makes this check viable only for a curated set of high-priority institutions, not as a universal screen. Vendor alternatives exist (Proxycurl for LinkedIn-based affiliation verification, ~$0.01–$0.10/lookup) but provide a different signal than the institutional directory.

### Operational realism

Exact matches are auto-cleared; the workflow cost is concentrated in the no-match and role-mismatch cases that require manual investigation. For no-match flags, the reviewer checks lab pages, department pages, and archive.org snapshots before escalating — a 10–20 minute task. The structural high false-positive load (postdocs not in central directories, GDPR-suppressed EU institutions, SSO-gated corporate directories, preferred-name mismatches) means the check fires `directory_no_match` frequently for legitimate customers. The SOP must treat directory misses as a soft signal, not a block — consistent with M07's low-scrutiny framing. Each screen produces a cached HTML blob as the audit artifact, demonstrating what the institution publicly said at the time of screening.

### Open questions

The legal and ethical framework for institutional directory scraping is evolving. US ToS-based restrictions on scraping are generally not enforceable without a click-through agreement and demonstrated harm (Brown et al. 2025; California Law Review "Great Scrape"), but EU GDPR is more restrictive — scraping personal data of EU data subjects requires a lawful basis, and "compliance with US export controls" is a defensible but not bulletproof legitimate-interest argument. The check's value proposition depends on whether the M07 measure is intended as a universal screen (where coverage gaps on ~99% of institutions make it impractical) or a targeted check on a curated set of high-priority institutions (where the adapter model works). Stage 5 also raised the question of role-mailbox handling (customers using `genomics-core@university.edu`) and department-relevance signaling, neither of which is currently specified.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** The high MISSED count (15+ attacker branches) reflects M07's low-scrutiny measure scope, not implementation failure. The check is designed as a soft signal; higher-scrutiny measures (M09, M18, M19, M20) are needed for intent discrimination.
- **`[unknown]` fields affecting policy implications:**
  - Name-mismatch rate in institutional directories: no data found. Affects false-positive volume estimate.
- **Stage 5 Moderate findings (specification improvements):**
  - Self-controlled "institutions" are indistinguishable from real ones (6 attacker branches). Requires complementary check validating the institution itself (M09) before trusting its directory. Scrape should cross-reference domain age/history (m02-rdap-age, m02-dangling-dns).
  - Non-Anglophone institutional directory coverage: adapters concentrated on US R1/R2. Foreign institutions should fall back to m07-google-site-search.
- **Stage 5 Minor findings:**
  - Role-mailbox customers: implementation does not specify handling for role accounts (e.g., `genomics-core@university.edu`).
  - Department-relevance signal: `matched_department` is returned but not evaluated for life-science relevance. Design choice per M07 low-scrutiny scope.
  - Stale directory entries: no `directory_stale_entry` concept; periodic caching could add temporal dimension.
- **06F flags not fully resolved:**
  - US postdoc count (~40K–60K) needs direct NSF NCSES citation.
  - ~5–10% central-directory unlisted rate is thinly derived.
  - Europe market share (~20–25%) derived by subtraction, not directly sourced.
  - Inside Higher Ed GDPR article is from 2018; may not reflect current EU practices.
- **Structural pairing required:** This check is explicitly complementary to m07-google-site-search (long-tail institutions), m07-incommon-edugain (federated authentication), and m07-proxycurl-linkedin (industry customers). Should not be evaluated or deployed in isolation.
- **GDPR / legal risk:** Scraping personal data from EU institutional directories requires a defensible lawful basis. Export-control compliance is an arguable legitimate interest but has not been tested in EU courts for this specific use case.
