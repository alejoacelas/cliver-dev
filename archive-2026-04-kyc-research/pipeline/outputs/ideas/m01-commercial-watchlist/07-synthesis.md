## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

A commercial watchlist screen runs every customer name (and, optionally, institutional affiliation) through a vendor-aggregated database of sanctions lists, politically-exposed-person registries, and adverse-media corpora. The three dominant vendors are LSEG/Refinitiv World-Check One, Dow Jones Risk & Compliance, and LexisNexis Bridger Insight XG. All offer REST APIs that accept a name and return match records with scores, source citations, and categorization (sanctioned, PEP, adverse media, state-owned entity). World-Check One also offers a "Zero Footprint" mode that avoids persisting customer data on LSEG infrastructure — relevant for privacy-conscious synthesis providers. Pricing is vendor-gated across all three; industry estimates suggest $0.50-$5 per check at mid-volume, plus annual license fees in the low-to-mid five figures.

### What it catches

The check catches customers who are already flagged somewhere — on a sanctions list, in a law-enforcement database, or in news articles about financial crime, fraud, or weapons proliferation. It extends beyond the free OFAC SDN list by aggregating non-US sanctions lists, PEP databases, and adverse-media feeds. In principle, this addresses the "denied individual" and "gradual legitimacy accumulation" attacker stories. However, the stage 5 hardening analysis found that none of the 19 modeled attacker stories actually engage sanctions screening — every modeled attacker has a name that trivially clears sanctions checks. The check's real value is catching the rare case where a customer happens to already be on a watchlist, not the adversarial cases modeled in the pipeline.

### What it misses

The check has zero predictive power for previously-unknown actors, which is the primary concern in biosecurity. It is purely retrospective: it catches people who are already known to be bad actors, not novel threats. Coverage gaps include the ~70% of gene synthesis market revenue from small commercial/biotech customers who will return no signal, and adverse media in non-English languages (vendor language coverage is unknown but likely thin for Chinese, Arabic, and Farsi media). The structural limitation is that this is a negative-list approach: absence of a match tells you nothing.

### What it costs

Per-check cost is vendor-gated but estimated at $0.50-$5 at mid-volume, plus an annual enterprise license in the low-to-mid five figures. Setup requires 4-8 engineer-weeks for integration. The hidden cost is the manual review burden: industry-wide, approximately 90% of sanctions screening alerts are false positives. For a DNA synthesis customer base — heavily international, disproportionately from high-population countries with common name-spaces — the false-positive rate will be at the high end. Each false positive requires a trained reviewer to open the vendor profile, read cited sources, and disposition the alert. SOE flags on Chinese or Saudi university researchers alone could generate a steady stream of reviews with near-zero biosecurity value.

### Operational realism

In practice, every hit enters a compliance reviewer queue showing the customer record, matched vendor profile, category, match score, and source URLs. The reviewer reads the cited sources (sanctions list entry, news article, court record) and dispositions: sanctions hits are treated as OFAC-equivalent (freeze order, escalate to compliance officer); PEP-only hits typically pass with an enhanced due diligence note; adverse-media hits require article review to distinguish subjects of investigation from incidental mentions. Each disposition is recorded with the vendor case ID and reviewer rationale, creating an auditable trail. Vendor outages trigger fallback to direct OFAC-only screening. The process is well-understood in financial services but novel for synthesis providers, who would need to build or hire compliance review capacity.

### Open questions

The core open question is whether commercial watchlists provide sufficient marginal value over free OFAC/sanctions screening to justify the cost for biosecurity purposes. The marginal value is PEP coverage, adverse media, and non-US sanctions lists — but PEP status has essentially no correlation with bioweapons intent, and adverse media coverage in the languages most relevant to biosecurity threats may be thin. The stage 5 finding that zero modeled attacker stories engage this measure raises a fundamental question about its relevance to the threat model.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Zero attacker-story engagement:** Stage 5 found that all 19 wg attacker branches model attackers whose names trivially clear sanctions screening. This means the check's biosecurity value rests entirely on unmodeled edge cases (e.g., a customer who happens to already be on a sanctions list). A policy decision is needed on whether this justifies the cost.
- **[vendor-gated] pricing for all three vendors:** Per-check and annual license costs require sales conversations with LSEG, Dow Jones, and LexisNexis. Without pricing, cost-benefit analysis is impossible.
- **[unknown] adverse-media language coverage:** Whether World-Check/Dow Jones/Bridger adequately cover Chinese, Arabic, and Farsi media is unknown and affects the check's value for biosecurity screening of international researchers.
- **Marginal value over free OFAC screening:** Commercial watchlists aggregate government lists that can be screened directly for free. The marginal value (PEP, adverse media, non-US lists) has low overlap with biosecurity risk. Whether this marginal value justifies five-to-six figure annual costs is a policy question.
- **False-positive burden on international researchers:** The ~90% false-positive rate, disproportionately affecting East Asian and South Asian researchers, raises equity concerns and operational cost questions that require policy input.
