## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The ROR disjointness rule resolves both the customer's institution and the voucher's institution to canonical identifiers in the Research Organization Registry (ROR) — a free, CC0-licensed database of ~110–120k research organizations maintained as a community-curated resource. The check then applies a structural rule: the two ROR IDs must not be the same and must not share a parent/child or sibling-via-common-parent relationship. If the voucher and customer are at the same institution (or closely related institutions in the same hierarchy), the voucher is rejected as non-independent. The provider can source ROR IDs from three upstream resolvers: the m18-ror institution-name lookup, ORCID employment records (which often contain disambiguated-organization identifiers that are ROR IDs), or a DKIM-domain-to-ROR mapping table. A soft signal flags cases where both parties are for-profit companies in the same city.

### What it catches

The check's primary target is same-institution vouching — the simplest and most natural form of collusion. It blocks PIs vouching for their own lab members, BSOs vouching for institutional customers, shell-entity self-vouching (accomplice at the same shell), manufactured IT personas at the same institution, dormant-domain fabricated vouchers on the same domain, and CRO self-vouching. For the visiting-researcher branch, blocking the host-institution sponsor forces the attacker to find a cross-institutional voucher, which is meaningful friction. The hardening stage confirmed that this structural barrier increases attacker cost: the `shell-nonprofit` source prices external voucher recruitment at $1k–$10k per event.

### What it misses

The check cannot address cross-institutional vouching by design — a voucher at a genuinely different institution passes disjointness regardless of their relationship to the attacker. Recruited co-conspirators at different institutions, cultivated external vouchers, and straw purchasers with disjoint voucher chains all pass. Authentication attacks (credential compromise, session hijack, inbox compromise) are orthogonal. Volume-driven rubber-stamping by an external voucher is not tested. The hardening stage flagged four Moderate concerns: the cross-department exception path is exploitable by BSOs claiming cross-cutting authority (M1); two purpose-built shells with separate RORs in different cities pass (M2); cross-lab mutual vouching passes (M3); and same-voucher reuse across CRO identity rotations is not tracked (M4).

### What it costs

Marginal cost is $0 — ROR is free, CC0-licensed, with generous rate limits (2000 requests/5min, or unlimited via bulk-dump). Setup cost is approximately 3–5 engineering days for API integration and relationship-walker. The main ongoing cost is reviewer time for adjudicating same-institution exceptions and complex hierarchy cases. The coverage research estimates that university-system and hospital-network pairs generate 15–30% false-positive rates, creating recurring reviewer burden.

### Operational realism

The check produces a clean, reproducible audit artifact: both ROR JSON records with timestamps, the relationship-walk result, and the reviewer's adjudication note. ROR data is public and re-fetchable for independent verification. The critical operational detail is the transition to required `client_id` in Q3 2026 (3 months from now) — the provider should register immediately to avoid throttling to 50 requests/5min. The bulk-dump alternative ($0, monthly refresh, ~50–200 MB) entirely avoids API throttling. The reviewer playbook for same-institution exceptions needs tightening: the hardening stage recommended requiring verifiable evidence (institutional letter, not just voucher's claim) and capping same-institution exceptions at a low rate.

### Open questions

The cross-department exception path (M1) is the most consequential design decision for this check. If the exception is too permissive, BSOs and institutional officers can bypass the disjointness rule by asserting cross-cutting authority. If too restrictive, legitimate cross-department vouching at large universities is blocked. The coverage research flagged that Gaps 2 and 3 (complex hierarchies, single-institution environments) lack numerical citations — Carnegie Classification or IPEDS data would strengthen these estimates. Gap 4 (shell organizations in ROR) is the most concerning attacker-relevant gap: a well-resourced attacker can establish a shell that enters ROR via predatory publications. The compounding coverage gap (Gap 5) — where non-OECD institutions simultaneously fail ROR, ORCID, and DKIM resolution — affects the same population across multiple checks.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Moderate hardening finding M1 (surviving):** Cross-department exception path is exploitable by BSOs/institutional officers claiming cross-cutting authority without verifiable evidence. Recommend requiring institutional letter (not self-assertion) and capping same-institution exceptions at a low rate.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2 (surviving):** Two-shell separate-ROR bypass (different cities) passes disjointness and evades the colocation soft signal. Recommend adding a `voucher_customer_both_company` flag when both are type `company` in ROR, regardless of colocation.
- **Moderate hardening finding M3 (surviving):** Cross-lab mutual vouching passes disjointness. Recommend adding a reciprocity detector (flag when org A vouches for org B and org B vouches for org A).
- **Moderate hardening finding M4 (surviving):** Same-voucher reuse across CRO identity rotations not tracked. Recommend tracking voucher-ID reuse across customers and flagging multiple unrelated entities within a time window.
- **Coverage Gap 4 (shell organizations in ROR):** A well-resourced attacker can establish a shell that enters ROR via predatory publications. Size [unknown]. No detection mechanism specified.
- **Coverage Gaps 2 and 3 size estimates:** University system count (~20), academic medical center count (~130), small college count (~2,000+), and biotech company count (40,000–50,000) are stated without citations. Carnegie Classification or IPEDS data would strengthen.
- **Coverage Gap 5 (compounding):** Non-OECD institutions simultaneously fail ROR, ORCID, and DKIM resolution — no automated check pathway for this population.
- **ROR Q3 2026 client_id requirement:** Imminent (3 months out). Provider should register for free client_id now.
- **No 06C (claim check on coverage) was run.** Coverage citations (ROR FAQs, year-in-review, STI 2022) not independently verified in a 06C pass.
