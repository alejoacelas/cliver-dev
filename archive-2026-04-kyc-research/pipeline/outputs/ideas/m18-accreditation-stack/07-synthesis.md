## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The accreditation registry stack cross-references a customer's claimed institutional accreditation against the relevant public registry from a suite of nine: CMS QCOR for CLIA/CAP clinical labs, AAALAC for animal-care accreditation, NIH OLAW for PHS-funded animal welfare assurance, A2LA/ANAB/UKAS for ISO 17025 calibration and testing labs, FDA BIMO for GLP-compliant facilities, and the Global BioLabs map for BSL-3+ and BSL-4 high-containment labs. When a customer claims an accreditation — for example, "we are a CLIA-certified clinical laboratory" or "we operate a BSL-4 facility" — the system queries the relevant registry by organization name and location. All registries are publicly accessible, anonymous, and free. None have documented APIs; integration would require web scraping or bulk-file download (CMS publishes a downloadable laboratory demographics file). The check fires flags when the claimed accreditation is not found, has lapsed, does not match the scope of the customer's stated work, or when a near-name-collision is detected. Flagged cases route to a human reviewer who follows a structured escalation protocol: re-query with name variants, request the certificate number from the customer, and if necessary contact the accrediting body directly.

### What it catches

The check catches three specific attacker patterns. First, accreditation overclaiming: when a shell entity, fake CRO, or revived dormant-domain entity claims an accreditation it does not hold, the registry lookup surfaces the discrepancy. This addresses the fake-accreditation and paper-shell-research-org attacker stories directly. Second, lapsed accreditations: when an acquired or dormant entity's accreditation has expired, the `claimed_accreditation_lapsed` flag fires — catching the dormant-domain and shell-company (acquisition variant) stories. Third, name-collision attacks: when a shell entity deliberately chooses a name close to a real accredited institution, the `accreditation_name_collision` flag provides a signal for manual investigation. The check also provides a vacuously correct pass for identity-layer attacks (inbox compromise, credential compromise, IT persona manufacturing), where the institution itself is legitimate and the bypass operates at a different layer.

### What it misses

The dominant limitation is structural: the check is a claim-verification tool, not a claim-requirement tool. An attacker who simply avoids claiming any accreditation entirely bypasses the check, and this is the low-cost strategy for most attacker stories (shell-company build-from-scratch, CRO framing, CRO identity rotation, community-bio-lab impersonation, gradual legitimacy accumulation). The stage 5 hardening analysis found that 9 of 18 assessed bypass methods are fully missed, and the missed set includes the most common attacker strategies. Additionally, the stack is US/UK-centric: an estimated 30–50% of synthesis-buying institutions worldwide hold accreditations only from national bodies not in the stack (DAkkS in Germany, NABL in India, CNAS in China, etc.). The Global BioLabs map covers only BSL-3+ and BSL-4 facilities, missing 96% of BSL-3 labs worldwide (3,515 vs 126 tracked), making the `bsl_claim_not_in_global_biolabs_map` flag very noisy. The GLP registry is the weakest in the stack — no consolidated public list exists, and GLP claims effectively fall through to manual review with no automated verification.

### What it costs

All nine registries are free for public lookup. The marginal cost per customer is estimated at $0.05–$0.20 in analyst time (~5 minutes at $50/hr fully loaded) when an accreditation claim triggers verification. Setup cost is estimated at $30K–$100K for building and maintaining scrapers across 7+ registry interfaces, mirroring small datasets (Global BioLabs is small enough for a manual mirror), and solving the institution-name normalization problem across registries. Ongoing maintenance is estimated at $20K/year. The name-normalization problem — matching "MIT" to "Massachusetts Institute of Technology" to "M.I.T." — is a real engineering challenge that drives much of the setup and maintenance cost.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is well-specified: a seven-step escalation from re-query with name variants through direct contact with the accrediting body. In practice, most flagged cases will be name-normalization misses (benign) or accreditation claims from foreign institutions whose national registries are not in the stack (benign). The reviewer's main judgment call is distinguishing these false positives from genuine overclaiming. The check produces an auditable record: query terms, response payloads or negative results, timestamps, and the reviewer's adjudication memo — sufficient to demonstrate due diligence. One operational risk is the BSL flag's noise level: because 96% of BSL-3 labs are absent from the Global BioLabs map by design, the `bsl_claim_not_in_global_biolabs_map` flag will fire frequently on legitimate customers, potentially desensitizing reviewers.

### Open questions

The form check and claim check both passed with minor flags. The claim check noted that Global BioLabs 2023 figures may be superseded by a 2024/2025 report and that the CAP and UKAS exact directory URLs remain unknown (mitigated by alternative lookup paths). The coverage research flagged that three key size estimates (foreign institutions at 30–50%, no-accreditation institutions at 40–60%, and OLAW/AAALAC overlap at 200–400) are best guesses without anchoring numerical proxies — tightening these would require market data on synthesis customer demographics by country and institution type. The fundamental open question is whether the screening workflow should require accreditation claims for certain order types (e.g., orders involving select-agent-relevant sequences), which would convert this from a passive verification tool to an active gate — a policy decision outside the scope of this technical assessment.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** Stage 5 found no Critical issues.
- **Moderate hardening finding M1 (structural):** The check provides signal only when an entity explicitly claims an accreditation it lacks; the dominant attacker strategy is to avoid claiming accreditation. This is a scope limitation, not a bug — but it means the check's preventive value is gated on whether the screening workflow *requires* accreditation claims for certain order types. This is a policy decision.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2:** Foreign institutional accreditation coverage is weak (US/UK-centric stack). Extending to the top 5–10 non-US/UK accreditation bodies by customer volume (DAkkS, NABL, CNAS, COFRAC, KOLAS) would partially close this gap but adds integration and maintenance cost per registry.
- **`[unknown]` fields affecting policy implications:**
  - CAP exact directory tool URL — mitigated by QCOR fallback path.
  - UKAS exact tool URL — needs a single targeted lookup to resolve.
  - CMS QCOR API availability — no public API found; integration via scrape or bulk download.
  - AAALAC, A2LA, ANAB, OLAW, Global BioLabs API availability — all `[unknown]`; all are web-only with no documented APIs.
  - GLP consolidated facility list — `[unknown]`; weakest registry in the stack; no automated verification path identified.
  - Number of classified DoD/IC biosafety labs — `[unknown]`.
- **`[best guess]` fields needing tighter anchoring:**
  - Setup cost ($30K–$100K initial; $20K/year ongoing) — not anchored to comparable integration projects.
  - Coverage gap sizes (30–50% foreign, 40–60% no-accreditation, 200–400 OLAW/AAALAC mismatch) — need market data on synthesis customer demographics.
- **Stale data risk:** Global BioLabs 2023 figures (69 BSL-4 / 57 BSL-3+) may be superseded. Check for 2024/2025 report before operationalizing.
- **BSL flag noise:** The `bsl_claim_not_in_global_biolabs_map` flag will fire on 96% of legitimate BSL-3 labs. Consider restricting this flag to BSL-4 claims only, or treating it as informational rather than a review trigger.
