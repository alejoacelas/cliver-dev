## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check queries a BIN-attribute lookup service when a customer pays with a card on an order claiming institutional affiliation. The service returns the card's product platform (consumer vs commercial) and product subtype — Visa Purchasing, Visa Corporate T&E, Mastercard Corporate Purchasing, and similar. A commercial or purchasing card from a recognized institutional issuer (US Bank PaymentNet, JPM Chase, Citi, PNC, Bank of America Merrill) generates a `pcard_positive` signal, providing positive corroboration that the billing instrument is consistent with the claimed institutional affiliation. A consumer credit or debit card generates a `pcard_consumer_on_institutional_claim` flag, routing the order to billing-address review. The data sources range from Visa's own VBASS service (accurate but vendor-gated, requiring a Visa client relationship) to third-party BIN databases like BinDB (commercially available but with significantly lower accuracy — only 32% of third-party BIN data was found accurate compared to Visa's own data, per a Pagos analysis).

### What it catches

The check catches attacker paths where a personal consumer card is used on a claimed-institutional order. This includes dormant-account-takeover Bypass D (the attacker falls back to a personal card because institutional billing has lapsed), dormant-domain Bypass A (personal card at a permissive provider), and inbox-compromise's own-real-card and prepaid-card paths. In each case, the consumer-category BIN generates a flag that routes to manual review. The check also provides genuine positive corroboration when a legitimate institutional researcher pays with their institution's P-card, strengthening confidence in orders that would otherwise rely solely on self-reported affiliation.

### What it misses

The check has a critical structural limitation: it produces false corroboration for LLC-based attackers. All seven LLC-cluster branches (shell-company, shell-nonprofit, cro-framing, cro-identity-rotation, biotech-incubator-tenant, community-bio-lab-network, gradual-legitimacy-accumulation) use fintech business bank accounts whose cards return `commercial` or `business` in the BIN product category. The `pcard_positive` signal actively validates the attacker's cover. Similarly, inherited institutional billing paths (dormant-account-takeover Bypasses A-C, account-hijack inherited payment) produce false corroboration because the card genuinely is institutional — used by the wrong person. Invoice and PO billing paths do not present a card at all, so the check does not fire. Stage 5 classified the false-corroboration issue as Moderate: the check's positive-signal design makes it counter-productive against the largest class of attackers by branch count.

### What it costs

The cost depends heavily on the data source. VBASS and Mastercard's BIN Lookup API require enterprise relationships with uncertain pricing. Third-party aggregator APIs cost approximately $0.001-$0.01 per lookup. BinDB's downloadable database costs an estimated $300-$2,000 per year with effectively zero per-check cost after that. If the provider already uses Stripe, some card metadata is available natively at no additional cost, though Stripe does not reliably distinguish consumer from commercial. Setup cost is negligible if using PSP passthrough fields; more significant if integrating directly with Visa or Mastercard. The check is corroborative only, so reviewer burden comes from the `pcard_consumer_on_institutional_claim` flag, which fires on an estimated 15-30% of academic orders (personal-card-with-reimbursement customers).

### Operational realism

The reviewer workflow is lightweight. When a commercial/purchasing BIN is detected on an institutional-claim order, the reviewer records it as a positive corroboration signal — no customer contact needed. When a consumer BIN is detected, the reviewer asks the customer to pay via institutional PO or provide a procurement-office confirmation. Unknown BINs fall through to other M12 signals. The check's value is concentrated on US institutional customers: the P-card model is primarily a US/Canada/UK phenomenon, so an estimated 30-40% of all synthesis orders (non-US institutional) receive no meaningful signal from this check. The audit trail is a compact per-order record including BIN, scheme, product name, issuer, category, source, and classification, meeting the M12 audit-trail requirement.

### Open questions

Stage 5 identified that `pcard_positive` must never be used as a standalone pass signal — it should always be combined with other M12 checks. A suggested refinement is to split the signal into `pcard_positive_known_institutional_issuer` (US Bank, JPM, Citi on recognized institutional BIN ranges) versus `pcard_positive_generic_commercial` (any other commercial BIN), adding a layer of granularity that partially addresses the false-corroboration problem. The BIN-attribute data accuracy gap (only 32% of third-party data accurate per Pagos) is the load-bearing variable: if the provider cannot access VBASS or Mastercard APIs, they must use a third-party database with lower reliability, and the check's signal quality degrades significantly. The claim check flagged the binlist.net rate limit as weakly supported (historical documentation, not confirmed in current docs).

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** Two Moderate findings:
  - Moderate-1: BIN check produces false corroboration for all LLC-based attackers (9 bypass paths). The `pcard_positive` signal actively validates shell companies and similar entities. Recommendation: split into `pcard_positive_known_institutional_issuer` vs `pcard_positive_generic_commercial`.
  - Moderate-2: BIN check does not fire on invoice/PO/ACH billing paths — card-only by design. Implementation should explicitly document this scope limitation.
- **Minor-1:** BIN check corroborates inherited institutional cards on account-takeover paths — structural limitation requiring different controls (MFA, phone re-verification).
