## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check targets a specific attack branch: an institutional IT administrator taking over a departed researcher's idle synthesis-provider account. When any account goes 180 days without a successful login, the next login attempt triggers a hard step-up requiring the customer to re-verify their identity through the provider's IDV vendor (Persona, Onfido, or equivalent). The customer captures a new selfie and presents a government ID document; the IDV vendor compares the new selfie against the selfie stored during original onboarding. If the selfies don't match — meaning the person logging in is not the person who originally created the account — the order session is blocked and the provider contacts the institution. On successful re-verification, the customer's authenticator is re-bound: old TOTP seeds and passkeys are revoked, and a fresh WebAuthn enrollment is required. The dormancy timer is implementable through standard IdP tooling (Okta Workflows "Suspend Inactive Users" template or an Auth0 post-login Action).

### What it catches

For dormant accounts specifically, this check is highly effective. The selfie-match-against-stored-portrait design is well-suited to the dormant-account-takeover branch because the attacker (an IT admin) is a different person than the original account holder — biometric mismatch is reliable. The check catches all common MFA bypass methods when applied to dormant accounts: email-channel MFA reset, infostealer TOTP seeds, AitM phishing, FIDO2 downgrade, SIM-jacking, and cloud passkey injection are all irrelevant because the re-IDV is factor-agnostic — it asks "are you the same human?" regardless of how you authenticated. Social engineering of provider support is also addressed because the dormancy flag persists until re-IDV completes, and the manual review SOP includes an institutional directory check ("does this researcher still have an active appointment?").

### What it misses

The check is scoped to dormant accounts only. Active-account compromise (credential stuffing, session hijacking, phishing of actively-used accounts) is entirely unprotected — the dormancy timer never fires. This is by design; other M16 ideas (m16-auth0-okta step-up, m16-no-sms-no-email-reset) cover active accounts. Two implementation ambiguities remain: whether restoring a session from a stolen cookie constitutes a "login" that resets the dormancy clock (if not, the check may not fire even for dormant accounts in a session-hijack scenario), and whether federated SSO assertions from a compromised institutional IdP update the dormancy timestamp. Both are Minor findings that can be resolved by specifying that dormancy is measured against authentication events at the provider's relying-party layer, not session continuity or upstream IdP timestamps.

### What it costs

The marginal cost per re-IDV event is approximately $1.50–$3.00, based on commonly cited Persona/Onfido per-inquiry pricing (exact rate is vendor-gated). Since less than 5% of the customer base is expected to trigger re-IDV in any 6-month window, the amortized cost is well under $0.20 per active customer per year. Setup cost is 1–3 engineer-weeks for a team already running Okta or Auth0, covering the dormancy timer wiring, IDV callback plumbing, and enrollment-revocation logic. The dominant operational cost is reviewer time for the ~1–3% of re-IDV events where the selfie match fails and manual comparison is needed.

### Operational realism

The customer experience for legitimate returnees is bounded: a ~3-minute selfie + document capture through the IDV vendor's SDK, preceded by an in-product modal explaining why re-verification is needed. Automatic adjudication handles the majority of cases (selfie matches, document passes, name/DOB match). Human reviewer involvement is reserved for failures: the reviewer pulls the original and new inquiries side by side, compares selfies, and checks the institutional directory. The authenticator re-bind on approval (revoke old factors, force fresh WebAuthn enrollment) is a strong secondary control that closes the email-channel MFA recovery bypass independently. The audit trail includes the dormancy event log, the IDV vendor inquiry record, the authenticator change event, and — for escalated cases — the side-by-side selfie comparison artifact, which lets a later auditor confirm the provider actually verified "same human as original."

### Open questions

The 6-month dormancy threshold is a provider design choice, not a regulatory requirement (NIST 800-63B/4 does not mandate long-period dormancy re-proofing). The claim-check suggested sourcing a more precise anchor from FATF/FinCEN perpetual KYC guidance. The selfie-aging problem (stored selfie degrades over years) suggests that periodic re-IDV even for active accounts (on a 2–3 year cycle) would keep the stored selfie fresh and reduce appearance-change false positives. Persona API rate limits are unknown. The interaction between federated login and the dormancy timer needs specification. The session-restoration vs. fresh-login distinction needs clarification.

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** One Moderate finding (active-account gap) is by design.
- **Moderate finding (by design) — Active accounts are entirely unprotected.** The dormancy timer only fires for accounts idle >180 days. Active-account compromise (the most common attack surface) is not addressed. Covered by other M16 ideas (m16-auth0-okta, m16-no-sms-no-email-reset).

- **Minor finding — Session restoration vs. fresh login ambiguity.** Whether a stolen-cookie session restoration counts as a "login" that triggers or resets the dormancy check is unspecified. Recommended: measure dormancy against authentication events, not session continuity.
