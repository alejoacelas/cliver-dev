## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check normalizes raw shipping-address country and region text into canonical ISO 3166 codes and then cross-references the result against a curated geofence of OFAC- and EU-sanctioned sub-national territories. The normalization layer uses open-source ISO 3166-1 and -2 datasets (Debian iso-codes, Unicode CLDR, or the pycountry Python wrapper) to convert free-text country names into standardized alpha-2/alpha-3 codes. The geofence layer is a small curated table — covering Crimea, the Donetsk and Luhansk People's Republics, occupied portions of Kherson and Zaporizhzhia oblasts, and DPRK name variants — built from OFAC Executive Orders (EO 13660, EO 14065), OFAC FAQ 1009, and 31 CFR Part 589. An address matching a geofenced territory triggers an automatic block regardless of the declared country code — this is critical because an address in occupied Crimea may be declared as either "Russia" or "Ukraine" depending on the submitter's perspective. Optionally, a vendor address-verification API (Smarty, Loqate) provides higher-confidence resolution at a few cents per lookup.

### What it catches

The check's independent adversarial value is concentrated on one narrow but high-severity scenario: the CIS variant of the foreign-institution attacker story, where the attacker ships to an address in a Russian-occupied Ukrainian territory. If the address text contains a sanctioned-territory keyword (Crimea, Donetsk, Luhansk, Sevastopol), the geofence fires and auto-blocks regardless of the declared country. This catches both naive and moderately sophisticated attempts — including cases where the attacker lists the country as "Russia" to avoid Ukraine-specific screening. The normalization layer's broader value is as plumbing: it provides the clean country-code input that m06-bis-country-groups requires for its Country Chart lookup. Without correct normalization, a mismapping of "Korea" without a North/South qualifier could pass North Korea (Group E:1) as South Korea (Group A:1) — a catastrophic miss.

### What it misses

The foreign-institution branch's primary targets (Brazil, Japan, India, Indonesia, Vietnam) contain no OFAC-sanctioned sub-regions. For these destinations, the normalization succeeds trivially and the geofence returns nothing — the check produces no independent signal. Stage 5 classified all non-CIS foreign-institution methods as MISSED. For the CIS variant, two gaps remain: (1) Russian-administered postal codes for occupied territories (29xxxx for Crimea, new ranges for Donetsk/Luhansk/Kherson/Zaporizhzhia) are not explicitly mapped in the implementation — an address with only a postal code and no territorial keyword evades the geofence; and (2) non-Latin script variants of sanctioned territory names (Cyrillic "Крим," "Донецьк") may not match a Latin-only keyword table. Address masking (VPNs, re-mailing services, virtual offices) is structural: normalization processes the declared address and cannot detect that the physical location differs.

### What it costs

Marginal cost is zero for the open-ingest path (local lookup). A vendor address-verification API adds approximately $0.005–$0.015 per address at low volumes. Setup cost is estimated at $5K–$15K for dataset ingestion, geofence table construction, and the OFAC EO refresh procedure. The ongoing maintenance cost is driven by the geofence table: as the conflict evolves, the occupied-vs.-unoccupied boundary shifts, Russian postal codes are reassigned, and OFAC may change the scope of sanctions on Kherson and Zaporizhzhia without legislative action. This creates a recurring curation burden that is modest in data volume but high-stakes in consequence.

### Operational realism

Geofence hits are auto-blocked with no reviewer override — the documentation and OFAC reporting are mandatory. The more operationally complex cases are false positives: displaced Ukrainian institutions whose organizational names still contain "Donetsk" or "Luhansk" but which now operate from government-controlled territory. These are rare in absolute number (DNA synthesis orders mentioning occupied territories are near-zero) but each is high-stakes — a wrongful block on a displaced Ukrainian university is both operationally and reputationally damaging. The `country_code_mismatch` flag (text-extracted region disagrees with declared country) is a valuable secondary signal that catches inconsistencies without requiring a positive geofence hit. Records include both BIS (15 CFR § 762.6) and OFAC (31 CFR § 501.601) retention obligations at 5 years each.

### Open questions

The partial-oblast problem — distinguishing occupied from unoccupied portions of Donetsk, Luhansk, Kherson, and Zaporizhzhia — has no clean off-the-shelf solution. Postal-code-based approaches either over-block (include government-controlled postal codes) or under-block (miss occupied-zone codes reassigned by Russian authorities). The Kelley Drye analysis cited in stage 6 documents the industry's struggle with this. Kherson and Zaporizhzhia are NOT in OFAC's comprehensively-sanctioned territory set as of late 2025/early 2026 — the SOP must distinguish "comprehensively sanctioned" (Crimea, DPR, LPR) from "subject to material support / EO 14024 risk" (Kherson, Zaporizhzhia). This distinction could change without notice under the Secretary of Treasury's EO 14065 authority.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** The check's near-zero leverage against non-CIS foreign-institution targets is structural — those countries have no sanctioned sub-regions.
- **`[unknown]` fields affecting policy implications:**
  - Address masking (VPN, re-mailing, virtual offices) prevalence in synthesis: no quantitative data. Normalization cannot detect this; complementary checks (m03-pobox-regex-sop, m03-usps-rdi-cmra, IP geolocation) are required.
- **Stage 5 Moderate findings requiring specification updates:**
  - Russian-administered postal codes for occupied territories: implementation should add a postal-code-to-region mapping (Pochta Rossii index ranges for Crimea 29xxxx and newly assigned ranges for Donetsk/Luhansk/Kherson/Zaporizhzhia). Source for postal codes not cited.
  - Non-Latin script geofence keywords: implementation should specify that the geofence keyword table includes Cyrillic, Ukrainian, and Russian-language variants of all sanctioned territory names, not just English transliterations.
- **06F flags not fully resolved:**
  - Gap 2 occupied-vs.-unoccupied area estimates (60–70% of Donetsk, ~90% of Luhansk) are best guesses from front-line maps; UNHCR/OCHA displacement figures could strengthen.
  - Gap 5 Russian-administered postal code ranges mentioned without a specific source (Pochta Rossii or comparative analysis).
- **Regulatory uncertainty:** Kherson and Zaporizhzhia sanctions scope can change without legislative action under EO 14065 — requires ongoing monitoring.
- **Cross-measure dependency:** This check is a prerequisite for m06-bis-country-groups. Normalization failures (especially North/South Korea ambiguity) propagate as catastrophic misses to the Country Chart lookup.
- **False-positive sensitivity:** Displaced Ukrainian institutions with "Donetsk"/"Luhansk" in their name are rare but high-stakes. An allowlist or organizational-status verification step may be warranted.
