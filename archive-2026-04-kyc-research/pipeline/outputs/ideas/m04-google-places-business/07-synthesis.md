## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check queries the Google Places API (New) Nearby Search endpoint at the coordinates of a customer's shipping address. It returns a list of businesses at or near the address with their types, names, photos, reviews, and operational status. A synthesis provider would compare the returned business categories against the customer's claimed institution. If no business is present, or only residential-adjacent categories (lodging, apartment building) appear, the system flags the order for human review. If the matched business has a type incompatible with the customer's claim, a category-mismatch flag fires. The check works globally wherever Google Places has data, but signal quality is strongest in US/EU urban areas and degrades in developing regions.

### What it catches

The check catches residential-address bypass variants across several attacker stories. When an attacker ships to a home with no Google Business Profile listing, the `no_places_business_at_address` flag fires. This catches the cheapest configurations: the community-bio-lab-network's residential garage lab, the dormant-domain story's $0 residential colocation, and the residential edge cases in cro-framing and gradual-legitimacy-accumulation. For the foreign-institution story, it catches residential addresses in OECD urban areas where Places coverage is robust. Unlike the county-assessor check, this idea provides some international coverage, though it is uneven.

### What it misses

All commercial-address bypass methods pass cleanly. Virtual offices (Regus, IWG), coworking spaces (WeWork), maker spaces, executive suites, and biotech incubator benches all have legitimate Google Places listings and produce no flag. The stage 5 hardening assessment identified a Moderate gap: the `places_category_mismatch` flag lacks a defined list of what categories are suspicious for a claimed lab. A virtual-office brand at a claimed CRO's address arguably should fire the mismatch flag, but defining this would require a curated brand list and would generate false positives for legitimate startups at shared workspaces. International coverage is a structural gap: in non-OECD rural areas and China, the check produces unreliable or no signal, affecting an estimated 10–20% of international orders.

### What it costs

Marginal cost per check is low: $0.032–$0.035 depending on the FieldMask SKU tier. This is among the cheapest API-based checks in the pipeline. Setup cost is minimal (API key + ~1 day integration), but legal review of the Google Maps Platform ToS is the gating cost. The 04C claim check clarified that the restrictive ToS clause (prohibiting use for housing/employment/credit/insurance decisions) specifically targets the Places Aggregate API, not necessarily the standard Places API, so the structural risk may be weaker than initially written. A legal opinion is still required before deployment.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is lightweight. When a flag fires, the reviewer sees the Places query response alongside the customer's claimed institution. Cross-referencing with Street View for signage is a quick manual step. The multi-tenant building mismatch is the main source of reviewer friction: in biotech hubs, a significant fraction of lab tenants share buildings where the dominant Places listing is the landlord or a coffee shop, not the lab. This generates category-mismatch flags that require manual determination of tenancy. Cross-referencing with m05-incubator-tenant helps for known incubator buildings. Every disposition is logged with the Place ID and adjudication rationale.

### Open questions

The ToS structural risk is the dominant unresolved issue. If Google's legal position is that standard Places API data cannot be used for B2B customer screening, this idea is undeployable regardless of its technical merits. The 04C claim check narrowed the risk (the restrictive clause targets the Aggregate API specifically), but this has not been confirmed with Google. Additionally, the estimate that 30–50% of sub-12-month startups lack a Google Places listing is an ungrounded best guess; the actual false-positive rate for new labs is unknown. Rate limits (default 600 QPM) may need to be raised for high-volume providers but the process for doing so is undocumented.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Structural: Google Maps Platform ToS risk.** The restrictive clause in the service-specific terms may prohibit using Places API output for customer screening. The 04C claim check clarified this clause targets the Places Aggregate API specifically, but a legal opinion is needed. If the use is prohibited, this idea is undeployable.
- **04C OVERSTATED: ToS scope conflation.** The stage 4 document conflated the Places Aggregate API ToS with the standard Places API ToS. The structural risk section should be revised to reflect this distinction (not done in v1 since no re-research loop was triggered).
- **[unknown] Rate limits:** Default 600 QPM documented; process for raising is unknown.
- **Stage 5 Moderate finding: category-mismatch lacks defined suspicious-type list.** The `places_category_mismatch` flag has no enumerated list of which business types are incompatible with a claimed lab. A curated virtual-office brand list could tighten coverage but would increase false positives.
- **06F flag: thin proxy for new-lab listing rate.** The 30–50% estimate for sub-12-month startups lacking a Places listing has no external citation. Direction is plausible but magnitude is speculative.
- **06F flag: multi-tenant building mismatch rate (20–40%)** is a best guess with [unknown] admission but no proxy data.
- **International coverage quantification.** The 10–20% figure for orders from Places-thin regions is a best guess. No direct data on Google Places listing density by country exists in the research.
