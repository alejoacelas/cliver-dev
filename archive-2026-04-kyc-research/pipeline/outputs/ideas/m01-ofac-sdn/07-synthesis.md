## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

An OFAC SDN screen runs the customer's legal name, date of birth, and address against the US Treasury's Specially Designated Nationals list and the Consolidated Sanctions list. The Treasury publishes these lists as free, public-domain XML/CSV files that can be pulled on a schedule with no authentication. Alternatively, a provider can call the OpenSanctions API (EUR 0.10 per call) or ofac-api.com (vendor-gated pricing), both of which wrap the same underlying list data with fuzzy matching and a structured response. The check produces a match score; any score above a threshold routes to a human reviewer who disambiguates using secondary identifiers (DOB, nationality, address) before allowing or blocking the order.

### What it catches

OFAC screening catches customers whose names appear on US sanctions lists — individuals and entities designated by the US government for involvement in terrorism, narcotics trafficking, weapons proliferation, or connections to sanctioned regimes (Russia, Iran, DPRK, Syria, Venezuela). In principle, this addresses the "denied individual" and "sanctioned-jurisdiction-routing" attacker stories. In practice, stage 5 found that none of the 19 modeled attacker stories actually engage sanctions screening — every modeled attacker has a name that trivially clears the check. The check's biosecurity value is therefore limited to the rare case where a customer happens to already be designated. Its primary value is legal compliance: US entities are required to screen against OFAC.

### What it misses

The check provides zero signal for the overwhelming majority of customers, both legitimate and malicious. The SDN list contains roughly 12,000-15,000 entries against a global pool of potential synthesis customers numbering in the tens of thousands. Novel biosecurity threat actors with no prior connection to a sanctioned program will never appear. The check is also US-jurisdictional: customers designated on EU, UK, or UN lists but not on OFAC would pass, leaving ~63% of the gene synthesis market (non-US) with a jurisdictional gap unless paired with m01-global-sanctions-union. Customers who provide only a name without DOB or nationality create unresolvable ambiguity on any fuzzy match.

### What it costs

Self-hosted on the free Treasury SLS feed, the marginal cost is $0 per check with an estimated 1-3 engineer-weeks of setup. Via OpenSanctions, it costs EUR 0.10 (~$0.11) per call. The real cost is reviewer time on false positives: common-name collisions disproportionately affect Chinese, Iranian, and Russian researchers (who may constitute 25-35% of academic synthesis customers globally and face false-positive rates perhaps 5-10x higher than researchers from countries with few SDN entries). Each false positive requires a reviewer to compare secondary identifiers, document the rationale, and release the order.

### Operational realism

The reviewer workflow is straightforward: hit appears in queue with matched entry, score, and fields; reviewer compares secondary identifiers; confirmed hits trigger an order freeze and a blocked-property report filed within 10 business days per 31 CFR 501.603; false positives are documented and released. The main operational challenge is threshold tuning: too aggressive catches more true positives but generates a flood of false positives; too lenient risks regulatory exposure. Weak-AKA entries (OFAC's own designation for low-quality aliases) add complexity — these should be lower-priority but still reviewed. Vendor API outages require fallback to a local copy of the SLS file. The audit trail (list snapshot date, score, matched entry, reviewer disposition) is well-defined and standard for regulatory compliance.

### Open questions

The 4C claim check flagged three minor overstatements about ofac-api.com (refresh cadence, source filter syntax citation, fuzzy-match algorithm attribution) that were corrected with hedging but not independently re-verified. More fundamentally, the question is whether OFAC screening should be treated as a standalone idea or merged with m01-global-sanctions-union into a single "comprehensive sanctions screening" implementation, since OpenSanctions bundles OFAC with all international lists for the same EUR 0.10 per call.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Zero attacker-story engagement:** Stage 5 found no modeled attacker stories engage this measure. OFAC screening is a legal compliance requirement, not a biosecurity detection tool. Its inclusion in a biosecurity pipeline is table-stakes, not a differentiating signal.
- **4C minor overstatements on ofac-api.com (REVISE verdict):** Three claims about ofac-api.com (refresh cadence, source filter syntax, fuzzy-match algorithm) were overstated in the implementation doc. Corrections applied via hedging but not independently re-fetched. Low impact since ofac-api.com is one of three implementation options.
- **Potential merge with m01-global-sanctions-union:** If implemented via OpenSanctions, OFAC screening and international sanctions screening share the same backend and API call. A policy decision is needed on whether to treat them as separate ideas for audit/reporting or merge into one.
- **[vendor-gated] ofac-api.com pricing:** Pricing requires account/sales contact.
- **[vendor-gated] OpenSanctions USD pricing and volume tiers:** Only EUR list price is publicly visible.
- **Discriminatory false-positive burden:** Chinese, Iranian, and Russian researchers face structurally higher false-positive rates. This creates equity concerns and may deter legitimate international researchers from using providers with rigorous screening. Requires policy input.
- **Secondary-identifier collection at onboarding:** The check's effectiveness depends heavily on collecting DOB, nationality, and address at customer signup. Whether synthesis providers currently collect this data (and whether customers will tolerate providing it) is an operational question.
