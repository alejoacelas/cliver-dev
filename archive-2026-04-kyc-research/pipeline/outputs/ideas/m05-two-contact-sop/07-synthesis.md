## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This is a manual reviewer SOP designed for borderline address-association cases that automated checks cannot resolve. When upstream M05 checks (campus polygon, incubator tenant directory, ROR/GLEIF) produce ambiguous results, the SOP fires and a compliance reviewer independently locates two contacts from the institution's public directory: one via phone (main switchboard, biosafety officer, or research compliance) and one via email (departmental admin on the `.edu` domain). Both channels must independently confirm that the named customer is affiliated with the institution and that the shipping address is an authorized receiving location. The SOP explicitly prohibits using any contact information provided by the customer, ensuring independence. The regulatory anchor is the HHS SynNA Guidance 2023 callback recommendation and the IGSC Harmonized Screening Protocol v3.0 independent-verification requirement.

### What it catches

The SOP is highly effective against shell entities, virtual offices, and fabricated institutions. When an attacker ships to a virtual office under a shell nonprofit, a CRO LLC, or a revived dormant-domain entity, the reviewer discovers that the "institution" has no real public directory, no switchboard, and no departmental email — the `two_contact_independence_failure` flag fires, which is treated as a strong negative signal. This cleanly catches shell-nonprofit, cro-framing (virtual-office path), dormant-domain, and cro-identity-rotation stories. For the inbox-compromise story, the SOP is effective because the reviewer calls the real institution (not the compromised customer) and asks about a CMRA address that the institution would not recognize as authorized. The SOP produces the strongest paper trail of any check in the pipeline: a human-to-human attestation with domain-verified email replies from institutional staff.

### What it misses

The SOP has three categories of blind spots. First, post-shipment diversions (carrier redirect via UPS My Choice / FedEx Delivery Manager, USPS COA, physical mailroom interception) occur after the provider releases the package and are invisible to any pre-shipment SOP. Second, genuine-but-malicious affiliations defeat the SOP by design: when an attacker holds a real visiting-researcher appointment, a real incubator bench lease, or a real lab position, the institution's staff will confirm the affiliation and the authorized address. The SOP confirms what the attacker constructed. Third, the accomplice-at-real-address path for the foreign-institution story is undetectable because the institution confirms affiliation and the address is the institution's own. The hardening assessment also identified that the SOP's trigger on in-system address changes is unspecified — if address changes bypass the SOP trigger, attackers in the inbox-compromise, account-hijack, and dormant-account-takeover stories can modify the approved shipping address without re-verification.

### What it costs

This is the most expensive check in the pipeline per case: $30–$60 per case at 30–60 minutes of reviewer time. Throughput is approximately 6–10 cases per day per full-time reviewer. A mid-size provider with 50K orders/year and a 5% borderline rate would need roughly 1 FTE dedicated to this SOP. Setup cost is $5K–$20K for SOP documentation, reviewer training, and template development. The high per-case cost means the SOP is appropriate only for escalated cases, not bulk screening — it should sit behind automated checks that filter out clear-pass and clear-fail cases.

### Operational realism

The SOP works well within a specific institutional archetype: mid-to-large academic institutions in English-speaking countries with public directories, multi-person labs, and a willingness to respond to third-party verification requests. For this population (estimated 40–60% of academic customers), the check produces reliable, high-quality signal with an unmatched paper trail. Outside this population, the SOP fails or produces false positives. Industry customers at large companies (30–45% of orders) have switchboards that will not confirm individual employees' purchasing authority. EU institutions under GDPR suppress directory entries. Foreign-language institutions present phone barriers. Single-PI labs have no independent second contact. Institutions that refuse third-party verification on principle produce false blocks on legitimate customers. The coverage research recommends pairing this SOP with automated checks (campus polygon, ROR/GLEIF) to reduce case volume and concentrate the SOP on cases where it is most likely to succeed.

### Open questions

The SOP does not specify whether it re-fires on in-system address changes — this is a Moderate hardening finding that affects the inbox-compromise, account-hijack, and dormant-account-takeover stories. The SOP also does not define how to handle non-traditional "institutions" (maker spaces, coworking, executive suites) that have some public contacts but not the two-channel directory the SOP assumes. The HHS SynNA Guidance 2023 quote is paraphrased from a web-search summary and should be verified verbatim against the PDF before publication. The IGSC v3.0 "verify independently" language is confirmed but the exact section should be cited.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Stage 5 Moderate: SOP trigger on address changes is unspecified.** The implementation does not state whether the SOP re-fires when a customer's shipping address is changed in-system. If not, attackers in the inbox-compromise, account-hijack, and dormant-account-takeover stories can modify the approved address without re-verification. Recommendation: explicitly require re-triggering on any shipping-address change.
- **Stage 5 Moderate: genuine-affiliation attacks are confirmed rather than caught.** The biotech-incubator-tenant, visiting-researcher, and gradual-legitimacy-accumulation stories involve attackers with real institutional affiliations. The SOP confirms these affiliations, producing a positive result for a malicious actor. Not addressable by this SOP — requires M18/M19/M20 legitimacy checks.
- **Stage 5 Moderate: post-shipment carrier redirect is structurally unaddressable.** No pre-shipment address-association SOP can catch UPS My Choice / FedEx Delivery Manager diversions. Requires carrier-level controls outside this idea's scope.
- **Stage 5 Moderate: accomplice-at-real-address (foreign-institution Method 2).** Institution confirms affiliation, address is the institution's own, accomplice receives and forwards. Not addressable by this SOP.
- **Stage 5 Minor: maker space / coworking / executive suite classification.** SOP does not define minimum criteria for what qualifies as a "public directory" for non-traditional institutions.
- **Stage 5 Minor: foreign-language barrier decision rule unspecified.** Implementation acknowledges the failure mode but does not specify when to use a translation service vs. when to flag as `two_contact_independence_failure`.
- **Stage 5 Minor: IT-persona-manufacturing contact priority.** SOP should specify that channel 1 (phone) targets research compliance / biosafety / EHS rather than department front desk, to maximize the chance of catching directory-only personas.
- **04C soft note: HHS SynNA Guidance quote is paraphrased.** Exact verbatim wording should be verified against the PDF before publication.
- **06F flag: corporate switchboard refusal rate (50–70%)** and **GDPR directory suppression rate (30–50%)** are both key best-guess estimates without external derivation.
- **06F flag: single-PI lab fraction (10–20%)** is a best guess with [unknown] search terms.
- **Scalability constraint.** At $30–$60/case and ~6–10 cases/day/FTE, this SOP is viable only for borderline escalations. Should be positioned behind automated M05 checks (campus polygon, ROR/GLEIF) to filter the case load.
