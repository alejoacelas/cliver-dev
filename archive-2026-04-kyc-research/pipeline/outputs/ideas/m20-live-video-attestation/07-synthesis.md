## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check requires a voucher — typically a PI, biosafety officer, or department head at the customer's institution — to personally attest to the legitimacy of each sequence-of-concern order via a structured, recorded process. At onboarding and annually, the voucher participates in a 15-minute video call (Mode A) where they present government ID, state the SOC names and intended use for the order, answer challenge questions testing whether they have actually reviewed the order, and affirm a standardized accountability statement that the call is recorded and may be reviewed in legal proceedings. For subsequent SOC orders by the same customer-voucher pair, the voucher completes a digital sign-off form (Mode B) requiring them to type the SOC names and intended use, answer a randomized challenge question, and digitally sign a per-order declaration. Rubber-stamping detection analyzes submission timing: if the voucher completes Mode B forms in under 30 seconds across more than 3 consecutive orders, the system auto-escalates to a full Mode A video call. Self-vouching is blocked by comparing government ID names between the orderer and voucher.

### What it catches

The check catches three threat classes. First, fake or synthetic voucher identities: the government ID check and live video call verify that the voucher is a real person matching their claimed identity, making it difficult for synthetic-identity factories to fabricate vouchers at scale. Second, inattentive rubber-stamping — the dominant mechanism by which legitimate-but-negligent vouchers approve orders without scrutiny. The v2 per-order content attestation forces the voucher to engage with order-specific details (SOC names, intended use, experiment description), converting "approve without reading" into "read, then approve." The accountability statement raises the perceived personal risk: the voucher is now on a recorded, order-specific, liability-framed record saying "I have reviewed this order and believe it is legitimate." Third, batch approval without review: Mode B's per-order requirement prevents a voucher from approving multiple orders in a single blanket sign-off. The rubber-stamping detection catches the fastest attempts to circumvent this.

### What it misses

The check has two fundamental structural limitations. First, it cannot detect malicious intent by a knowing-complicit voucher. A co-conspirator voucher who is briefed on the order details will name the SOCs, explain the (false) intended use coherently, answer challenge questions convincingly, and affirm the accountability statement with full awareness of the implications. No identity or content attestation can distinguish this from a legitimate voucher. The accountability framing shifts the cost calculation (the co-conspirator is now personally on record) but does not block the attack. Second, the human reviewer's ability to detect deepfakes is demonstrably poor: cited research shows human detection rates of approximately 24.5% for high-quality video deepfakes. A well-resourced adversary using real-time face-swap technology with a briefed human operator behind the deepfake has a plausible >50% chance of passing the human visual assessment. The implementation includes "optional vendor augmentation" for deepfake detection, but framing this as optional understates the vulnerability — for SOC orders involving sophisticated adversaries, vendor-level deepfake detection should be a strong recommendation or requirement. Additionally, Mode B digital sign-offs are vulnerable to delegation: a voucher may share SSO credentials with a lab manager or assistant who fills out the form on their behalf, breaking the accountability link without triggering any automated detection.

### What it costs

Mode A video attestation costs approximately $25–30 per session (15-minute call plus 10-minute write-up). Mode B digital sign-off costs approximately $2–5 per order in reviewer time. For a typical customer with an established voucher placing 10 SOC orders per year, the annual cost is approximately $75–80 ($25–30 for the annual Mode A renewal plus ~$50 for 10 Mode B sign-offs). Setup cost is $30–55K, covering video-call infrastructure, the Mode B form platform, rubber-stamping detection logic, order-detail distribution automation, and analyst training. Optional deepfake-detection vendor integration (Facia.ai, Sensity AI) adds per-session costs of $0.10–$0.50 but vendor pricing is gated behind sales contacts.

### Operational realism

The Mode A/Mode B split is the central design choice for operational scalability. Mode A (full video call) is resource-intensive but high-assurance: the reviewer sees the voucher's face, hears their voice, checks their ID, and poses order-specific questions in real time. Confining Mode A to onboarding, annual renewals, and auto-escalations keeps the call volume manageable. Mode B (per-order digital sign-off) scales to high SOC-order volumes with minimal reviewer burden; the 20% random spot-check provides quality assurance without reviewing every submission. The rubber-stamping detection is a behavioral signal — it catches the most egregious cases (sub-30-second submissions) and auto-escalates to Mode A, creating a credible threat that discourages minimal-effort sign-offs. The accountability statement is a deliberate friction element: by explicitly framing the recording as potential legal evidence, it converts the video call from a "verification formality" to an "on-the-record accountability event." The full audit trail — per-order attestation records, recording hashes, rubric scores, timing data, and digital signatures — provides a comprehensive compliance record for each SOC order.

### Open questions

The 30-second / 3-consecutive-order rubber-stamping threshold is a starting calibration that may need adjustment after observing actual submission-time distributions. The fraction of Mode B sign-offs completed by delegates rather than the named voucher is unquantified and may be substantial (~10–20% in academic labs where credential sharing is common). The deepfake detection gap is the most concerning technical vulnerability: human visual assessment is unreliable, and the "optional" vendor augmentation should be reconsidered for SOC orders. No data exists on PI willingness to vouch under the v2 accountability framing — the liability-explicit language may reduce voucher availability for risk-averse PIs, creating a tension between deterrence and access.
