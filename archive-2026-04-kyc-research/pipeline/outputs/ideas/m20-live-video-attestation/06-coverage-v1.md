# Coverage research: Live video attestation with per-order content attestation and challenge questions

## Coverage gaps

### Gap 1: Vouchers who rubber-stamp despite accountability framing (determined co-conspirators or negligent PIs)
- **Category:** Legitimate or compromised vouchers who go through the Mode A video call and Mode B digital sign-off process but provide rote, minimally-compliant answers without genuine scrutiny of the order. This includes: (a) PIs who are knowingly assisting a malicious actor (co-conspirators); (b) PIs who are overwhelmed and treat the attestation as another compliance checkbox despite the accountability framing.
- **Estimated size:** [unknown — searched for: "compliance attestation rubber stamping rate academic research", "PI rubber stamping IRB attestation frequency"]. The implementation doc's Mode B rubber-stamping detection (30-second threshold) catches the most egregious cases. However, a voucher who takes 2 minutes to fill out a form with stock answers ("for our ongoing CRISPR research") passes the timing check while still not genuinely reviewing the order. [best guess: the v2 per-order content attestation and challenge questions substantially raise the bar compared to v1, but determined rubber-stampers who provide plausible-sounding answers (especially PIs familiar with the customer's general research area) will pass. Perhaps 5-15% of Mode B sign-offs involve minimal genuine scrutiny despite passing all automated checks.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the check collects attestation content that creates a deterrent record, but cannot distinguish genuine scrutiny from sophisticated rote compliance.
- **Reasoning:** The v2 accountability framing converts rubber-stamping from a private act to a recorded, order-specific, liability-creating act. This is a meaningful deterrent for risk-averse PIs (the inattention class) but not for co-conspirators who accept the liability. The coverage gap is the residual population of vouchers who are either co-conspirators or who have habituated to the accountability framing.

### Gap 2: Customers in time zones or regions where video-call scheduling creates prohibitive friction
- **Category:** Legitimate customers whose vouchers are in time zones far from the provider's compliance operations team, creating scheduling friction for Mode A video calls. Also includes vouchers at institutions in regions with unreliable internet connectivity where video calls are technically difficult.
- **Estimated size:** Industry data shows KYC onboarding abandonment rates of 40-60% when processes are poorly designed [source](https://www.jumio.com/how-to-reduce-customer-abandonment/). Video KYC calls average 3-4 minutes but scheduling and coordination can add days of delay. [best guess: for a US-based provider, vouchers in East Asia (+12-15h offset), South Asia (+10-12h offset), and Africa represent perhaps 20-30% of the global voucher pool. Scheduling a 15-minute video call across these time zones adds 2-5 business days of delay per attestation. Abandonment is unlikely for established research relationships but creates friction and delays orders.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-positive (in the sense of creating undue friction for legitimate customers) — the voucher is real and willing but the scheduling logistics delay or complicate the order.
- **Reasoning:** The implementation doc mentions "timezone coordination and potentially language barriers" as a failure mode but does not quantify the affected population. Mode B digital sign-off (for subsequent orders) partially mitigates this for established vouchers, but the annual Mode A renewal and first-time voucher onboarding still require a live call.

### Gap 3: Vouchers who lack SOC-specific knowledge for the ordered sequences
- **Category:** Legitimate vouchers (typically department heads, biosafety officers, or senior PIs) who can attest to the customer's general institutional affiliation and research area but do not have detailed knowledge of the specific sequences of concern in the order. The v2 challenge questions require the voucher to name SOCs and explain the experiment, which may exceed the voucher's actual knowledge even for legitimate orders.
- **Estimated size:** The implementation doc mitigates this for large orders (>10 SOCs: voucher names 3 representative ones). However, even for small orders, a department-head voucher may not know the specific gene names in a student's order. [best guess: for vouchers who are not the direct PI of the customer's project (e.g., department chairs, BSOs vouching for a lab they oversee), perhaps 30-50% cannot name specific SOCs without referring to notes. The implementation doc's rubric item is "voucher correctly named SOC(s)" — a voucher who says "I'd need to check my notes" fails this item, triggering an escalation for what may be a perfectly legitimate order.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-positive — legitimate voucher fails the challenge-question rubric despite the order being legitimate, triggering hold/escalation.
- **Reasoning:** The tension is between testing genuine order scrutiny (which requires SOC-specific knowledge) and the reality that many vouchers have project-level but not order-level knowledge. The implementation doc partially addresses this by accepting "project-level answers" but the rubric item for naming SOCs is binary (Y/N), creating a hard gate.

### Gap 4: Deepfake attacks that defeat the human reviewer's visual assessment
- **Category:** Sophisticated adversaries who use real-time deepfake technology to impersonate a voucher on the Mode A video call. The reviewer's visual assessment ("no technical signs of deepfake") is the primary detection mechanism, supplemented by optional vendor augmentation.
- **Estimated size:** Human detection rates for high-quality video deepfakes are approximately 24.5%, meaning humans fail to identify ~75% of deepfakes [source](https://www.brightdefense.com/resources/deepfake-statistics/). An iProov study found only 0.1% of participants correctly identified all fake and real media [source](https://deepstrike.io/blog/deepfake-statistics-2025). Real-time deepfake technology is increasingly accessible — widely available detection technology catches only about 65% of deepfakes, and detection effectiveness drops 45-50% against real-world (non-lab) deepfakes [source](https://www.brightdefense.com/resources/deepfake-statistics/). [best guess: a well-resourced adversary using current real-time face-swap technology has a >50% chance of passing a human reviewer's visual assessment on a standard video call. The v2 challenge questions and per-order content attestation add barriers (the deepfake must also produce coherent spoken answers about the order), but if the attacker has briefed a human operator behind the deepfake, this is achievable.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-negative — the deepfake passes as a real person, and the attestation content is provided by a human operator behind the deepfake facade.
- **Reasoning:** The implementation doc acknowledges deepfake risk and includes "no technical signs of deepfake (Y/N)" as a rubric item plus optional vendor augmentation. The coverage gap is that human visual detection is demonstrably unreliable against current deepfake technology, and the "optional" vendor augmentation means many implementations will rely solely on human judgment.

### Gap 5: Orders where no qualified voucher exists or is willing to attest
- **Category:** Legitimate SOC orders from customers who cannot identify a qualified voucher. This includes: (a) independent researchers or consultants not affiliated with an institution; (b) researchers at institutions where no senior colleague is willing to serve as a voucher (common for controversial research or for junior researchers new to an institution); (c) researchers at small companies where the only person with relevant knowledge is the orderer themselves (blocked by the self-vouch deconfliction rule).
- **Estimated size:** [best guess: independent researchers and sole-proprietor consultants represent a small fraction of SOC-order customers — perhaps 2-5%. However, the "no willing voucher" problem may be more common: PIs who are too busy, who don't want the liability exposure of the accountability statement, or who don't know the customer well enough to vouch. The v2 accountability framing (recorded, liability-creating) may make some PIs *less* willing to vouch, not more. The net effect on voucher availability is unknown.] [unknown — searched for: "PI willingness to vouch for student orders synthesis", "voucher availability biosecurity screening"]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** no-signal — the order cannot proceed without a voucher. For legitimate customers who genuinely cannot find a voucher, this is a hard block.
- **Reasoning:** The implementation doc does not specify a fallback for customers with no available voucher. The order is presumably held indefinitely or rejected. For the small population of legitimate customers in this category, the check creates a complete barrier to service rather than a false positive.

### Gap 6: Mode B digital sign-off circumvention by delegated signers
- **Category:** Vouchers who delegate Mode B sign-off duties to a lab manager, administrative assistant, or student, who then fills out the form on the voucher's behalf using the voucher's credentials. The digital sign-off captures IP, timestamp, and typed content but cannot verify that the person filling out the form is the voucher rather than a delegate.
- **Estimated size:** [best guess: in academic labs, administrative delegation of compliance tasks is common. Perhaps 10-20% of Mode B sign-offs are completed by someone other than the named voucher, particularly for high-volume labs where the PI oversees many orders. The rubber-stamping detection (timing analysis) does not catch delegation — a delegate who takes 3 minutes to fill out the form passes all automated checks.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the form is completed but the accountability link (voucher personally attested) is broken. The recorded content and digital signature create a nominal paper trail, but the actual attestation was not by the voucher.
- **Reasoning:** Institutional SSO authentication partially mitigates this (the delegate must have access to the voucher's SSO credentials), but shared credentials within labs are not uncommon. The implementation doc does not address delegation as a failure mode.

## Refined false-positive qualitative

1. **Vouchers failing SOC-naming challenge** (Gap 3): The dominant false-positive source. Department-head or BSO vouchers who oversee (but don't directly supervise) the customer's work may not know specific gene names, triggering the `voucher_soc_content_failed` flag. The mitigation (accepting project-level answers for >10 SOC orders) helps for large orders but the rubric is still binary for small orders.
2. **Timezone scheduling friction** (Gap 2): Not a false positive in the technical sense (no flag is thrown) but creates significant operational friction for the ~20-30% of vouchers in distant time zones, disproportionately affecting customers at Asian and African institutions.
3. **PI unwillingness to vouch under accountability framing** (Gap 5): The liability-explicit framing may deter risk-averse PIs from vouching, converting a willing voucher into an unwilling one. This is the intended deterrent effect for the negligent-PI class but may also affect cautious-but-legitimate PIs.
4. **Rubber-stamping detection false positives**: Vouchers who are fast typists or who complete the form quickly because they genuinely know the order well may trip the 30-second threshold, especially if they pre-read the email summary carefully before opening the form.

## Notes for stage 7 synthesis

- The v2 per-order content attestation and accountability framing are substantial improvements over v1 for the "rubber-stamping by inattention" threat class. The deterrent effect of a recorded, order-specific, liability-framed video call is real and difficult to quantify but likely significant for risk-averse PIs.
- The deepfake gap (Gap 4) is the most concerning for the "sophisticated adversary" threat model. Human deepfake detection rates (~24.5%) are far too low for this to be a reliable check against state-level actors. The "optional vendor augmentation" for deepfake detection should be upgraded to a strong recommendation or requirement for SOC orders.
- The structural limitation is Gap 1: no amount of procedural hardening can distinguish a determined co-conspirator who provides genuinely knowledgeable, order-specific answers from a legitimate voucher. The v2 accountability framing shifts the cost calculation (the co-conspirator is now on a recorded, liability-creating record) but does not eliminate the gap.
- Mode B digital sign-off is a well-designed friction reduction for established vouchers, but the delegation risk (Gap 6) and the rubber-stamping residual (Gap 1) mean it provides meaningfully lower assurance than Mode A. The spot-check rate (20% random sample) should be calibrated based on observed rubber-stamping rates after deployment.
