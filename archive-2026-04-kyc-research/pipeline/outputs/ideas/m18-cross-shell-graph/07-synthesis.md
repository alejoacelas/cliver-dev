## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The cross-shell rotation graph is an internal database that stores a small set of identifying fingerprints for every institution that has previously been screened by the synthesis provider. Fingerprints include: registered agent, officer/director name with month-year of birth, beneficial owner, registered address, GLEIF corporate parent, domain registrant, TLS certificate serial number and issuer, hosting infrastructure fingerprint (IP, ASN, JARM hash), and NIH PI identifier. These are sourced from Companies House (officers, PSCs), OpenCorporates (US SOS agent/officer data), GLEIF (parent/ultimate-parent relationships), Censys (hosting/TLS pivots), crt.sh or SSLMate (certificate transparency), WHOIS/RDAP, and NIH RePORTER. When a new customer arrives, the system projects their fingerprints onto the graph and fires a flag whenever two or more independent fingerprints match a previously screened entity — with special attention to entities that were previously denied or flagged. A linked-entity panel is presented to a human reviewer who evaluates each shared fingerprint, applying signal-strength weighting (shared CT cert = very high; shared officer with DoB = medium-high; mass-formation agent = noise).

### What it catches

The check's primary design target is the CRO identity rotation pattern, where a single operator stands up serial shell entities to avoid cumulative scrutiny. When the operator reuses officers, registered agents, hosting infrastructure, or TLS certificates across shells — the common, low-cost approach — the graph detects the linkage. This also catches serial shell operators across the shell-nonprofit and shell-company stories. The check provides complementary signal to the other m18 ideas: where the Companies House/SOS check confirms legal existence and the accreditation stack verifies claims, the cross-shell graph detects the cross-entity linkage that no single-entity check can see. The strongest fingerprint dimensions are shared CT cert serial (very high signal, hard to fake), shared officer name with DoB (medium-high), and shared GLEIF parent (high for entities with LEIs).

### What it misses

The check has three structural blind spots. First, the cold-start problem: the graph provides zero signal for a first-time operator with no prior entities in the screening database. A new provider or one with thin history sees near-zero matches for one to two years. Second, a well-funded attacker can defeat the graph by using different accomplices, different registered agents, different hosting, and different domains for each shell entity — achieving zero fingerprint overlap at significantly increased cost and complexity. Third, the graph detects cross-entity linkage but not intra-entity fingerprint changes: a dormant-domain attacker who changes the officers and hosting of a single reanimated entity is not caught because there is no second entity to link to. Identity-layer attacks (inbox compromise, credential compromise, IT persona manufacturing) are entirely outside scope — the institution is real. Single-entity patience strategies (gradual legitimacy accumulation, community bio lab) produce no graph signal because there is only one entity.

### What it costs

Most fingerprint sources are free (Companies House, GLEIF, crt.sh, NIH RePORTER). OpenCorporates and Censys add marginal cost of roughly $0.10–$1.00 per customer when all sources are queried. The dominant cost is engineering: building the graph schema, ingestion pipelines, fingerprint normalization, fuzzy matching for officer-name variants, and historical backfill of all prior customers. Setup cost is estimated at $80K–$300K, with $30K–$100K/year ongoing. The wide range reflects uncertainty about fuzzy-matching complexity and the depth of historical backfill required. Storage cost is trivial at this fingerprint granularity (under $500/month). SSLMate ($1,000/month) replaces crt.sh for production-grade CT searching.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is well-specified with per-fingerprint signal weighting. In practice, the two most operationally demanding maintenance tasks are the mass-formation-agent discount list and the cloud-hosting noise filter. Delaware's top registered agents each serve hundreds of thousands of entities, making `cross_shell_shared_agent` fire constantly on unrelated companies. Similarly, two entities on the same Cloudflare or AWS plan produce meaningless hosting matches. Both require actively maintained whitelists/exclusion lists. An attacker who understands the discount list can exploit it by deliberately using mass-formation agents. The check produces a full audit trail: graph query results, matched edge values, the reviewer's adjudication memo, the version of the discount list applied, and append-only graph-state history so prior decisions can be reconstructed against the historical (not current) graph.

### Open questions

The form and claim checks both passed. The claim check flagged two minor missing citations: Companies House PSC DoB/address redaction policy (well-known but uncited) and the OpenCorporates cross-reference to the sibling idea. The coverage research found that the WHOIS/RDAP registrant fingerprint is effectively dead — 89% of domains now have redacted registrant data — and should be dropped from the default fingerprint set or noted as a legacy dimension. The cold-start problem (M1) is the most consequential open question: IGSC-level graph sharing across providers would dramatically reduce it, but this is a governance/antitrust/GDPR question, not a technical one. The intra-entity fingerprint-change detection gap (M3) is an implementable extension that would catch dormant-domain and acquisition-variant attacks.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.**
- **Moderate hardening finding M1 (cold-start):** The graph provides zero signal for first-time operators. A provider needs one to two years of screening history before cross-shell detection becomes effective. IGSC-level graph sharing across providers would mitigate this but raises governance, antitrust, and GDPR concerns.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2 (per-shell isolation):** A well-funded attacker can defeat the graph by using different accomplices, agents, hosting, and domains for each shell. The cost is high but not prohibitive. Behavioral/temporal fingerprints (formation date clustering, order timing patterns) are a potential extension but higher false-positive and harder to engineer.
- **Moderate hardening finding M3 (intra-entity change detection):** The graph detects cross-entity linkage but not intra-entity fingerprint changes. Adding officer-change and hosting-change tracking within a single entity's history would catch dormant-domain reanimation and entity acquisitions.
- **`[vendor-gated]` fields requiring sales conversation:**
  - Censys commercial pricing for production screening volume.
- **`[best guess]` fields needing tighter anchoring:**
  - Setup cost ($80K–$300K) — very wide range; should be decomposed into graph schema, ingestion, fuzzy matching, and backfill components.
  - Cold-start threshold (<500 customers) — no proxy cited for graph density needed for effective cross-shell detection.
  - Cloud hosting noise ("Cloudflare proxies ~20% of websites") — widely known but uncited.
- **WHOIS/RDAP registrant fingerprint effectively dead:** ~89% domain redaction rate. Should be dropped from default fingerprint set or explicitly noted as legacy.
- **Mass-formation agent discount list and cloud-hosting noise filter:** Both require active, ongoing maintenance. Neither has a specified maintenance process in the current implementation. An attacker who understands the discount rules can exploit them.
- **Fuzzy-matching tuning:** The false-positive vs false-negative tradeoff for officer-name matching is non-trivial and requires ongoing calibration. No specific approach (edit distance, phonetic, ML-based) is specified.
