## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

Cross-tenant biometric dedup performs 1:N face-template matching across all customers in a synthesis provider's identity-verification database. When a new customer enrolls (or re-verifies at a SOC-order step-up), the system extracts a face embedding from the selfie captured during the IDV session and searches the entire template database for matches above a similarity threshold. If the same face appears bound to a different identity (different name, DOB, or institution), the system flags a potential synthetic-identity factory or identity-rotation scheme. Implementation options include vendor-hosted solutions (Jumio Face Lookup, MetaMap Duplicate Detection, Didit free-tier face search, Regula Face SDK) or in-house embedding models with a vector database. The check requires explicit customer consent for biometric template storage and cross-customer comparison, which is regulated under BIPA, GDPR, and CCPA/CPRA.

### What it catches

The check is most effective against one specific scenario: a single person enrolling under multiple distinct identities at the same provider. This directly addresses the it-persona-manufacturing attacker story (one person creating two personas in the same institutional domain) and catches accomplice reuse when the same individual fronts for multiple shell nonprofits or incubator-tenant entities at the same provider. The 1:N match produces a high-confidence signal — a face similarity score above 0.95 between different identity records is strong evidence of identity rotation. The check also creates a deterrence signal: knowing that biometric dedup is in place raises the cost of multi-identity schemes at that provider.

### What it misses

The check has extensive structural blind spots. Stage 5 identified a Critical finding: the cross-vendor dedup gap. The cro-identity-rotation attacker story explicitly routes around within-tenant dedup by selecting at most one provider per IDV vendor. No commercial cross-provider biometric sharing system exists; only government systems (India's Aadhaar) achieve cross-organization dedup, through a centralized national authority inapplicable to the private-sector synthesis market. Beyond this, fresh real accomplices per entity produce genuinely different faces with no collision. Face morphing on forged documents creates templates that sit between two face clusters and may not trigger similarity thresholds. ATO-inherited IAL2 and shared-account scenarios pre-empt the check entirely because no fresh biometric is captured. Single-identity-per-person attackers — the majority of real-identity branches — produce exactly one template with no collision.

### What it costs

This is the most expensive idea in the pipeline. Vendor-hosted options (Jumio, MetaMap) cost an estimated $2–$8 per check inclusive of IDV and dedup, all vendor-gated. An in-house implementation costs $100K–$300K in setup (model integration, vector DB, bias testing, privacy review, consent UX, regulatory compliance) plus $30K–$80K/year ongoing, with a marginal query cost of $0.01–$0.10. On top of the technical costs, the privacy/consent framework requires an estimated $20K–$50K in legal review and consent-flow engineering. A bias audit (essential before deployment) costs $10K–$30K from an external testing firm.

### Operational realism

The manual review burden centers on institution-change false positives: an estimated 5–10% of returning customers per year will re-enroll with a new affiliation, producing a true biometric collision that is a false alarm for identity rotation. The review workflow must distinguish "same person, new institution" from "same person, multiple fake identities" — which requires the reviewer to verify the affiliation change with the new institution. Demographic bias is both an operational and reputational risk: NIST FRVT documents within-group false-match-rate variation of up to 7,203x across algorithms. Disproportionate flagging of certain demographic groups creates fairness concerns and potential regulatory exposure. The cold-start problem means the check provides no value until the provider has accumulated enough templates (~1,000+), which may take a small provider years.

### Open questions

The form check flagged that Jumio's actual developer API endpoint path for Face Lookup is not publicly documented (vendor-gated behind account creation). The claim check flagged Jumio's "200+ countries" and "billions of data points" as marketing language that should be qualified as vendor-reported. Similarity-score thresholds (0.95, 0.85) in the review SOP are design choices, not vendor-recommended or empirically calibrated values. The BIPA written-consent requirement is cited without a URL (common legal knowledge, but should be strengthened). The cross-provider customer overlap estimate (30–50% using 2+ providers) is speculative with no sourcing.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Critical finding C1 (surviving): Cross-vendor dedup gap.** The check's primary stated use case is catching identity rotation, but the most sophisticated rotation branch (cro-identity-rotation) explicitly defeats it by using each provider only once per IDV vendor. No commercial cross-provider biometric sharing exists. This is a structural industry-level gap that cannot be fixed at the implementation level. It would require an industry-wide shared biometric blocklist, which raises its own massive privacy and governance challenges.
- **[vendor-gated] All vendor pricing.** Jumio, MetaMap, and Didit enterprise pricing are not publicly available. The $2–$8 per check estimate is an industry range, not a quoted price.
- **[vendor-gated] Jumio Face Lookup API specification.** Developer docs are behind account creation. Actual endpoint path, request format, and response schema not publicly documented.
- **[unknown] Biometric consent opt-in rates.** No data on what fraction of customers decline consent for cross-customer biometric comparison. This directly determines whether the check can run at all in BIPA/GDPR jurisdictions.
- **[unknown] Cross-provider customer overlap.** Estimated 30–50% of customers use 2+ providers, but this is speculative. Determines the magnitude of Gap 1.
- **Similarity-score thresholds need calibration.** The 0.95/0.85 thresholds are design choices, not calibrated against a specific embedding model or test population. Must be validated before deployment, ideally with a demographic-stratified test set.
- **Demographic bias audit required.** NIST FRVT documents extreme variation in false-match rates across demographic groups. Deployment without a bias audit creates fairness and regulatory risk. Estimated cost: $10K–$30K.
- **Naming: "cross-tenant" is misleading.** The implementation establishes that no cross-provider (cross-tenant in the IDV-vendor sense) dedup exists. The check is within-tenant only. The name inherits from the spec stage and may set incorrect expectations.
- **Cold-start timeline.** The ~1,000+ template threshold is a rough estimate. For a provider processing 500 IDV sessions/year, the check provides no value for the first 2+ years.
- **Cost-benefit question.** This is the most expensive and privacy-invasive idea in the pipeline. Its marginal value is limited to same-person multi-identity enrollment at a single provider — a narrow scenario. Whether the security benefit justifies the cost ($2–$8/check + $100K+ setup + ongoing compliance) relative to simpler M14 checks requires a human judgment call.
