## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check verifies whether a customer's shipping address is physically associated with the institution they claim to represent. It queries three public registries — ROR (the global registry of research organizations, covering ~120,000 institutions), GLEIF (the Legal Entity Identifier system, covering ~2.93 million entities primarily in financial services), and the UK Companies House register (~5.45 million entities) — to obtain canonical addresses for the claimed institution. In its v2 form, the check augments these registries with OpenStreetMap campus polygons (queried via the Overpass API) and GeoNames campus-center coordinates (linked from ROR records). A resolution cascade tries each source from highest to lowest precision: GLEIF or Companies House street-level matching first, then OSM campus polygon point-in-polygon testing, then GeoNames point with a configurable radius (~500m), and finally city-level matching as a last resort. The provider geocodes the customer's shipping address and tests it against the best available reference. A mismatch at any resolution triggers a flag for human review; the absence of any registry record for the claimed institution triggers a separate flag. The resolution level is recorded in the audit log so reviewers know the evidentiary strength of each result.

### What it catches

The check catches attacker strategies that rely on providing an address not associated with the claimed institution. For the ghost-office, address-spoofing, and shell-company stories, any customer claiming a known institution but shipping to a different city or country is caught at all resolution levels. The v2 augmentation extends detection to same-city bypass methods — the dominant attacker strategy identified across the inbox-compromise, foreign-institution, visiting-researcher, dormant-domain, and account-hijack stories. When an OSM campus polygon is available (estimated at 60–80% of OECD research institutions), a CMRA, virtual office, or residential address in the same city but outside the campus boundary is now detected. The GeoNames point fallback catches addresses more than ~500m from the campus center even without a polygon.

### What it misses

Three structural gaps limit the check. First, entities with no record in any of the three registries — estimated at 10–20% of legitimate synthesis customers, including early-stage biotech startups, community biology labs, and non-UK commercial entities — produce only a binary "not found" flag that is indistinguishable from a malicious entity without registry presence. Second, non-OECD institutions with poor OSM mapping coverage (estimated at 15–25% of academic customers, concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and South Asia) fall back to city-level matching, where same-city attacker addresses pass undetected. Third, attackers operating from within the institution (inside-institution paths) or using residential addresses that happen to fall within the campus polygon pass the check by construction. Shell entities whose registered address matches their shipping address also pass by construction. Carrier-level redirect after order placement is a post-check bypass that no address-verification check can catch.

### What it costs

The marginal cost per check is less than $0.02. All three registries (ROR, GLEIF, Companies House) are free. GeoNames is free at the 20,000-credit/day tier. The Overpass API is free but a self-hosted instance is recommended for production use at ~$50–100/month. Geocoding adds ~$0.005/check via Google or is free via self-hosted Nominatim. Setup cost is estimated at 2–3 engineer-weeks, covering integration with five external APIs, the resolution cascade logic, OSM polygon caching, and fuzzy name matching between ROR and OSM.

### Operational realism

When a `canonical_address_mismatch` flag fires, the reviewer sees the resolution level that triggered it. A street-level mismatch (from GLEIF or Companies House) is the strongest signal and the simplest disposition: the reviewer checks whether the institution has known satellite offices at the shipping address. A polygon mismatch requires the reviewer to check for satellite campuses, affiliated hospitals, or research parks not captured in OSM — a more judgment-intensive task. A GeoNames-point mismatch with no available polygon is the weakest actionable signal; the reviewer must consult the institution's website for campus locations and apply wider tolerance. City-level mismatches flag only cross-city or cross-country discrepancies and carry low false-positive burden but also low discriminatory power. The dominant source of false positives is multi-campus shipping: large research universities with satellite sites generate polygon mismatches that require reviewer investigation. A campus-polygon cache with manual satellite-campus additions could reduce this burden but adds operational cost. Every check produces a full audit trail: all canonical addresses retrieved, the polygon geometry, geocoded coordinates, resolution level, match result, and all matched IDs.

### Open questions

The GeoNames premium pricing ($480/year) was not independently confirmed and may have changed. The Overpass API rate limit for sustained use is undocumented for the public instance; production deployments should budget for self-hosting. The actual overlap between ROR institution names and OSM `amenity=university` tags has not been empirically measured — the 60–80% OECD coverage estimate is a best guess. The accuracy of GeoNames point coordinates for universities (estimated at 200–500m) has no published validation study. These unknowns affect operational calibration (radius thresholds, fallback behavior) but not the fundamental design.
