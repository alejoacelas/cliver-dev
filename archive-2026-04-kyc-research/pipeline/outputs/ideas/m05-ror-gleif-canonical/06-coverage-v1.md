# Coverage research: ROR / GLEIF / Companies House canonical address cross-reference + OSM campus polygon augmentation

## Coverage gaps

### Gap 1: Entities not in any registry (no ROR, no GLEIF LEI, no Companies House record)
- **Category:** Legitimate DNA synthesis customers whose organization has no record in ROR, GLEIF, or Companies House. This includes: early-stage biotech startups (<2 years old, not yet in ROR or GLEIF), community biology labs, independent biosafety consultants ordering reagents, and small contract research organizations in non-UK jurisdictions without a national registry integration.
- **Estimated size:** ROR contains ~120,000+ organizations [source](https://ror.org/). GLEIF has ~2.93 million active LEIs globally, but these are concentrated in financial-services entities [source](https://www.gleif.org/en/newsroom/blog/the-lei-in-numbers-global-transparency-and-digitalization-push-drives-lei-adoption-in-2025). Academic and government research institutes account for ~54% of gene synthesis customers [source](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/us-gene-synthesis-market-report). The commercial remainder (~46%) includes pharma, CROs, and biotech startups. [best guess: ~10-20% of legitimate synthesis-ordering entities have no record in any of the three registries — primarily early-stage startups, community bio labs, and small commercial entities outside the UK. ROR's 120K organizations cover most academic institutions but miss commercial biotech; GLEIF covers large financial entities but <5% of research institutions; Companies House is UK-only.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** no-signal — the `institution_no_canonical_record` flag fires, but this is indistinguishable from a malicious entity with no registry presence.
- **Reasoning:** The check's entire signal depends on having a canonical record to compare against. For entities outside all three registries, the check produces only a binary "not found" flag, which triggers manual review but provides no positive or negative signal about the entity's legitimacy. The provider must rely on other ideas (m18 institution-legitimacy checks, m09 corp-registry-stack) for these entities.

### Gap 2: Non-OECD academic institutions with weak OSM polygon coverage
- **Category:** Legitimate academic researchers at universities in sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and parts of South America whose institution is in ROR but has no OpenStreetMap campus polygon, forcing a fallback to GeoNames point (±500m) or city-level matching.
- **Estimated size:** ROR added 8,000+ records in 2025, with nearly 20% in East Asia [source](https://ror.org/blog/2026-03-03-ror-annual-meeting-highlights/). OSM building/feature completeness in developing regions is substantially lower than in OECD countries — studies show OSM completeness below 20% for East Asia & Pacific, 12% for Middle East & North Africa, and 9% for South Asia in urban building data [source](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-39698-6). [best guess: <30% of universities in sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and South Asia have an `amenity=university` polygon in OSM, compared to >80% in the US/EU as estimated in the implementation doc]. The gene synthesis market outside North America accounts for ~63% of revenue [source](https://www.towardshealthcare.com/insights/dna-synthesis-market-sizing). [best guess: non-OECD institutions may represent 15-25% of academic synthesis customers, and the majority of these fall back to city-level or GeoNames-point matching.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the check falls back to city-level matching, which the implementation doc itself notes fails to catch same-city attacker addresses (the dominant attack strategy identified in C1).
- **Reasoning:** The v2 OSM polygon augmentation specifically targets same-city bypass. For institutions without OSM polygons, the check regresses to v1 city-level behavior. This gap is geographically concentrated in the regions where OSM mapping activity is lowest.

### Gap 3: Multi-campus and distributed institutions
- **Category:** Legitimate customers shipping to satellite campuses, teaching hospitals, research parks, or off-campus lab buildings that are not captured by the institution's primary OSM polygon or GeoNames point.
- **Estimated size:** [best guess: among large OECD research universities, ~40-60% operate multiple campuses or have research facilities >1km from the main campus. Major research universities (e.g., UC system, University of London, Max Planck institutes) routinely have 3-10+ sites. The fraction of synthesis orders shipped to non-primary-campus addresses is unknown but plausible at 15-30% for large multi-site institutions.] [unknown — searched for: "fraction of university research conducted at satellite campuses", "multi-campus university research facility percentage off-main-campus"]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-positive — the shipping address is legitimate but falls outside the OSM polygon or GeoNames-point radius for the primary campus, triggering `canonical_address_mismatch` with `resolution_level: polygon` or `resolution_level: point`.
- **Reasoning:** OSM multipolygon relations help for campuses mapped as separate polygons linked to the same institution, but many satellite sites are mapped as separate entities or not mapped at all. The implementation acknowledges this but does not quantify the false-positive burden.

### Gap 4: Commercial biotech entities with GLEIF records but shipping to lab facilities (not HQ)
- **Category:** Legitimate commercial customers (pharma companies, CROs) that have a GLEIF LEI record with a headquartersAddress or legalAddress corresponding to their corporate HQ, but whose synthesis orders ship to a lab facility at a different physical address.
- **Estimated size:** [best guess: among the ~30-50% of large commercial biotech/pharma entities that have LEIs, perhaps 50-70% ship synthesis orders to lab facilities that differ from the GLEIF-registered HQ address. This is structurally similar to Gap 3 but affects the GLEIF street-level layer rather than the OSM polygon layer.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-positive — the GLEIF street-level match (the highest-resolution layer) fires a mismatch because the shipping address is the lab, not the HQ.
- **Reasoning:** GLEIF records contain legal and headquarters addresses, not operational lab addresses. A pharma company headquartered in Basel but ordering synthesis to their Boston R&D facility would trigger a street-level mismatch. The cascade design means the check would fall back to lower-resolution layers (OSM polygon for the Boston facility, if mapped), but the GLEIF mismatch would still be logged and could generate review burden.

### Gap 5: Customers in countries with no national company registry integration (non-UK)
- **Category:** Legitimate commercial entities outside the UK that are not in ROR (because they are commercial, not research institutions) and not in GLEIF (because they have no LEI). These entities have records in their national company registries (DE Handelsregister, FR Infogreffe, SG ACRA, etc.) but the implementation only integrates Companies House (UK).
- **Estimated size:** The UK represents ~5-8% of global gene synthesis market revenue [best guess: derived from the ~37% North America share and Europe's ~25% share, with UK as a subset of Europe]. The remaining non-US, non-UK commercial entities — a substantial fraction of the ~46% commercial customer base — have no street-level registry source in this implementation. [best guess: 20-30% of all synthesis-ordering entities are non-UK commercial entities without ROR or GLEIF records, falling entirely through to city-level matching or no-signal.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** no-signal or weak-signal — falls through the cascade to city-level only, or to `institution_no_canonical_record` if not in ROR.
- **Reasoning:** The implementation doc acknowledges this ("Other national company registries could extend this but require per-country integrations — addressed by m09-corp-registry-stack") but does not quantify the coverage loss from UK-only registry integration.

### Gap 6: Residential addresses near campus (within OSM polygon or GeoNames radius)
- **Category:** Attackers or legitimate customers using residential addresses that happen to fall within the OSM campus polygon or within 500m of the GeoNames point. University-adjacent housing, student housing within campus boundaries, and off-campus apartments in dense urban areas near small campuses.
- **Estimated size:** [best guess: in dense urban university settings (e.g., NYU in Manhattan, UCL in London, university districts in Tokyo), the campus polygon or 500m radius may encompass hundreds of residential addresses. For suburban campuses with large polygons, student housing and nearby residential areas are commonly within the boundary. The fraction of synthesis orders shipped to such addresses is likely small but non-zero — perhaps 1-5% of orders from customers at urban universities.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-negative (for attackers) / no issue (for legitimate customers) — the address passes the polygon/point check despite not being an institutional facility.
- **Reasoning:** This is the residual bypass path even after the v2 OSM augmentation. An attacker who obtains a residential address within the campus polygon or GeoNames radius passes the check. The implementation does not address this because the polygon is meant to approximate the institutional footprint, not exclude residential addresses within it.

## Refined false-positive qualitative

1. **Multi-campus shipping** (Gap 3): Legitimate orders shipped to satellite campuses, hospitals, or research parks trigger `canonical_address_mismatch` at polygon or point resolution. This is the dominant false-positive source for large research universities. The reviewer must cross-reference the institution's website for satellite locations.
2. **Lab-vs-HQ mismatch for commercial entities** (Gap 4): GLEIF street-level mismatch when a pharma/CRO ships to a lab facility, not the registered HQ. Cascade fallback to lower-resolution layers mitigates but does not eliminate reviewer burden.
3. **OSM polygon boundary errors** (from implementation doc): ~5-10% of OSM university polygons have boundary errors that could cause legitimate addresses at the campus edge to fall outside.
4. **Institutions without OSM polygons in OECD countries** (subset of Gap 2): Even in OECD countries, ~20% of institutions may lack OSM polygons, forcing GeoNames-point fallback. Addresses >500m from the point but on a legitimate campus extension trigger false positives.
5. **Non-OECD institutions falling back to city-level** (Gap 2): City-level matching produces no false positives (it only flags cross-city mismatches) but provides no discrimination for same-city attacker addresses — this is a weak-signal gap, not a false-positive gap.

## Notes for stage 7 synthesis

- The v2 OSM polygon augmentation is a genuine improvement over v1 for OECD institutions, converting city-level matching to campus-level for an estimated 60-80% of OECD research institutions. But the improvement is structurally limited to regions with good OSM coverage.
- The largest coverage gaps are for non-registry entities (Gap 1, ~10-20% of customers) and non-OECD institutions without OSM polygons (Gap 2, ~15-25% of academic customers). These gaps overlap with regions where the gene synthesis market is growing fastest (East Asia, South Asia).
- The false-positive burden from multi-campus institutions (Gap 3) could be substantial for providers serving large research universities. A campus-polygon cache with manual satellite-campus additions would mitigate this but adds operational cost.
- This idea pairs naturally with m09-corp-registry-stack (fills Gap 5 for non-UK commercial entities) and m05-google-places-campus (alternative campus-boundary source that may fill OSM gaps).
- Gap 6 (within-polygon residential addresses) is a structural limitation that no polygon-based check can fully address; it requires complementary checks (m05-incubator-tenant, m04-usps-rdi) to distinguish institutional from residential addresses within the polygon.
