## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The NSF + UKRI + CORDIS check extends the funded-institution signal from the m18-nih-reporter idea to three additional major Western public research funders. It queries the NSF Award Search Web API for US-funded awards, the UKRI Gateway to Research API for UK Research Council grants, and a locally indexed bulk dump of the EU CORDIS database for Horizon 2020 and Horizon Europe projects (including ERC grants). All three sources are free and require no authentication. The NSF API returns awards from 2007 onward searchable by awardee name. The UKRI API provides organisation-to-project linkages. CORDIS has no live search endpoint, so the implementation builds a local index from the bulk CSV/XML dump. Together with NIH RePORTER, the four-funder stack covers the largest public research funders in the US, UK, and EU. The check fires flags when no funder has any record of the institution in the past five years, when a jurisdiction mismatch is detected (institution claims one country but appears in a different country's funder), and when the customer's named individual does not appear among funded PIs.

### What it catches

Like the NIH RePORTER check, this check's value lies in the quality of its positive signal: a publicly funded research institution has been vetted by a national funding agency. The check correctly flags shell entities, CRO facades, community labs, and gradually-built entities as lacking public funding. Its key incremental value over NIH RePORTER is geographic: UKRI covers ~10,920 UK organisations (including 504 research/academic institutions and 9,121 SMEs in 2024–25), and CORDIS covers approximately 4,641 participating organisations across 162 countries. This means a UK university or a German research institute that has no NIH record but does have UKRI or Horizon Europe funding is correctly identified as legitimate. The `funder_jurisdiction_mismatch` flag adds a cross-checking dimension: an institution claiming to be in the UK that appears only in NSF records but not UKRI raises a soft anomaly.

### What it misses

The combined four-funder stack (NIH + NSF + UKRI + CORDIS) covers roughly 15,000–18,000 distinct institutions (before deduplication for overlap). This is substantial but far from comprehensive. The largest remaining gap is non-US/UK/EU institutions: Asia-Pacific holds approximately 38% of the gene synthesis market, and major national funders (JSPS in Japan, NSFC in China, NRF in South Korea, CNPq in Brazil) are absent from the stack. An estimated 30–40% of global synthesis-buying research institutions are in countries with no coverage. Commercial entities (70–80% of commercial customers) and teaching-only institutions (~2,000 US colleges without NSF awards) are also outside coverage. The dormant-domain bypass carries over from NIH RePORTER: a defunct institution's historical grants persist across all three databases, allowing an attacker who acquires the institution's identity to inherit the funded-institution signal.

### What it costs

All three sources are free, making the marginal cost per check $0 with approximately 3–5 seconds of wall time for sequential queries. Setup cost is roughly one engineer-day for the three API clients, CORDIS dump ingestion, and cross-funder name normalization. This makes it extremely cheap to add as a complement to the NIH RePORTER check. The main engineering investment is in name normalization across three different funder naming conventions: NSF uses US-centric names, UKRI uses UK names, and CORDIS uses legal names in the original language (often non-English).

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff mirrors the NIH RePORTER playbook but adds a jurisdiction-fit step: the reviewer first determines which funders the institution should appear in based on its claimed country, then interprets the results accordingly. A US institution absent from both NIH and NSF over five years is a stronger negative than a UK institution absent from NSF (which is expected). The four-step playbook (confirm expected funder profile, re-run with aliases, escalate if no funder record across all sources, document) is clear. The CORDIS participants field presents an operational challenge: it is a semicolon-delimited string of organisation names, not a normalized entity list, so substring matching can produce false positives when institution names share common tokens ("Institute of Technology"). The implementation mitigates this with exact + alias lookup rather than substring, but the residual risk remains. The check produces a detailed audit record per funder: hit counts, sample award IDs, total funding, and the reviewer's disposition.

### Open questions

The claim check flagged two minor issues: the UKRI API endpoint path may need verification against the GtR-2 PDF, and the NSF rate limit `[unknown]` could be upgraded to a best guess of 1 req/s based on community patterns. The hardening analysis found three Moderate issues: defunct-institution funding inheritance (M1, same mitigation as NIH RePORTER — check whether all grants are expired), CORDIS substring matching false positives (M2, partially addressed by exact + alias lookup), and non-US/UK/EU coverage gap (M3, structural limitation with no implementation fix — adding JSPS/NSFC/NRF would require new integrations). The coverage research noted that the combined four-funder institution count (~15,000–18,000) likely overstates distinct coverage due to deduplication overlap (many institutions receive funding from multiple sources). A shared name-normalization layer backed by ROR aliases would reduce false negatives across all four funder sources.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.**
- **Moderate hardening finding M1:** Dormant-domain branch inherits defunct institution's funding history across NSF, UKRI, and CORDIS. Same mitigation as NIH RePORTER: check whether most recent grants are all expired (end date >2 years ago, no active status) and add `funder_record_all_expired` sub-flag.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2:** CORDIS participants substring matching can produce false matches across institutions sharing name tokens. Mitigated by exact + alias lookup but residual risk remains for institutions with common-token names.
- **Moderate hardening finding M3:** Non-US/UK/EU coverage gap is structural. No open funder database covers Asia-Pacific, Latin America, or Africa at scale. Adding JSPS, NSFC, NRF would require new integrations with potentially non-English, non-standardized APIs.
- **Claim check REVISE (minor):** UKRI API endpoint path should be verified against GtR-2 PDF before v2. NSF rate limit `[unknown]` can be upgraded to `[best guess: 1 req/s]`.
- **`[unknown]` fields affecting policy implications:**
  - NSF API rate limit — not published; community guidance suggests 1 req/s.
  - UKRI API rate limit — not published.
  - CORDIS update cadence — `[unknown]`; community sources suggest monthly to quarterly.
- **`[best guess]` fields needing tighter anchoring:**
  - Non-US/UK/EU institution coverage gap (30–40%) — needs synthesis customer geography data.
  - Commercial entities without funder records (70–80%) — needs market segment breakdown.
  - Name normalization failure rate (10–20%) — no derivation cited.
  - Combined distinct institution count (~15,000–18,000) — overstates due to deduplication overlap.
  - US customers funded only by non-stack agencies (5–10%) — consider citing Federal R&D budget by agency.
- **Cross-funder name normalization:** The operational bottleneck. A shared normalization layer seeded from ROR aliases would benefit all four funder sources (NIH + NSF + UKRI + CORDIS).
- **Complement to m18-nih-reporter:** This idea is explicitly designed as a geographic extension. The two ideas should be evaluated together in stage 8 per-measure synthesis.
