## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check runs every customer order through one or more sequence-screening services — Aclid (commercial), Battelle UltraSEQ (commercial), and SecureDNA (free, from the Swiss-based SecureDNA Foundation) — then reconciles the screening results with the customer's SOC self-declaration. Each vendor analyzes the submitted sequences against its own curated threat database and returns a per-sequence verdict including whether an SOC was detected, the inferred organism and threat class, a homology or confidence score, and the matching region. A reconciliation engine normalizes these outputs and compares them against the customer's declared intended use, host organism, and SOC status. When the screening vendors identify an SOC that the customer did not declare, the `soc_screening_disagreement` flag routes the order to a scientific reviewer. When vendors disagree with each other, `vendor_disagreement` flags the order for expert adjudication.

### What it catches

The reconciliation is strongest against misdeclaration and non-declaration — the most common and lowest-effort M15 evasion methods. When a lab manager orders a select-agent gene under a vague "molecular biology research" declaration, or when a compromised inbox is used to claim exemption from SOC disclosure, the screening vendors identify the specific SOC while the declaration provides no matching information. The disagreement is immediate and unambiguous. Per-order-line reconciliation also cuts through bulk-order noise: even when SOC orders are hidden among hundreds of legitimate orders under a facility-wide generic declaration, each individual order line is independently screened and compared. Multi-vendor reconciliation provides an additional layer: if one vendor's database has a gap, another vendor may catch what the first missed, and the `vendor_disagreement` flag surfaces these coverage differences for review.

### What it misses

The reconciliation check is structurally blind to honest-declaration-with-fabricated-justification — when an attacker correctly declares the SOC and provides a plausible cover story (e.g., "diagnostic assay development for [pathogen]"), the declaration and screening output agree, and the reconciler finds nothing wrong. This affects eight attacker stories spanning the purpose-built, insider, and account-hijack branches. Novel sequences not in any vendor database (de novo designed pathogens, codon-optimized variants) produce no vendor flag and no reconciliation signal — a fundamental limitation of homology-based screening. Short sequences below vendor minimum screening windows (~200 bp for some Battelle parameter sets) have reduced sensitivity, enabling a fragment-splitting evasion strategy; the estimate that 20–40% of order lines may fall below 200 bp lacks a citation and is flagged as under-sourced.

### What it costs

SecureDNA is free. Aclid and Battelle pricing are vendor-gated, with a best-guess range of $0.10–$2 per sequence at enterprise tier, putting the all-three-vendors cost at roughly $0.20–$4 per order line. Setup cost is 4–8 engineer-weeks to integrate three vendor APIs, build the reconciliation engine, and train scientific reviewers. The dominant ongoing cost is reviewer time for adjudicating flagged orders. The 7–9% non-regulated SOC match rate from benign select-agent genes (per the Battelle sensitivity study) means a meaningful fraction of flagged orders are false positives, and reviewer fatigue from this noise is the primary operational risk to the check's effectiveness.

### Operational realism

The reviewer's core task is distinguishing between three cases: (a) the customer failed to declare an SOC that vendors identified — the classic misdeclaration catch, requiring customer contact; (b) vendors disagree and the reviewer must adjudicate based on the specific organism and homology threshold — requiring deep biosafety expertise; and (c) the flag is a false positive from a benign gene in a select-agent organism — requiring the reviewer to assess whether the specific gene has pathogenic function. Case (c) dominates the queue volume. The audit trail includes per-vendor JSON responses (immutable), the reconciler diff, and the reviewer's adjudication, creating a complete record of which vendors were live at order time and what was decided. The reporting pathway for confirmed-malicious orders follows the provider's incident-response policy; the implementation references HHS Screening Framework Guidance but this citation needs legal review.

### Open questions

The vendor-disagreement adjudication rule is unspecified — the implementation does not define whether an any-flag-wins or majority-flag-wins policy applies when vendors disagree. The minimum order-line length for effective screening is not enforced, leaving the fragment-splitting attack partially unaddressed. The SecureDNA API endpoint URL was not found despite searching (client code is on GitHub but the deployment endpoint is undocumented). Aclid and Battelle API specifications, pricing, and auth models are all vendor-gated and would require sales conversations to resolve. The FBI WMD Coordinator reporting reference needs a direct citation or should be softened to "per the provider's incident-response policy."
