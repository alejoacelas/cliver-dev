## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The NIH RePORTER check queries the National Institutes of Health's public research project database for grants awarded to the customer's claimed institution in the past five fiscal years. The API is free, requires no authentication, and returns detailed project records including award amounts, principal investigators, project dates, and organization details. The system searches by organization name using an internal alias table (seeded from ROR aliases) to handle common name variations, and counts active and recent awards. A non-zero, multi-year award history is strong positive evidence: an NIH-funded institution has passed rigorous federal review of its research environment, fiscal management, and institutional capacity. Zero awards produces a soft negative flag that the reviewer interprets in context — many legitimate institutions (foreign, industrial, non-medical) have no NIH grants.

### What it catches

The check's strength is the quality of its positive signal: NIH funding cannot be faked. An attacker cannot game this check without literally obtaining a federal research grant, which requires genuine research infrastructure, federal compliance (OMB Uniform Guidance, institutional assurances), and multi-year review. This makes NIH funding the hardest-to-forge institutional legitimacy signal in the m18 stack. The check correctly flags shell nonprofits, shell companies, CRO facades, community labs, and gradually-built entities as unfunded during their buildup period. The five-year lookback window means the check remains effective throughout the typical gradual-legitimacy-accumulation timeline (12–24 months). The `nih_pi_count_anomaly` flag (institution with only one PI ever) provides a "tiny org" signal that combines with shell-organization indicators from other checks.

### What it misses

The check has one genuine bypass: the dormant-domain branch. A defunct institution that had NIH grants in the last five years still appears funded in RePORTER. An attacker who acquires the defunct institution's domain and claims its identity inherits the legitimacy signal — the check produces a false validation. The implementation does not cross-check whether the institution is currently operational (e.g., whether grants have been terminated, whether the DUNS/UEI is still active in SAM.gov). The name-collision edge case is a second concern: if the fuzzy-matching fallback is too loose, a shell with a deliberately adjacent name might inherit a real institution's grants in the search results. Beyond these, the check provides no discriminating signal for foreign institutions (>95% have no NIH record), commercial entities (80–90% have no NIH record), or any entity type that the reviewer would categorize as "expected negative." The check covers approximately 2,500 institutions out of the tens of thousands that buy gene synthesis.

### What it costs

The NIH RePORTER API is free with no authentication, making the marginal cost per check $0. The rate limit (1 request per second, large jobs restricted to off-hours) is the only constraint. Setup cost is approximately half an engineer-day for the API client and name-normalization table. This makes the NIH RePORTER check one of the cheapest and fastest to implement in the m18 stack — high signal quality at near-zero cost, albeit with narrow coverage.

### Operational realism

The manual review playbook is well-structured: the reviewer first confirms the name search was not defeated by formatting (trying aliases), then categorizes the institution by NIH eligibility (US biomedical academic vs foreign vs industrial vs non-medical). A US biomedical academic institution with no NIH funding in five years is a substantive flag warranting escalation. A foreign company or industrial CRO with no NIH funding is an expected result that the reviewer documents and moves on. This tiered approach prevents the check from generating excessive noise. The check produces a comprehensive audit record: search terms tried, alias list used, result counts and sample projects, final flag status, and the reviewer's disposition. The positive signal (funded institution) is self-documenting — the grant records themselves are the evidence.

### Open questions

The form and claim checks both passed. The claim check suggested upgrading the ToS `[unknown]` to note that NIH RePORTER data is US government public domain with no use restrictions. The hardening analysis found two Moderate issues: the dormant-domain inheritance bypass (M1, addressable by cross-referencing project_end_date and SAM.gov entity status) and name-collision fuzzy-match bleed (M2, addressable by tightening the fuzzy fallback to suggest alternatives for human review rather than auto-matching). The coverage research confirmed the positive-evidence asymmetry: the check covers ~2,500 institutions but the signal quality for those institutions is extremely high. The foreign-institution gap (>95% with no NIH record) is the largest by population and is partially addressed by the sibling m18-nsf-awards idea.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.**
- **Moderate hardening finding M1:** Dormant-domain branch inherits defunct institution's NIH funding history, producing false validation. Mitigation: cross-reference `project_end_date` of most recent grant — if all grants ended >2 years ago with none active, add `nih_funding_all_expired` sub-flag. Also consider checking SAM.gov entity status for the organization's DUNS/UEI. This is a parameter/logic refinement.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2:** Name-collision fuzzy matching could produce false validation by bleeding a real institution's grants into the shell's search results. Mitigation: tighten fuzzy fallback to exact/alias-table matches only; use fuzzy path only to suggest alternatives for human review, not to auto-match.
- **`[unknown]` field:**
  - NIH RePORTER commercial use terms — claim check suggests this can be upgraded to "no use restriction; US government public domain" per NIH Data Sharing FAQ.
- **`[best guess]` fields needing tighter anchoring:**
  - Foreign institutions as prime recipients in RePORTER ("fewer than 500") — NIH Data Book at report.nih.gov/nihdatabook likely has exact count.
  - New US biomedical institutions in gap (100–300) — loosely anchored; NSF NCSES or BLS data could sharpen.
  - Name normalization failure rate (5–15%) — no proxy cited.
- **Soft-flag design limitation:** The `no_nih_funding_5yr` flag is correctly soft, but this means it adds minimal friction for shell entities operating under cover types (CRO, startup, community lab) that reviewers expect to be NIH-unfunded. The check's value is concentrated in the positive signal (presence of grants confirms legitimacy), not the negative signal.
- **Foreign-institution coverage gap:** The check provides no value for ~50%+ of synthesis customers at non-US institutions. The m18-nsf-awards idea (NSF + UKRI + CORDIS) partially closes this gap.
