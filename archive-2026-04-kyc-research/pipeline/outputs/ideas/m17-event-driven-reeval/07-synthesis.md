## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

Event-driven re-evaluation implements the perpetual-KYC (pKYC) pattern for synthesis providers' pre-approval rosters. Rather than granting permanent pre-approved status after initial screening, the provider subscribes to four event feeds: corporate-ownership change events from the OpenCorporates API (covering 235+ million companies in 140+ jurisdictions), daily delta files from OFAC's Sanctions List Service, breach-credential hits from SpyCloud/Constella, and an internal dormancy timer. A scheduled daily job matches incoming events against the entity record store. When a match fires, the entity is demoted from "pre-approved" to "needs-review" and new SOC orders are frozen until a reviewer adjudicates — re-promoting, conditionally approving with a fresh legitimacy check, or revoking pre-approval.

### What it catches

The check's primary independent value is catching the acquisition variant of the shell-company attacker story: an attacker who purchases an existing company with a live provider account creates corporate filing events (officer/director changes, registered-agent changes) that OpenCorporates surfaces and the event router matches to the pre-approved entity. The pre-approval is demoted exactly when the new owner appears. The same mechanism catches the biotech-incubator-tenant shortcut of buying an aged dormant biotech LLC. The OFAC delta feed catches entities or officers newly added to the sanctions list. The breach-event and dormancy-timer feeds provide orchestration value by routing m16-spycloud-breach and m16-dormancy-reidv signals through the same demotion workflow — though the detection logic is derivative of those sibling ideas, not independent.

### What it misses

Stage 5 identified a Critical structural gap: the build-from-scratch purpose-built-organization attack — the canonical attack against pre-approval lists — generates no events on any of the four subscribed feeds. The gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, build-from-scratch shell-company, biotech-incubator-tenant primary path, and bulk-order-noise-cover branches all operate by constructing stable entities that file normally, age normally, and produce no corporate-event changes. Event-driven re-evaluation fundamentally cannot detect an entity that generates no events. This gap is not addressable by tuning the implementation — it requires a different kind of check (periodic calendar-based re-evaluation that re-runs full legitimacy checks). Additionally, stage 6 found that 30–50% of pre-approved entities may be academic institutions or government labs outside OpenCorporates entirely, and that jurisdiction data-freshness lag (1–12 months in some US states) creates an exploitation window for the acquisition variant.

### What it costs

The OFAC delta feed is free. OpenCorporates commercial pricing is vendor-gated; best estimates place per-entity per-year amortized cost at $0.10–$1.00 for ongoing monitoring, with total annual contracts in the low five figures for ~100K entity records. SpyCloud/Constella costs are per m16-spycloud-breach. Internal engineering for the event router is estimated at 4–8 engineer-weeks for initial setup. The dominant ongoing cost is reviewer time on triggered cases, which is heavily influenced by false-positive noise: OFAC name collisions (estimated 10–50 false demotions/year for a 500-entity roster) and routine officer turnover at large institutions (potentially hundreds of benign demotions/year for an R1-university-heavy roster) may make the system unsustainable without careful threshold tuning.

### Operational realism

The reviewer decision matrix has three outcomes: re-promote (the event was benign), conditionally approve (require a fresh m18-institution-legitimacy check before the next order), or reject (revoke pre-approval, notify customer, retain audit trail). For OFAC matches, the immediate-hold path escalates to legal/compliance. The audit trail includes the raw event payload, the demotion/promotion decision with reviewer name and rationale, and cross-links to any orders that were frozen. A monthly full reconciliation against entity records catches silent event-feed gaps. Customer communication is standardized: "Your account is paused pending re-verification; most reviews complete in 2 business days."

### Open questions

The Critical finding — that patient entity construction generates no events — cannot be resolved within this idea. It requires a companion measure: periodic calendar-based re-verification (e.g., annual re-run of full legitimacy checks) for all pre-approved entities regardless of events. The size-threshold exemption proposed for routine officer-turnover noise (exempt entities with >N officers) needs concrete definition before deployment, or large-institution demotions will overwhelm reviewers. The OpenCorporates jurisdiction latency for US states is not quantified — some states may lag 6–12 months, leaving the acquisition variant's exploitation window open for a significant period. Whether academic institutions (non-profit corporations) actually appear in OpenCorporates, or are structurally invisible, needs empirical validation against the provider's actual customer roster.

## Section 3: Open Issues for Human Review

- **Critical finding (stage 5):** The build-from-scratch purpose-built-organization attack generates no events on any of this idea's four subscribed feeds. This is the canonical attack against pre-approval lists, used by 3 of 7 mapped attacker stories (gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, build-from-scratch shell company, biotech-incubator-tenant primary path). The gap is structural: event-driven re-evaluation cannot catch an entity that operates stably. Requires a companion periodic re-evaluation measure.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** OpenCorporates jurisdiction data-freshness lag creates an exploitation window for the acquisition variant. The attacker can acquire a company in a slow-reporting US state, use the pre-approved account for SOC orders, and complete before the ownership-change event surfaces. Recommendation: weekly (not monthly) reconciliation for entities with SOC-active accounts.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** OFAC fuzzy-match noise may desensitize reviewers. Tune the matching threshold specifically for the pre-approved entity population.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** The breach-event and dormancy-timer feeds are architecturally overlapping with m16-spycloud-breach and m16-dormancy-reidv. Stage 7 clarification: these are orchestration/wiring contributions, not independent detection mechanisms. Do not double-count coverage.
