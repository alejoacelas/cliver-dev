## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This idea addresses the time-decay problem in phone verification: a phone number verified at account creation may be ported, swapped, or abandoned months later. The system re-verifies the customer's phone-on-file by sending an SMS one-time code on a regular cadence (every 6–12 months) and on every high-risk event (account recovery, MFA reset, billing change, first sequences-of-concern order). In parallel with the SMS, it queries Telesign's PhoneID endpoint for the sim_swap attribute, which returns a risk indicator on a 1–4 scale reflecting how recently the SIM was changed and whether the change pattern looks fraudulent. If the risk indicator is 3 or higher (recent suspicious swap), the account is frozen and the reviewer routes to the callback SOP for manual confirmation with the customer's institution.

### What it catches

The check is purpose-built for SIM-swap account takeover. When an attacker SIM-swaps a target PI's phone number to receive MFA codes, the Telesign risk_indicator flags the swap, the account freezes, and the attacker is blocked before completing the order. This also works for credential-compromise scenarios where the attacker SIM-swaps to gain control. When a credential-compromise attacker instead enrolls a fresh VoIP number, the phone-change event triggers re-verification on the old number, which the attacker cannot complete. The cadence mechanism also creates friction for inbox-compromise attackers who enter the real PI's mobile on the profile — the PI receives an unexpected verification code, and the attacker cannot receive it.

### What it misses

The check is narrowly scoped to the customer-profile phone and to SIM-swap patterns. It does not cover VoIP numbers placed on supporting documents (the inbox-compromise attacker's fabricated callback number) — that scenario requires the callback SOP. It does not detect non-SIM-swap account takeover (session theft, AitM token replay) unless those attacks happen to coincide with a cadence check or trigger a high-risk event. Fresh prepaid burner SIMs (used by shell nonprofits and other purpose-built entities) have no swap history and return a clean risk indicator. And if an attacker substitutes their own real mobile number on the profile, the check validates it because it is a genuine, non-swapped mobile line.

### What it costs

Per cadence cycle per customer: approximately $0.12 (SIM-swap query + SMS). Annualized at a 6-month cadence: roughly $0.24 per customer per year, plus event-driven checks. Setup cost is moderate: 2–4 person-weeks of engineering to build the scheduled job infrastructure, integrate Telesign or CAMARA APIs, build the OTP flow, and implement the freeze-on-swap workflow. The operational cost burden comes from false positives: at a 6-month cadence, an estimated 10–15% of customers will have undergone legitimate SIM or carrier changes, all of which trigger the sim_swap_recent flag and require reviewer intervention to unfreeze.

### Operational realism

The system runs as an automated background process with human escalation. Most customers pass re-verification silently (SMS verified, no swap detected). The manual-review handoff occurs only when a swap is detected or SMS verification fails, routing to the callback SOP. The main operational challenge is the false-positive rate from legitimate SIM changes — device upgrades, carrier switches, and eSIM provisioning all look like swaps to carrier-side detection. With US telecom churn at 15–25% annually, a 6-month cadence will freeze a meaningful number of legitimate accounts. The reviewer-mediated path absorbs this but at cost. International coverage is uneven: Telesign covers 16 countries for SIM-swap data, leaving customers in uncovered countries with SMS-only verification (no swap-detection overlay).

### Open questions

Telesign SIM-swap pricing is sourced from a third-party blog (dropcowboy.com), not from Telesign directly. The claim check recommended reframing as vendor-gated. CAMARA/Open Gateway SIM Swap pricing is unknown. The trigger list for re-verification may have a gap: stage 5 recommended adding "new order after login from a new device/IP" to catch non-SIM-swap account takeover via session theft. The eSIM false-positive rate (Gap 4) is unsourced. Whether Telesign's risk_indicator correctly distinguishes fraudulent from legitimate swaps at scale is an empirical question that cannot be resolved without production data.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical findings.** Both Moderate findings address scope/trigger gaps with concrete suggestions (callback SOP for supporting-doc phones; new-device trigger for session theft).
- **[vendor-gated] Telesign SIM-swap pricing:** Third-party blog source; exact pricing requires Telesign sales quote.
- **[unknown] Telesign API rate limits:** Not documented publicly.
- **[unknown] CAMARA SIM Swap pricing and commercial availability:** No public pricing. Coverage expanding unevenly.
- **[unknown] eSIM adoption rate:** No source cited for the 15–25% new-activation figure. Growing rapidly with Apple's eSIM-only US iPhone models.
- **Moderate-2 trigger gap (session theft):** Stage 5 recommended adding "new order after login from new device/IP" to the high-risk event trigger list. This requires authentication-system integration that may not be trivial.
- **False-positive operational burden:** At 10–15% per cadence cycle, the volume of legitimate SIM-change freezes could be substantial for a provider with thousands of active customers. Cadence tuning (6 vs 12 months) directly affects both security value and operational cost — no guidance exists for the optimal cadence in this context.
- **Minor-1 (burner SIMs):** Fresh prepaid burner SIMs are structurally invisible. Carrier-name enrichment (flagging prepaid carriers) is a potential soft signal but would generate false positives on legitimate prepaid users.
