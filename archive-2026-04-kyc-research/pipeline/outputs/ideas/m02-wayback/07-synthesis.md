## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check queries the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine CDX server to retrieve the capture history of the customer's email domain. The CDX API returns timestamped records of every snapshot the Wayback Machine has taken of the domain, including the first-ever capture date, capture frequency, and content digests. The check computes three derived signals: the domain's first-seen date in the Wayback Machine, the longest gap between consecutive captures (a proxy for dormancy), and whether recent content differs categorically from older content (detected by feeding before-and-after snapshot text to an LLM classifier). The diagnostic pattern is a domain that was captured regularly, went silent for over a year, then reappeared with entirely different content — the fingerprint of a dormant-domain drop-catch where an attacker acquires the lapsed domain of a defunct institution and stands up a new website. This is the only M02 idea that detects content-level changes over time.

### What it catches

The check's unique contribution is catching dormant-domain reanimation — the `dormancy_gap` plus `recent_content_pivot` combination directly addresses the `dormant-domain` attacker story's primary bypass. It also catches typosquats (freshly registered lookalike domains have no Wayback history) and shell-nonprofits in their 4-12 week pre-aging window (sparse or no captures). For aged-domain purchases at auction, the check catches cases where the prior domain content was incongruent with the attacker's claimed use (e.g., a pet shop domain now claiming to be a biotech CRO). Fresh-domain variants of several purpose-built-organisation stories produce `no_wayback_history` as a soft signal, useful in combination with m02-rdap-age.

### What it misses

The check misses all 11 genuine-institutional-email attacker stories — these attackers operate within real institutional domains whose Wayback history is extensive and consistent, providing no signal. It also fails entirely against the `gradual-legitimacy-accumulation` branch, which explicitly builds 12 months of consistent biotech-themed content to defeat history-based checks. Content-congruent aged-domain purchases (where the prior domain was already in a related field) produce no pivot signal. Approximately 20-30% of biotech startup websites use SPA frameworks, degrading the content classifier to uselessness (only a JS loader is captured). An estimated 5-10% of institutional domains block the Wayback Machine via robots.txt, creating false `no_wayback_history` results for legitimate organisations. The effective coverage for the check's most distinctive capability (content-pivot detection) is probably limited to ~30-40% of all synthesis customers — those with established, traditionally-rendered websites and existing Wayback capture histories.

### What it costs

Marginal cost is approximately $0.001 per check, driven by the LLM call for content-category classification. The CDX API itself is free. Setup cost is approximately one engineer-day to build the CDX query wrapper and the classifier prompt. No vendor contract or API key is required. The ongoing cost is near-zero, though the LLM classifier adds a latent dependency on prompt maintenance and calibration.

### Operational realism

When `dormancy_gap` or `recent_content_pivot` fires, the reviewer is presented with timestamped Wayback snapshot URLs showing the domain's content before and after the gap, alongside the classifier's before/after text excerpts. This is one of the more intuitive review experiences: the reviewer can visually confirm whether the domain's content has changed character. The `no_wayback_history` flag is the weakest operationally — it fires on both fresh shell domains and fresh legitimate startups, and the SOP correctly requires combination with RDAP age before escalation. The main operational risk is the content-pivot classifier's precision: legitimate rebrands (estimated 2-5% of domains per year) will trigger the high-priority `recent_content_pivot` flag, and these are precisely the cases where the reviewer must spend the most time investigating. The audit trail consists of compressed CDX JSON, first/last seen dates, sampled snapshot URLs, and the classifier's verdict with text excerpts.

### Open questions

The Internet Archive's CDX rate limits are undocumented; practical community guidance suggests staying under 10 requests/second with exponential backoff, but no formal policy exists. The ToS permissibility of systematic KYC queries has not been formally confirmed, though per-customer-order volume is well within the spirit of research and verification use. The LLM classifier's precision and recall for content-category-change detection are unspecified — the implementation notes the ~$0.001 cost but does not benchmark performance. A false-positive-prone classifier could flood the review queue with legitimate rebrands. The SPA/JS-rendering gap (20-30% of biotech startups) is estimated without any citation; even a proxy from BuiltWith or similar would strengthen this.
