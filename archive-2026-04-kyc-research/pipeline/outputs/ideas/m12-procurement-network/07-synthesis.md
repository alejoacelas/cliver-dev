## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This idea verifies that a synthesis provider has an active supplier-registration relationship with a customer's claimed institution through one of three procurement networks: PaymentWorks (used by ~150+ US universities), JAGGAER (deployed at US public universities and state-level procurement), or SAM.gov (required for US federal entities). The check is triggered when a customer claims a US institutional affiliation and requests invoice or purchase-order billing. The provider's accounts-receivable team maintains an internal CRM recording which institutions they are registered with, on which platform, with which supplier ID. For a new order, the analyst looks up the claimed institution in the CRM. If an active registration is found, the billing relationship is confirmed. For federal customers, the SAM.gov Entity Management API (free, public, documented at open.gsa.gov) is queried to confirm the institution's UEI is in Active status. PaymentWorks and JAGGAER do not expose public supplier-status APIs, so the operational pattern is internal record-keeping rather than real-time API lookup.

### What it catches

The check is strongest against attackers who fabricate institutional affiliations they do not actually have. A shell company, shell nonprofit, or newly incorporated LLC claiming to be affiliated with a real university will trigger `procurement_no_supplier_registration` because the shell entity has no procurement-office relationship with the provider. Similarly, an attacker who reanimates a defunct entity's domain (dormant-domain) will fail the check because the defunct entity has no active procurement relationship. The check provides a hard-to-forge positive signal: active supplier registration requires the institution's procurement office to approve the relationship, which an attacker cannot fabricate without compromising the procurement office itself.

### What it misses

The check has two structural blind spots. First, it validates the billing *path* (provider-to-institution relationship) but cannot distinguish authorized from unauthorized *users* of that path. Account-takeover, account-hijack, and credential-compromise attackers inherit a fully legitimate institutional billing relationship; the check confirms it. Second, the check's trigger condition is scoped to customers who claim institutional affiliation with invoice/PO billing. Entities that present as themselves — shell LLCs, CROs, biotech incubator tenants, community bio-lab networks — never claim to be another institution, so the check does not fire. Stage 5 classified both as Moderate findings (structural scope limitations, not implementation defects). The callback fallback in the SOP has a Minor weakness: for small entities with no recognizable procurement office, the only publicly available contact may be attacker-controlled.

### What it costs

SAM.gov queries are free but rate-limited to 10/day on the public tier (higher tiers require authorized roles). PaymentWorks and JAGGAER lookups are effectively free because they are internal CRM checks — marginal cost is ~$0.50–$3 in analyst time. The major cost is setup: the provider must register as a supplier with each institution, a process that takes ~15–30 minutes per institution with approval timelines of days to weeks, and requires annual or biennial renewal. Across hundreds of R1/R2 universities, this is a substantial one-time onboarding investment plus ongoing maintenance.

### Operational realism

In practice, the check is performed by the provider's accounts-receivable or customer-onboarding analyst during order intake. The analyst consults the internal CRM, confirms or denies supplier registration, and records the result. When registration is absent, the SOP escalates to a procurement-office callback — the analyst independently looks up the institution's AP/procurement contact (not using customer-supplied information) and calls to confirm order authorization. If the callback fails or the institution cannot be reached, the order is held and the customer is offered a card-payment path (which routes to BIN/AVS checks). Every invocation produces an auditable record including the procurement system checked, supplier ID, registration status, dates, and any callback log.

### Open questions

The claim check flagged two weakly-supported figures: the "~150+ universities" count for PaymentWorks adoption and the "~20 minutes" registration time estimate. Neither is structurally wrong but both should be softened in production use. The coverage research noted that revenue-weighted market-share figures (used to estimate gap sizes) may diverge from order-count shares, since non-US orders tend to be smaller. The effective addressable scope of this check — US institutional customers using invoice/PO billing through PaymentWorks/Jaggaer-enrolled institutions — may be as narrow as 15–25% of all synthesis customers by count.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical findings.** Both Moderate findings from stage 5 are structural scope boundaries (billing-path vs. user-authorization; trigger condition vs. self-presenting entities), acknowledged by design and addressed by complementary controls.
- **[vendor-gated] PaymentWorks API access:** No public developer portal or supplier-status lookup API. Would require sales contact to determine whether any partner/API integration is possible. Currently the operational pattern is internal CRM — functional but not automatable beyond the provider's own records.
- **[vendor-gated] JAGGAER API access:** Same situation. JAGGAER advertises enterprise buyer APIs but not supplier-side status-check APIs.
- **[unknown] Fraction of academic orders paid personally:** Stage 6 searched for proxies and found none. Estimated 5–15% by best guess. This affects false-positive rate estimation.
- **SAM.gov rate limit viability:** The 10 req/day public-tier limit is very low for production use. The claim check marked this as STALE-RISK — the limit may change, and the provider may need an upgraded role for operational volume.
- **Callback SOP weakness for small entities:** Stage 5 Minor-1 noted that for entities without a recognizable independent procurement office, the callback step is inherently unreliable. The SOP should specify that such cases escalate directly to card-payment requirement rather than attempting a callback.
- **Revenue vs. order-count gap:** Stage 6F flagged that coverage-gap sizing uses revenue-share proxies but the operationally relevant metric is order count. The two may diverge, particularly for non-US orders.
