## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

Stripe Identity is a hosted identity verification product that combines government-issued document scanning with selfie biometric matching and passive liveness detection. The customer uploads a photo of their ID (passport, driver's license, or national ID card) and takes a selfie; Stripe's ML models verify the document's authenticity, match the selfie to the document photo, and assess whether the selfie is a live capture rather than a photo-of-a-photo or injection. The result is a structured `VerificationSession` object containing the verified identity (name, date of birth, address, document details) along with sub-check statuses and, as of v2, an Insights system providing ordinal risk levels (`low`/`elevated`/`high`) beyond the binary pass/fail. The provider integrates Stripe Identity via its existing Stripe account, rendering a hosted verification UI to the customer and receiving webhook notifications on completion. At $1.50 per verification, it is the lowest-cost hosted IDV option among the major vendors.

### What it catches

Stripe Identity catches document fraud (forged, altered, or stolen government IDs), synthetic identity presentation (AI-generated face that does not match a real document), and basic presentation attacks (photo-of-a-photo, printed masks) via its passive liveness check. The post-verification name/DOB comparison against the account-holder record catches cases where a real document belongs to someone other than the claimed orderer. For SOC orders, the recommended dual-vendor architecture adds a second liveness gate via Jumio (ISO 30107-3 Level 2, claims deepfake and injection-attack detection) or Onfido (Level 1 + Level 2 Motion, exposes source-integrity breakdown including digital tampering and emulator usage). This escalation path catches sophisticated injection attacks — deepfakes, camera-virtual-device injection, face morphing — that Stripe's opaque PAD may miss.

### What it misses

Three structural gaps limit the check. First, Stripe Identity's PAD/liveness assessment is opaque: no PAD confidence score, no injection-detection flag, no ISO 30107-3 conformance level is published. If a sophisticated adversary defeats Stripe's liveness with a deepfake injection, the session returns `verified` with no distinguishing signal. The dual-vendor escalation mitigates this for SOC orders but does not help non-SOC orders processed Stripe-only. Second, identity verification is orthogonal to authorization: confirming that a person is real says nothing about whether they are authorized to order sequences of concern. Every customer who passes Stripe Identity still requires a separate authorization check (m17, m20, m15) for SOC orders. Third, fronted-accomplice attacks (a real person with a real ID ordering on behalf of a malicious principal) are structurally undetectable by any IDV check — the accomplice's identity is genuine.

### What it costs

The marginal cost is $1.50 per document+selfie verification. For providers already using Stripe as their payment processor, setup cost is approximately 1–2 engineer-weeks (hosted modal, webhook handler, reviewer queue). If the dual-vendor escalation is implemented for SOC orders, the combined cost rises to $4.50–$6.50 per SOC order (Stripe pre-screen + Jumio/Onfido escalation). Jumio and Onfido pricing is vendor-gated and volume-dependent. Device-attestation integration (Apple App Attest, Google Play Integrity) adds approximately one additional engineer-week of setup but no per-check cost.

### Operational realism

When a verification fails or returns elevated Insights, the reviewer opens the Stripe Dashboard to inspect document images, selfie captures, and sub-check results. For document-tampering or selfie-mismatch flags, the standard disposition is denial unless the customer can present a higher-assurance alternative. For SOC orders, any `elevated` or `high` Insight triggers mandatory escalation to Jumio or Onfido — the order is held until the escalation vendor completes its liveness-certified verification. The per-SOC-order re-proofing mandate (no cached verifications) prevents account-takeover scenarios from inheriting a prior legitimate verification. The audit record includes the Stripe session ID, full sub-check results, Insight values, and a `pad_assurance_source` field documenting whether the final PAD assurance came from Stripe (opaque) or an ISO 30107-3 certified vendor. This gives auditors a clear view of the assurance level applied to each order.

### Open questions

Stripe's specific Insight names and definitions are not fully enumerated in public documentation and require Dashboard access to inspect. Stripe's ISO 30107-3 conformance level (if any) is unpublished despite marketing claims of iBeta testing. The demographic performance of Stripe's selfie matching (false-rejection rates by skin tone, age, gender) is unknown — NIST FRVT shows industry-wide disparities but Stripe-specific data is unavailable. Stripe's image retention period under GDPR is undocumented. Identity-specific API rate limits beyond the standard Stripe ceiling are not surfaced publicly. These unknowns affect operational calibration and compliance posture but not the fundamental dual-vendor architecture.
