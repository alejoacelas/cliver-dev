# Coverage research: Stripe Identity (low-friction document + selfie IDV)

## Coverage gaps

### Gap 1: Customers in countries where Stripe Identity is not available to the provider
- **Category:** Legitimate DNA synthesis customers whose orders are processed by a provider based in a country where Stripe Identity is not available. Stripe Identity is generally available for businesses in ~47 countries (primarily US, EU, UK, AU, CA, SG, JP) and in beta for additional countries [source](https://docs.stripe.com/identity/use-cases). Providers based outside these countries cannot use Stripe Identity at all. Additionally, even providers in supported countries may face limitations verifying documents from certain non-supported document-issuing countries.
- **Estimated size:** Stripe can verify document authenticity for ID documents from over 100 countries [source](https://docs.stripe.com/identity/verification-checks), but the provider must be based in a supported country. [best guess: most major DNA synthesis providers (Twist, IDT, GenScript, Azenta) are headquartered in countries where Stripe is available (US, China — though China may not be supported for Identity). For providers in non-Stripe countries, this idea is entirely inapplicable. Among customers of providers in supported countries, document verification covers most passport-holding customers globally, but national IDs and driver's licenses have more limited country coverage.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** no-signal — the check cannot be initiated.
- **Reasoning:** This is a platform-availability gap, not a data-quality gap. It primarily affects non-Western providers or customers who can only present documents from countries with limited Stripe coverage.

### Gap 2: Customers without government-issued photo ID
- **Category:** Legitimate researchers who do not possess a government-issued photo ID document that Stripe Identity can verify. This includes: (a) individuals in countries where national ID systems are underdeveloped or not universally issued; (b) researchers whose ID documents are expired; (c) edge cases where the customer's only ID is in a format Stripe doesn't support (e.g., certain non-Latin-script national IDs).
- **Estimated size:** The World Bank ID4D dataset estimates ~850 million people globally lack official identification [source](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202302/global-estimate-for-people-without-id-reduced-to-850m-id4d-report). However, DNA synthesis customers are overwhelmingly professionals with institutional affiliations and travel documents. [best guess: <2% of legitimate synthesis customers lack a verifiable government photo ID. The gap is functionally negligible for OECD-based customers but may affect a small number of researchers in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia where over 100 million unbanked adults lack ID documentation [source](https://issuu.com/world.bank.publications/docs/9781464818974/s/17148312).]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** no-signal — the customer cannot complete the verification flow.
- **Reasoning:** DNA synthesis is a specialized professional service with a customer base that skews heavily toward credentialed professionals. The no-ID population is almost entirely outside the synthesis customer base, making this gap theoretically present but practically tiny.

### Gap 3: First-attempt verification failures due to image quality, lighting, or device limitations
- **Category:** Legitimate customers whose document scan or selfie fails on the first attempt due to poor image quality (blur, glare, bad lighting, incorrect angle) or device limitations (low-resolution camera, incompatible browser). These customers are not fraudulent but experience friction and may abandon the process.
- **Estimated size:** DHS testing found that only 5% of biometric ID proofing systems achieved a success rate of at least 90% for legitimate users [source](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202503/remote-selfie-verification-challenges-raise-questions-about-us-fraud-reduction-plans). Industry data suggests first-attempt document verification failure rates of ~5-15% for legitimate users, with most failures recoverable on retry [source](https://withpersona.com/blog/identity-verification-accuracy-rates-always-require-context). [best guess: Stripe Identity's first-attempt failure rate for legitimate users is likely 5-10%, with most recoverable via retry. The abandonment rate (users who fail and don't retry) is likely 2-5% of all verification attempts.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-positive (legitimate customer blocked or delayed by verification failure)
- **Reasoning:** Image-quality failures are the dominant source of friction in document+selfie IDV. They disproportionately affect customers using older devices, customers in low-light environments, and customers whose ID documents are worn or damaged. The implementation's manual review path handles persistent failures, but each failure adds delay to the order.

### Gap 4: Customers whose verified identity does not link to institutional authorization
- **Category:** All customers who pass Stripe Identity (real person, real document, real face) but whose verified identity provides no signal about whether they are authorized to order sequences of concern. Stripe Identity verifies *who you are* but not *what you're authorized to do*. A verified identity is necessary but not sufficient for biosecurity screening.
- **Estimated size:** 100% of customers who pass Stripe Identity. This is not a "gap" in the traditional sense but a structural limitation of the check: identity verification is orthogonal to authorization verification. [best guess: for non-SOC orders, identity verification alone may be sufficient. For SOC orders, 100% of verified customers still require a separate authorization check (m17, m20, m15) before the provider can assess biosecurity risk.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the check confirms the person is real but provides no signal about their legitimacy as a synthesis customer.
- **Reasoning:** This is the fundamental coverage boundary of any IDV check applied to biosecurity. A real person with a real passport can be a state-sponsored agent, a dual-use researcher, or a malicious actor. Stripe Identity screens out synthetic identities and document fraud but does not address the "real person, bad intent" threat model. The implementation doc acknowledges this implicitly by positioning Stripe Identity as a "lower-friction tier" within M14, not as a standalone biosecurity check.

### Gap 5: Demographic bias in selfie matching (skin tone, age, facial features)
- **Category:** Legitimate customers from demographic groups where facial recognition / selfie-matching algorithms have historically shown higher false-match or false-non-match rates — particularly darker-skinned individuals, older adults, and individuals with certain facial features or head coverings.
- **Estimated size:** [unknown — searched for: "Stripe Identity demographic bias false rejection rate skin tone", "Stripe Identity fairness audit results", "selfie verification bias dark skin"]. Stripe does not publish demographic performance breakdowns for its selfie matching. Industry-wide, NIST FRVT testing has consistently shown higher false-non-match rates for darker-skinned and female faces across many vendors [best guess: false-non-match rates may be 2-5x higher for darker-skinned individuals compared to lighter-skinned individuals, based on NIST FRVT general findings, though Stripe's specific performance is unknown].
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** false-positive — disproportionate verification failure for demographic subgroups.
- **Reasoning:** Demographic bias in facial recognition is well-documented across the industry. Without Stripe-specific fairness data, the provider cannot assess whether this gap is material for their customer base. For synthesis providers with a globally diverse researcher customer base, this is a potential equity concern.

### Gap 6: Opacity of PAD/liveness assessment for SOC orders
- **Category:** All SOC-order customers verified through Stripe Identity alone (without Jumio/Onfido escalation). The provider cannot independently assess the strength of Stripe's presentation attack detection (PAD) / liveness check. If a sophisticated adversary defeats Stripe's PAD with a deepfake injection, the session returns `verified` with no distinguishing signal.
- **Estimated size:** This affects 100% of SOC orders processed through Stripe-only verification. The implementation doc recommends escalation to Jumio/Onfido for SOC orders, but if the provider chooses Stripe-only for cost reasons, the entire SOC-order population is exposed. [best guess: if the provider implements the recommended dual-vendor path, this gap affects only the pre-screen stage and is mitigated by escalation. If not, all SOC orders — likely 5-15% of total orders based on typical SOC hit rates — are exposed to Stripe's opaque PAD.]
- **Behavior of the check on this category:** weak-signal — the check returns `verified` even if PAD is defeated, with no way for the provider to distinguish a genuine verification from a successful deepfake attack.
- **Reasoning:** The v2 implementation extensively documents this gap and recommends compensating controls (device attestation, Jumio/Onfido escalation). The gap is not in the implementation design but in the structural opacity of Stripe's liveness assessment. It is included here because the coverage research should quantify the population at risk if the recommended escalation is not implemented.

## Refined false-positive qualitative

1. **Image-quality failures** (Gap 3): The dominant false-positive source. Affects 5-10% of first attempts, with most recoverable on retry. Disproportionately affects customers with older devices or worn documents.
2. **Demographic bias in selfie matching** (Gap 5): Potentially 2-5x higher false-rejection rates for certain demographic groups. Unquantifiable for Stripe specifically due to lack of published fairness data.
3. **Document-format limitations** (subset of Gap 1): Customers whose only ID is in a format Stripe doesn't support experience a hard block, not a soft false positive.
4. **Selfie-to-document mismatch for legitimate reasons**: Customers who have changed appearance significantly since their ID photo was taken (weight change, aging, medical conditions, head coverings not present in ID photo) may fail the selfie-to-document match.

## Notes for stage 7 synthesis

- Stripe Identity's coverage for the DNA synthesis customer base is structurally high: the customer population is overwhelmingly credentialed professionals with government photo ID and modern devices. Gaps 1 and 2 (no-ID, no-Stripe-availability) are small.
- The material coverage gaps are all about what identity verification *cannot tell you*: it cannot confirm institutional authorization (Gap 4), it cannot guarantee PAD strength for SOC orders (Gap 6), and it may have unquantified demographic bias (Gap 5).
- The implementation doc's recommendation to use Stripe as a low-friction pre-screen with Jumio/Onfido escalation for SOC orders is well-calibrated to these gaps. The coverage analysis supports this architecture.
- Gap 4 (identity ≠ authorization) is the most important finding for stage 7: Stripe Identity must be paired with authorization-verification ideas (m17, m20, m15) to be meaningful for biosecurity. As a standalone check, it filters synthetic identities but does not address the core biosecurity threat model.
