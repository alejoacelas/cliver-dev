## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This is a configuration and written-policy idea, not an API integration. The provider disables SMS and voice OOB authenticators at the IdP level (Okta, Auth0, or equivalent) and configures password-reset and "lost authenticator" workflows to require a second-factor challenge — email alone never authorizes a credential change. When a customer loses their authenticator, the recovery path routes to a help-desk video call where an agent verifies the customer's identity against their stored onboarding selfie (or triggers a fresh IDV inquiry), verifies a knowledge factor not present in the email account, and walks the customer through enrolling a new hardware token. A notification is sent to the customer's institutional security contact, providing an out-of-band alert that defeats silent account takeover. SOC orders are blocked for 24 hours after any factor reset. The policy is anchored by NIST SP 800-63B-4 (classifying SMS as a restricted authenticator) and CISA's Dec 2024 Mobile Communications Best Practices guidance ("Do not use SMS as a second factor").

### What it catches

The check directly closes the cheapest and most common MFA bypass vector: email-based account recovery. When an attacker compromises a customer's institutional email (the entry point for account-hijack, credential-compromise, and dormant-account-takeover branches), they can no longer use that email to reset MFA and enroll their own factor. The $5 credential-stuffing path (infostealer log → password reset → MFA re-enrollment) is blocked at the MFA re-enrollment step, forcing the attacker to either possess the existing TOTP seed (low probability) or invest in more expensive AitM phishing ($120–$350). SIM-jacking and SS7 interception are eliminated because SMS is not accepted as a factor at all. The FIDO2 downgrade attack is partially mitigated — the SMS fallback target is removed, though TOTP relay remains viable. Social engineering of provider support is mitigated by the video-call SOP and institutional notification, though not eliminated.

### What it misses

The check addresses only the recovery workflow. Attacks that bypass recovery entirely — session hijacking via stolen cookies, possession of an exfiltrated TOTP seed, real-time AitM phishing relay, cloud passkey injection, and grace-period exploitation — are not in scope and are addressed by sibling M16 ideas (m16-auth0-okta, m16-webauthn-yubikey, m16-order-time-stepup). Federated-login attacks (IdP impersonation, IdP MFA policy relaxation) bypass the provider's recovery flow entirely, as the attacker logs in through the upstream IdP without triggering any provider-side reset event. Help-desk social engineering remains the primary residual risk: the SOP mandates video calls and IDV, but a sufficiently skilled attacker who impersonates a panicked customer can potentially social-engineer a help-desk agent into bypassing the procedure. The weekly audit-log review catches violations after the fact but provides no real-time prevention.

### What it costs

Configuration cost is zero — disabling SMS and email-only reset is a settings change in the IdP admin console. The operational cost is in downstream consequences: every "I lost my authenticator" ticket now requires a 15–30 minute video call instead of a 2-minute email link, at $5–15 per event in human time (plus $1.50–$3 if IDV is invoked). With an estimated 5–10% of the customer base needing authenticator recovery per year, a provider with 10,000 active customers would handle 500–1,000 recovery tickets annually at $2,500–$15,000/year in direct help-desk cost. Setup cost is 2–6 engineer-weeks plus PM and support-lead time for SOP writing, help-desk training, customer communication, and the in-product enrollment-replacement flow. The customer experience impact (10x slower recovery) may drive churn in price-sensitive segments.

### Operational realism

The help-desk SOP is the load-bearing operational artifact. Its six steps (triage, second-factor challenge, video call with IDV, logging, institutional notification, cooling-off) create a structured and auditable recovery process. The institutional security contact notification is the key anti-silent-takeover control — even if the attacker successfully recovers the account, the legitimate user receives an out-of-band heads-up. The `recovery_notification_bounced` flag catches cases where the notification fails. The audit trail includes IdP log entries for every factor change and policy evaluation, the help-desk ticket with video-call recording and IDV inquiry ID, and the notification record. The make-or-break operational question is help-desk adherence to the SOP under pressure — the 0ktapus campaign demonstrated that even organizations with SOPs can be compromised when help-desk agents deviate under social-engineering pressure.

### Open questions

The SOP lacks a mandatory dual-authorization requirement for factor resets that don't produce a passing IDV inquiry — adding dual-agent sign-off would close the single-agent social-engineering path. The 24-hour cooling-off period blocks SOC orders but does not block other account modifications (address, payment, contact info); an attacker could use this window to stage changes before the SOC block lifts. The institutional security contact can be suppressed if the attacker controls the IT department's distribution list (as in the dormant-account-takeover scenario); sending notifications to multiple channels (security contact, department chair, personal email) would mitigate this. The IdP audit-log event names listed are best-guesses and should be verified against the Okta System Log API event catalog. Okta/Auth0 audit log retention defaults are unknown. The help-desk social-engineering success rate estimate (2–5% per attempt) lacks a specific citation.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** One Moderate finding.
- **Moderate finding — Help-desk social engineering remains viable despite SOP.** The SOP mandates video call + IDV for all factor resets, but the detective control (weekly audit-log review) catches violations only after the fact. No preventive dual-authorization requirement exists for recovery events without a passing IDV inquiry. Recommended: dual-agent sign-off for any recovery that does not produce a `recovery_completed_with_idv` event.
- **Minor finding — Cooling-off period scope too narrow.** 24h SOC order block does not cover account modifications (address, payment, contact info). Attacker could stage changes during cooling-off. Recommended: block all account modifications during cooling-off.
- **Minor finding — Institutional security contact as single point of failure.** If the contact is controlled by the attacker (e.g., IT department distribution list in dormant-account-takeover scenario), notification is suppressed. Recommended: multi-channel notification (security contact + department chair + personal email).
