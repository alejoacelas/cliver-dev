## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check verifies that a customer's claimed institution exists as a registered legal entity by querying four registry systems: UK Companies House for UK companies, the UK Charity Commission for charities registered in England and Wales, US state Secretaries of State via the OpenCorporates aggregation layer for US companies, and the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search for US nonprofits. Companies House provides a free, well-documented REST API with a 600-requests-per-5-minutes rate limit. The Charity Commission offers a similar free API (currently in beta). For the US, direct integration with all 50 state SOS portals is impractical — no state has a good API — so OpenCorporates (or KYB aggregators like Middesk or Cobalt Intelligence) serves as the aggregation layer. IRS TEOS provides both a web lookup tool and downloadable bulk data files (Pub. 78, 990 series, Automatic Revocation List) that can be mirrored locally. The check returns legal status, incorporation date, registered address, officers/directors (where available), and for nonprofits, tax-exempt status and revocation history.

### What it catches

The check's strongest use case is detecting dissolved or defunct entities: when an attacker acquires the expired domain of a defunct research institution and attempts to order sequences under the dead entity's name, the registry shows dissolved/struck-off status, and the `registry_dissolved` flag fires. This directly addresses the dormant-domain attacker story. For newly-formed shell entities — shell nonprofits, shell companies, CRO facades — the check provides useful secondary signals: `registry_recently_incorporated` flags entities that are too new relative to the customer's claims of being an established institution, and officer/PSC patterns (single officer at a mass-formation address) can be routed to cross-shell analysis. The `teos_revoked` flag catches nonprofits that have lost tax-exempt status, a strong negative signal. The `registry_filing_overdue` flag catches shells that neglect their filing obligations with Companies House.

### What it misses

The check confirms legal existence, which is necessary but not sufficient for institutional legitimacy — and which the attacker has deliberately constructed. A well-built shell company is a real legal entity with active status, a valid registration, and an EIN. The check provides no signal against a patient attacker using the gradual-legitimacy-accumulation strategy (aged entity, current filings, established track record). Five bypass methods are fully uncovered: aged shells, entities with no legal registration, foreign institutions, clean acquisitions without visible officer changes, and cross-entity rotation patterns. The Delaware/Wyoming/New Mexico opacity gap is qualitatively distinct: the check finds the entity but the registry record is so thin (no officer names, no beneficial ownership) that it provides a weak signal at best. The stage 5 hardening analysis found no Critical findings but flagged DE/WY opacity and foreign coverage absence as Moderate gaps.

### What it costs

Companies House and the Charity Commission are free. IRS TEOS is free via bulk download. The cost is dominated by the US SOS aggregation layer: OpenCorporates charges for paid tiers (estimated $0.05–$0.50 per lookup at moderate volume), and KYB aggregators like Middesk or Cobalt run $500–$5,000/month. The composite marginal cost per customer is estimated at $0.05–$1.00. Setup cost is estimated at $20K–$60K for integrating all four sources, normalizing fields across jurisdictions, setting up the TEOS bulk-mirror refresh cycle, and solving cross-jurisdiction name matching.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is structured as a seven-step escalation. In practice, most flagged cases will be name-normalization misses (benign), foreign institutions outside the stack's US/UK coverage (benign), or determination-pending US nonprofits (benign). The dissolved-entity flag is the highest-signal flag and should trigger immediate escalation to a biosecurity officer. The `registry_recently_incorporated` flag requires a defined threshold (not specified in the current implementation — 6 months? 12? 24?) to be operationally useful. Officer/PSC pattern analysis from Companies House is valuable but depends on the reviewer routing suspicious patterns to the cross-shell-graph idea for linkage analysis. The check produces a full audit trail: query terms, response payloads, officer snapshots, 990/determination letter references, and the reviewer's adjudication memo.

### Open questions

The form and claim checks both passed. The claim check suggested upgrading the Charity Commission rate-limit `[unknown]` by registering for the developer portal, which is a minor gap. The coverage research flagged that the dominant structural gap is geographic: with Asia-Pacific holding ~38% of the global gene synthesis market and only US/UK entities covered, an estimated 50–65% of synthesis-buying institutions are outside coverage. OpenCorporates already covers 140 jurisdictions; extending the implementation to use this non-US/UK data is a configuration change rather than a new integration, but would require field normalization work. The `registry_recently_incorporated` threshold needs to be defined before operationalization. The DE/WY/NM opacity gap could be partially addressed by requiring direct SOS pulls via Middesk/Cobalt for entities in those states, at additional cost.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.**
- **Moderate hardening finding M1:** OpenCorporates staleness + Delaware/Wyoming/New Mexico opacity create a blind spot for the most popular US shell-company formation jurisdictions. The entity passes the check but the record reveals almost nothing about who controls it. Partial mitigation: escalate DE/WY/NM entities to a secondary KYB service (Middesk, Cobalt) for direct SOS pulls, and require customer-provided officer names.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2:** Foreign institution coverage is absent. The check covers US and UK only. Extending to OpenCorporates' full 140-jurisdiction coverage is a straightforward implementation change but requires field normalization for non-US/UK registries.
- **`[unknown]` fields affecting policy implications:**
  - Charity Commission API rate limit — not publicly documented; needs developer-portal registration to determine.
  - IRS TEOS REST API — none found; bulk download is the canonical programmatic path.
- **`[best guess]` fields needing tighter anchoring:**
  - Composite marginal cost ($0.05–$1) — wide range driven by OpenCorporates/KYB tier uncertainty.
  - Setup cost ($20K–$60K) — not anchored to comparable integration projects.
  - Foreign coverage gap (50–65% of institutions outside US/UK) — conflates market revenue share with institution count.
  - Unincorporated entities (2–5% of orders) — no proxy cited.
- **Undefined threshold:** `registry_recently_incorporated` needs a defined recency window before operationalization.
- **IRS TEOS monthly lag:** recently-revoked organizations (last 30 days) may not yet appear in the Automatic Revocation List.
- **Charity Commission scope:** England & Wales only. Scottish charities (OSCR, ~25,000) and Northern Ireland (CCNI, ~6,000) are complete blind spots. Adding these registries would require separate integration.
- **OpenCorporates staleness:** self-acknowledged by OpenCorporates. The `registry_stale_data_warning` flag is a reasonable mitigation but the underlying lag is not controllable.
