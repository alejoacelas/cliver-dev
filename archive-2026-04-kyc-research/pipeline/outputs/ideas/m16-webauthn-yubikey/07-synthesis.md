## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This idea enforces phishing-resistant authentication by requiring SOC-ordering customers to enroll a FIDO2 hardware security key — typically a YubiKey 5 series — and configuring the identity provider to accept only WebAuthn as the MFA factor, with no fallback to SMS, TOTP, or email magic links. The IdP validates attestation during enrollment against an enterprise AAGUID allowlist, ensuring that only genuine hardware tokens (not software passkeys synced via iCloud or Google Password Manager) are accepted. At every login on a SOC-ordering session, the browser performs a WebAuthn assertion ceremony — a cryptographic challenge-response bound to the relying-party origin — that the IdP verifies. The per-authentication cost is $0; the cost is in hardware ($50–$100 per customer for one or two keys) and in the operations program for distribution, replacement, and accommodation.

### What it catches

Hardware token enforcement directly closes the most dangerous technical bypasses in the account-hijack and credential-compromise attacker stories. AitM phishing relays (Tycoon 2FA, EvilProxy) are defeated because WebAuthn's origin-binding prevents a phishing site at a different domain from producing a valid signature — the relay receives the user's password but not a usable WebAuthn assertion. Infostealer-exfiltrated TOTP seeds are rendered useless because TOTP is no longer an accepted factor. FIDO downgrade attacks fail because there is no fallback authenticator to downgrade to, and the `webauthn_downgrade_attempt` flag fires on any attempt. SIM-jacking is irrelevant (SMS is not a factor). Cloud passkey injection via a compromised Apple/Google account is blocked by the AAGUID allowlist, which excludes synced passkey providers. Google's experience — zero successful phishing across 85,000 employees after deploying security keys — is consistent with the model, though that claim is sourced via secondary reporting.

### What it misses

The idea does not address attacks that bypass the authenticator entirely. Session hijack via stolen cookies does not trigger a WebAuthn ceremony (addressed by m16-order-time-stepup's `max_age=0`). MFA recovery via email allows an attacker to enroll a *new* hardware token — a legitimate YubiKey purchased for $25–$50 passes attestation, since the AAGUID allowlist verifies token provenance, not holder identity (addressed by m16-no-sms-no-email-reset's video-call SOP). Social engineering of provider support can similarly result in attacker enrollment of a genuine key. Stage 5 also identified an implementation gap: federated logins via enterprise SSO may not enforce the WebAuthn requirement if the upstream IdP authenticates with password+TOTP and the provider does not validate the `amr` claim on the returned token. This affects an estimated 15–25% of academic customers who authenticate via federation, with perhaps >50% of federated academic IdPs not yet enforcing phishing-resistant MFA.

### What it costs

Per-authentication cost is zero. The dominant cost is hardware: $50 per YubiKey 5 NFC at retail, with a recommended two keys per customer ($100 total). For a provider with 5,000 SOC-eligible customers, this is $500K in hardware (or passed to customers as a self-purchase requirement). YubiEnterprise Subscription offers bulk pricing, a 25% replacement allowance, and second-key discounts for organizations with 500+ users. Setup cost is estimated at 2–4 engineer-weeks for IdP configuration, plus a meaningful operations program for token procurement, shipping (now available to 175 countries via YubiEnterprise Delivery), and customer training. The ongoing operational cost is the help-desk load for lost tokens, firmware-recall rollovers, and accommodation of edge cases (disability, import restrictions, field researchers).

### Operational realism

The review workload centers on enrollment exceptions and token lifecycle. Reviewers handle: attestation-validation failures (customer tried to enroll a non-allowlisted authenticator), enrollment-ceremony failures (browser/USB issues), downgrade-attempt telemetry (potential attack signal), and lost-token replacement (video-call IDV per the m16-no-sms-no-email-reset SOP, plus shipping a replacement). The audit trail is strong: the attestation chain stored at enrollment proves the key was genuine Yubico hardware, and the per-login signature counter detects cloned credentials. For the YubiEnterprise pre-registration path, the procurement record links a specific YubiKey serial number to a specific customer.

### Open questions

The mass-credential-rollover procedure for a firmware recall (like the Infineon EUCLEAK side-channel disclosure) is not specified — a simultaneous revocation of many enrolled credentials would create a wave of users unable to authenticate. The affected AAGUID list for EUCLEAK is [unknown]. The enforcement mechanism for federated SSO is underspecified: the implementation flags the problem but does not commit to validating `amr` on every federated login or to disabling federation for SOC accounts. Whether the provider subsidizes hardware tokens or requires customer self-purchase has major implications for adoption, especially among international customers in developing economies who face both cost barriers and distribution/import restrictions simultaneously.

## Section 3: Open Issues for Human Review

- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** An attacker can purchase a legitimate YubiKey ($25–$50, commercially available) and enroll it. The AAGUID allowlist verifies token provenance, not holder identity. This is by-design and addressed by sibling SOP controls (m16-no-sms-no-email-reset video-call, m16-dormancy-reidv selfie match), but hardware-token enforcement alone is not sufficient for recovery-channel attacks.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** Enterprise SSO federation may not enforce WebAuthn. The provider must validate `amr` on every federated login and reject sessions without a phishing-resistant authenticator type. If the upstream IdP does not support `amr` claims, federation should be disabled for SOC-eligible accounts. The implementation flags this as a failure mode but does not specify the enforcement mechanism.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** YubiKey supply-chain recall procedure (mass-credential-rollover playbook) is not documented. A staged rollover plan, temporary backup authentication path (video-call IDV only), and proactive customer communication timeline are needed.
