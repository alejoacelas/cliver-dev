## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

FIDO2/WebAuthn order-time step-up is an authentication control that requires the customer to prove physical possession of a cryptographic authenticator — a hardware security key (e.g., YubiKey) or a platform authenticator (Touch ID, Windows Hello, Android biometric) — every time they submit a synthesis order. The check uses the W3C WebAuthn standard: the provider's server generates a fresh cryptographic challenge, the customer's browser calls the authenticator to sign it, and the server verifies the signature against the public key stored at onboarding. Because the assertion includes the relying party's origin and a fresh challenge, it is phishing-resistant by design — an attacker operating a look-alike site cannot proxy the handshake. The provider implements the server side using free, open-source libraries (SimpleWebAuthn, py_webauthn, etc.) with no third-party API dependency and no per-check cost. The authenticator was registered during onboarding alongside the customer's IAL2 identity proofing, so the step-up proves that the same human who passed IDV is the one placing this specific order.

### What it catches

FIDO2 step-up is highly effective against account-takeover and session-hijack attack patterns. It defeats password-only ATO, SIM-swap attacks (no SMS in the loop), and session-hijack scenarios because the assertion is bound to a fresh challenge per order. It catches the "ATO inherits prior IAL2 pass" bypass — even if an attacker has the victim's password, they cannot produce a valid assertion without the physical authenticator. It catches the IDV-session handoff exploit (the attacker completing liveness on a different device cannot assert from the original holder's authenticator). It blocks shared-account misuse where the current orderer is not the person who enrolled. And it blocks email-only ordering (inbox-compromise branch) if the provider enforces portal-only SOC orders, since a FIDO2 assertion cannot be produced via email.

### What it misses

The check has several structural blind spots. First, it cannot detect fronted-accomplice attacks: if a willing accomplice genuinely enrolls their own FIDO2 credential and genuinely asserts at order time, the step-up passes natively. This covers identity rotation, shell-nonprofit, and biotech-incubator-tenant branches. Second, it cannot detect real-identity-throughout attackers who enroll their own credential with their own identity — it provides deterrence (audit trail) but not detection. Third, same-person multi-persona operation is invisible to FIDO2 alone. The highest-leverage operational bypass is social-engineering of the support desk to re-enroll an authenticator without requiring fresh IAL2 re-proofing. The implementation mandates IAL2 for recovery, but the bypass targets the human process, not the protocol. A manual-review fallback that waives the FIDO2 requirement would similarly defeat the control entirely.

### What it costs

Marginal cost per assertion is effectively $0 — it is in-house computation with no third-party API call. The main costs are (a) hardware keys if the provider ships them: $14–$80 per customer one-time, or $15k–$50k per 1,000 customers at bulk pricing, and (b) engineering integration, estimated at 1–2 sprint-weeks to implement the RP server against an open-source library. If the provider relies on platform authenticators (Touch ID, Windows Hello) instead of shipping hardware keys, the marginal hardware cost drops to $0 but coverage depends on customers' device capabilities.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is well-defined: six distinct flags with prescribed actions. The most common operational scenario is `webauthn_no_credentials` at rollout — potentially 20–40% of customers will need an enrollment campaign before the check produces any signal. Shared-lab and core-facility accounts (10–25% of academic orders) present a structural mismatch: FIDO2 binds to a single human, but these accounts represent facilities. The provider must either mandate per-user accounts (changing the account model) or accept degraded assurance. CRO/commercial accounts with high staff turnover (5–15% of orders/year) trigger repeated re-enrollment ceremonies, each requiring fresh IAL2. The synced-passkey trade-off is the dominant policy decision: accepting synced passkeys (credentialBackedUp = true) covers ~80–95% of passkey holders but degrades from "same physical device" to "same cloud account"; requiring device-bound credentials preserves the security guarantee but restricts coverage to hardware key holders. Each assertion is stored as a durable audit record linking customer, order, credential, and timestamp — suitable for regulator review.

### Open questions

The implementation does not specify whether FIDO2 is required as a standalone order-time gate or alongside fresh IDV re-proofing for high-risk orders. If standalone, it does not defend against an attacker who enrolled legitimately via deepfake at onboarding. The support-desk hardening SOP (dual-approval for credential re-enrollment, mandatory ticket with fresh IAL2 evidence, automated audit of all re-enrollment events) is recommended but not specified in the implementation. The CRO turnover figure ("20–30% annual in CRO clinical roles") cited in coverage research lacks a specific source.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.** Stage 5 passed with no Critical flags.
- **Moderate finding M1 (support-desk social engineering):** The highest-leverage bypass is social-engineering support to re-enroll an authenticator without IAL2. The implementation warns against this but cannot enforce human behavior. Needs operational hardening SOP: dual-approval, mandatory IAL2 screenshot, automated audit of re-enrollment events.
- **Moderate finding M2 (synced passkey policy):** The implementation suggests but does not mandate `credentialBackedUp = false`. The provider must make an explicit policy decision: device-bound only (high security, low coverage) vs. synced passkeys accepted (high coverage, lower security). This trade-off fundamentally shapes the check's coverage and should be resolved before deployment.
- **Moderate finding M3 (fronted-accomplice structural gap):** FIDO2 cannot address willing-accomplice branches. Cross-measure mitigation needed.
- **Minor finding m1 (standalone vs. paired with IDV):** Unclear whether FIDO2 step-up is the sole order-time gate or is paired with fresh IDV re-proofing. Affects defense against deepfake-at-onboarding scenarios.
