## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check maintains a curated list of approximately 30–50 known biotech-incubator building addresses across major US biotech hubs (Cambridge/Kendall, South San Francisco, San Diego, NYC, Seattle). When a customer's shipping address matches a known incubator building, the system checks whether the customer's organization appears in that incubator's public tenant directory, which is scraped weekly via a custom HTML parsing pipeline. No incubator publishes a structured API for tenant data, so the system depends on web scraping of public-facing "our residents" pages. If the organization is not found, the system flags the order and the reviewer contacts the incubator manager directly by email to confirm or deny tenancy. The check is extremely narrow by design: it fires on fewer than 1% of orders.

### What it catches

The check directly targets the biotech-incubator-tenant attacker story, which describes an adversary who uses a biotech incubator address to inherit institutional credibility. When an attacker claims an incubator address but is NOT actually a tenant (fraudulent claim), the tenant directory lookup returns no match, and the `incubator_address_no_tenant_listing` flag fires. The manual escalation (contacting the incubator manager) can then confirm the fraud. The check's value is as a cost-raiser: to defeat it, an attacker must actually pay for a real bench-space lease at $1,500–$5,000/month, a significant barrier. For the dormant-domain story, the check catches the specific case where the attacker uses a tracked incubator building without being a tenant.

### What it misses

The check's structural limitation is that it cannot distinguish a legitimate incubator tenant from a malicious one. The biotech-incubator-tenant attacker model explicitly pays for a real bench-space lease, becoming a genuine tenant who appears in the directory. At that point, the check confirms the attacker positively. This is inherent in any check that relies on tenant directories — the attacker's strategy is precisely to become a real tenant. Additionally, the check has no signal whatsoever for the vast majority of attacker stories: every non-incubator address type (virtual offices, coworking, maker spaces, residential, freight forwarders) is invisible because those addresses are not on the curated denylist. The check is complementary to m05-google-places-campus, which covers campus-polygon containment but cannot distinguish legitimate from malicious incubator tenants either.

### What it costs

The per-check lookup cost is effectively $0 (in-memory index against a scraped dataset). Scraping infrastructure runs approximately $5–20/month. The real costs are in setup and maintenance: building scrapers for ~10 incubators takes approximately one engineer-week, and each incubator requires ~30 minutes/month of maintenance as HTML layouts change. The maintenance burden grows linearly with the number of tracked incubators. When the manual review fires, the cost is $8–25 per case (10–30 minutes of reviewer time, including emailing the incubator and waiting for response). The 5-business-day response window for incubator confirmation creates order-hold delays.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is well-designed but operationally heavy. When the flag fires, the reviewer must email the incubator's operations manager and wait up to five business days for a response. If the incubator does not respond, the order is soft-held and the customer must provide alternative legitimacy evidence through other M05 or M18 checks. For JLABS and other invitation-only programs with no public tenant directory, the check defaults to manual contact for every order — the entire JLABS resident population (~520 companies globally) gets the `incubator_address_known_low_directory_quality` flag. The false-positive rate among triggered cases is estimated at 10–30% due to directory lag (new tenants not yet listed), stealth-mode startups, and incomplete directories. This is high for such a narrow-scope check and means a significant fraction of legitimate incubator-based customers experience order delays.

### Open questions

The total number of biotech-relevant incubators in the US is unclear. One source lists 16 notable programs; the coverage research estimates 50–100+ including smaller and regional programs, meaning the curated denylist of ~30–50 may miss 30–50% of relevant addresses. International biotech incubators are not tracked at all. The scraping ToS question (whether incubators' website terms permit automated scraping) is unresolved. The stealth-mode startup fraction (estimated 5–15% of residents) is a best guess with no external source, but it directly affects the false-positive rate.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Structural: real incubator tenants pass the check positively.** The biotech-incubator-tenant attacker model explicitly pays for a real lease ($1,500–$5,000/mo) to defeat this check. The check's value is cost-raising, not blocking. This is inherent and cannot be fixed within this idea's design.
- **Structural: extremely narrow firing surface (<1% of orders).** The check fires only on ~30–50 known incubator addresses. All other orders are invisible. Value is concentrated entirely on the biotech-incubator-tenant story.
- **JLABS directory gap.** JLABS (13 locations, ~520 resident companies) has no comprehensive public tenant directory. The check defaults to manual contact for all JLABS addresses, with uncertain response.
- **[unknown] Scraping ToS for incubator websites.** Searched for but not found. LabCentral, BioLabs, IndieBio do not appear to publish anti-scraping clauses, but this has not been confirmed.
- **[unknown] Incubator directory update frequency.** Searched for but not found for specific incubators. The 4–12 week lag estimate is a best guess.
- **06F flag: total biotech incubator count (50–100+)** relies on a single list of 16 and extrapolation. The denylist's completeness is hard to assess.
- **High false-positive rate among triggered cases (est. 10–30%).** Driven by directory lag, stealth-mode startups, and incomplete directories (especially JLABS). Each false positive costs $8–25 in reviewer time plus order-hold delay.
- **Maintenance burden.** Scrapers must be updated as incubators rebrand (IndieBio became SOSV in 2026), redesign web pages, open new locations, or close. This is an ongoing operational cost that grows with the denylist size.
