## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check queries third-party breach-intelligence services — SpyCloud, Constella, and the free HIBP Pwned Passwords API — to determine whether a customer's credentials have appeared in known data breaches or infostealer logs circulating on darknet marketplaces. At each login, the provider's authentication pipeline makes parallel async calls: HIBP receives only the first 5 characters of the password's SHA-1 hash (k-anonymity model, fully privacy-preserving), while SpyCloud or Constella receive the customer's email and a password hash for an exact-match lookup against their recaptured breach corpus. A weekly batch sweep also checks all active customer emails for newly ingested breaches. When an exact email+password match is found, the session is blocked immediately and the customer is forced to reset their password and re-enroll MFA from a fresh device before any SOC ordering is permitted.

### What it catches

The check directly addresses the cheapest and most common credential-theft path in the credential-compromise attacker story: marketplace purchase of infostealer logs, which the attacker story prices at $5. SpyCloud's entire business model is ingesting these same marketplace logs and surfacing them to defenders. Credential stuffing attacks using credentials from older, widely-circulated breaches are also caught via HIBP. SpyCloud additionally tracks infostealer families and often captures TOTP seeds alongside credentials, enabling escalation to institutional IT via the `infostealer_machine_hit` flag when the customer's device itself is compromised. The multi-vendor approach (SpyCloud + Constella + HIBP) broadens corpus coverage, though no combination reaches 100%.

### What it misses

The check is fundamentally backward-looking: it can only detect credentials that already exist in a vendor's breach corpus. Real-time AitM phishing attacks (Tycoon 2FA, EvilProxy) harvest credentials and use them immediately, before they appear in any dataset — this is structurally missed. Session hijack via stolen cookies bypasses the check entirely because no login event occurs. Stage 5 identified two Moderate process gaps: a 48-hour ingest window between marketplace posting and SpyCloud surfacing the credential (an attacker who buys and uses a log same-day may act before the check fires), and a password-reset race condition where an attacker can reset the password after the breach is detected but before the forced-reset takes effect on the weekly-sweep path. Stage 6 found that vendor corpus completeness is structural — any single vendor likely covers 40–70% of circulating breach data — and that non-Western regional coverage is weaker, affecting an estimated 5–10% of customers.

### What it costs

The HIBP Pwned Passwords API is free with no authentication required. SpyCloud and Constella pricing is vendor-gated; best estimates place per-lookup costs at roughly $0.001–$0.01, with enterprise annual contracts in the low-five to low-six-figure range. Setup cost is modest for HIBP alone (1–2 engineer-weeks) but substantial for the commercial vendors (3–6 engineer-weeks of integration plus 4–8 weeks of procurement). The most significant ongoing cost is not financial but operational: email-only hits will be extremely common in an academic customer base (an estimated 30–50% of academic emails appear in at least one breach), generating soft warnings that could overwhelm reviewers if not filtered or triaged by severity.

### Operational realism

The review queue differentiates three tiers: email+password hits (block immediately, force reset, escalate infostealer-sourced hits to institutional IT); email-only hits (soft warning); and HIBP password-only hits (mandate change at next login). The reviewer's key decision on email+password hits is whether the breach source was a third-party breach (customer is an innocent victim but still needs to reset) or an infostealer infection on the customer's own device (the machine is compromised, institutional security must be contacted). The audit trail consists of per-check logs with user ID, timestamp, vendors called, hit type, breach source, and action taken, cross-linked to the IdP forced-reset event and (if applicable) the order record that was blocked.

### Open questions

The privacy architecture for SpyCloud/Constella integration is unresolved: does the provider send password hashes to the vendor for server-side comparison, or does the vendor push breach records for local comparison? The latter is more privacy-preserving and may be required by GDPR or institutional data-sharing policies. The 48-hour timing window between infostealer infection and breach-corpus availability creates a race condition that this idea cannot close — behavioral signals at login (device fingerprint, geographic anomaly) are the standard complement but are outside this idea's scope. The volume of email-only hits in an academic customer base needs operational testing to determine whether current SOP thresholds are sustainable.

## Section 3: Open Issues for Human Review

- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** 48-hour ingest window creates a race condition for same-day infostealer-log purchase and use. The check may not yet have the data when the attacker acts. Complementary behavioral signals recommended but outside this idea's scope.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** Password-reset race condition — attacker can reset password via email after breach detection but before the forced-reset takes effect on the weekly-sweep path. The at-login path blocks immediately, but the weekly sweep only forces reset "on next login." Recommendation: immediate session revocation and account lock on any weekly-sweep hit, not just next-login reset.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** Privacy/legal risk of sending password hashes to SpyCloud/Constella. The data-flow architecture (server-side vs. local comparison) is unspecified. SpyCloud's "Automated Remediation" product suggests local comparison is possible, but the implementation does not commit to either model.
- **[vendor-gated] SpyCloud pricing:** Enterprise contracts in low-five to low-six-figure range; per-lookup cost best-guessed at $0.001–$0.01. Sales conversation required.
- **[vendor-gated] Constella pricing:** Sales-contact-only. No public pricing information available.
- **[vendor-gated] SpyCloud and Constella rate limits:** "High-volume REST-based APIs" advertised but no specific limits published.
- **[vendor-gated] SpyCloud complete API schema:** Full field list is sales-gated; implementation relies on product-page descriptions.
- **[vendor-gated] Vendor-side query log retention policies:** Not publicly documented.
- **[best guess] Vendor corpus coverage (40–70%):** Wide range with no supporting derivation beyond general characterization. Stage 6 form check flagged this as improvable.
- **Stage 6 form-check flag:** Gap 3 (unique high-entropy passwords) is not a true coverage gap — the check is working as intended for these customers. Included "for completeness" but dilutes the gap list.
- **Operational concern:** Email-only hit volume in academic customer base (estimated 30–50% of emails in at least one breach) may overwhelm review queue with soft warnings. Needs threshold tuning and filtering before deployment.
