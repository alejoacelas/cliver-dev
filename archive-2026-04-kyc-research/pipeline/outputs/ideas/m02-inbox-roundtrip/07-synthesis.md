## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

Inbox round-trip verification is the standard "double opt-in" pattern: during signup, the provider sends a signed token (a JWT with RS256 or ES256 signing, carrying an expiration, issuance time, and unique identifier) to the customer's claimed email address. The customer must click a link containing the token within 24-72 hours. The server validates the token's signature and uniqueness, records the click metadata (IP, user agent, latency), and marks the email as verified. The check uses any commodity transactional email provider (SES, Postmark, SendGrid, Mailgun, Resend) as the mailer — this is a pluggable internal component, not an external dependency in the meaningful sense.

### What it catches

The check confirms that the customer controls the inbox associated with their claimed email address at the moment of signup. This blocks customers who claim an email they cannot receive mail at: non-functional mailboxes, typos in the email field, and casual fabrication. The `roundtrip_bounced` flag catches non-existent mailboxes. The `roundtrip_clicked_suspicious` flag provides a weak secondary signal when click metadata looks bot-like (datacenter IP, headless user agent, sub-2s latency). However, stage 5 found that every modeled wg attacker branch either owns the claimed domain and therefore the inbox, or has compromised a real institutional inbox — in both cases, round-trip verification passes by construction. The implementation explicitly acknowledges this: round-trip is "necessary but insufficient."

### What it misses

The check is structurally blind to any attacker who controls the claimed inbox — which is the defining feature of every sophisticated attacker branch modeled in the wg corpus. Inbox-compromise attacks (credential phishing, breach-dump reuse, AitM, helpdesk social engineering, insider access) all produce genuine inbox control that authentically passes verification. Self-owned-domain attacks (CRO framing, shell company, shell nonprofit, biotech incubator tenant, gradual legitimacy accumulation) trivially pass because the attacker registered the domain and controls its mail server. The click-metadata heuristics are designed for automated bots, not for human attackers using residential proxies or VPNs. Forwarding-rule detection is underspecified — a single-hop forward to a personal device in the same city produces no detectable anomaly.

### What it costs

This is one of the cheapest possible checks: total marginal cost is under $0.001 per verification (dominated by the mailer cost of $0.0001-$0.001 per email). Setup requires an estimated 3-5 engineer-days to build the token-generation, verification-endpoint, and status-tracking flow. The dominant operational cost is the false-positive burden from corporate URL scanners (Proofpoint, Mimecast, Microsoft Defender Safe Links), which auto-click verification links to scan for malware. An estimated 40-60% of institutional customers' email flows through at least one URL-scanning gateway, producing bot-like click metadata that triggers the suspicious-click heuristic. Any provider deploying this check at scale should invest in scanner-UA detection and a "re-click" flow, or accept a high false-positive rate on the click-analysis layer.

### Operational realism

The basic round-trip flow is frictionless for most users: they receive an email, click a link, and proceed. Failures are handled with automatic retries (max 2) and a clear error message. The reviewer queue receives only the suspicious-click and expired-token cases. In practice, the corporate URL scanner problem means the reviewer queue will be populated primarily by legitimate institutional researchers whose email gateway pre-clicked the link — not by actual threats. The reviewer compares click metadata to the claimed customer location and escalates geographic mismatches, but this is a judgment call with limited automated support. The audit trail (token issuance timestamps, click metadata, bounce codes) provides strong traceability for after-the-fact investigation, even when the check itself did not block the attacker.

### Open questions

Two Moderate findings from stage 5 remain: (1) the click-metadata heuristics are too narrow — they target bots, not human attackers with residential proxies, and geographic-mismatch checking is in the reviewer playbook but not automated; (2) forwarding-rule detection is underspecified — the implementation describes a "multiple distinct click sources" heuristic that does not match how forwarding actually works. Neither finding changes the structural picture: round-trip verification is a necessary baseline, not a security boundary. The key question is whether to invest engineering effort in improving click-metadata analysis (marginal return) or accept the check's structural limitations and rely on layered M02 checks for real adversarial detection.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Structural limitation acknowledged:** All 19 wg attacker branches pass round-trip verification by construction. The check's value is as a baseline gate and audit artifact, not as a defense against sophisticated attackers. This is explicitly documented in the implementation and is not a finding — it is a design constraint.
- **Moderate: click-metadata heuristics too narrow for human-operated compromised inboxes.** The suspicious-click detection (datacenter IP, headless UA, sub-2s latency) targets bots, not human attackers using residential proxies. Geographic-mismatch checking is in the reviewer playbook but not automated. Adding automated geographic comparison would marginally improve detection but increase false positives (researchers travel, use VPNs).
- **Moderate: forwarding-rule detection is underspecified.** The "multiple distinct click sources in a short window" heuristic does not match how mail forwarding typically works (single click from forwarding destination). The detection mechanism for forwarding-based inbox compromise needs specification.
- **Corporate URL scanner false positives affect 40-60% of institutional customers.** Scanner-UA detection and a "re-click" flow are needed before deployment at scale, or the suspicious-click heuristic should be disabled (accepting the loss of that secondary signal).
- **[unknown] fraction of researchers with limited internet access who cannot complete verification.** Searched for but not quantified. Marginal for most deployments.
- **[best guess] mailer pricing (SES, Postmark).** Not independently verified via vendor pricing pages; appropriate hedging applied.
