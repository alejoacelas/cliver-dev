## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

This check uses two authoritative US government rosters to gate pre-approval for SOC ordering. The first is the NIH OSP IBC Registration Management System (IBC-RMS), which as of June 1, 2025 publicly posts rosters of all institutions with an active Institutional Biosafety Committee, including the IBC Chair, Biological Safety Officer, and IBC Contact with their contact information. The provider matches a customer's institution against IBC-RMS; on match, the provider emails the listed BSO to confirm the customer is affiliated and operates within the IBC's purview. The second is the Federal Select Agent Program (FSAP), which maintains a registry of 230 entities authorized to work with select agents — but this registry is not publicly accessible for security reasons. FSAP verification degrades to a manual SOP: the customer attests to registration and provides their Responsible Official's contact; the provider emails the RO directly to confirm. Customers whose institutions are not in either roster receive an `ibc_not_listed` flag and are routed to alternative verification via m18 (institution legitimacy) and m19 (individual legitimacy).

### What it catches

The check's primary value is as a gating mechanism: purpose-built entities (from the gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, shell-company, and biotech-incubator-tenant attacker stories) will not have IBC registration and are therefore denied the pre-approval fast track, forcing them through the harder m18/m19 verification path. This does not detect or stop the attack — it merely increases the attacker's cost by denying fast-track status. For the bulk-order-noise-cover branch (rogue core-facility technician at an R1 university), the BSO confirmation provides a governance attestation: it confirms the institution has biosafety oversight and the customer operates within it. For dormant-account-takeover, the BSO callback can surface "this researcher has departed" — but only if the callback is re-triggered on dormancy return.

### What it misses

The check provides zero signal against account-hijack and credential-compromise: the attacker inherits the legitimate user's IBC affiliation, and the BSO would confirm the legitimate PI is real and affiliated. The BSO confirmation is person-level, not order-level: for the bulk-order-noise-cover branch, a rogue technician who is genuinely within the IBC's purview would be confirmed by the BSO, even if specific sequences in their batch orders are unauthorized. The combined coverage gaps are severe: commercial biotech companies (30–40% of customers), foreign institutions (40–50%), and US academic institutions without NIH-funded rDNA work all receive no signal from this check. Only the subset of US NIH-funded academic institutions with active IBC registration and current BSO contacts gets reliable coverage — this is a high-value but narrow population.

### What it costs

IBC-RMS data is free. The FSAP component has no data cost but incurs $5–$25 in human time per attestation verification via RO callback. Setup cost is estimated at 2–4 engineer-weeks for the IBC-RMS scraper/ingest pipeline, with ongoing maintenance against UI changes (since there is no documented API). The BSO confirmation email workflow is the largest ongoing cost: it requires waiting for a human reply (14-day timeout), and the fallback to m18/m19 adds further processing time. For the 230-entity FSAP roster, full manual verification costs approximately $1,150–$5,750 in human time.

### Operational realism

The BSO confirmation email is the load-bearing operational step. The provider sends a short standardized message to the BSO listed in IBC-RMS asking to confirm the customer's affiliation. A positive reply stores the confirmation email thread as the audit artifact. A negative reply ("this person is not affiliated") triggers rejection and evidence retention. No reply within 14 days triggers a fallback to alternative verification. The practical challenge is BSO responsiveness: if 10–20% of BSO contacts in IBC-RMS are stale, a meaningful fraction of verifications will stall and fall through to the slower m18/m19 path. The institution name normalization problem (matching customer-provided organization strings to IBC-RMS canonical names) requires a ROR-based normalization layer.

### Open questions

Whether a formal data-sharing agreement with CDC/APHIS could provide FSAP entity-level data (as opposed to FOIA) is unexplored. The IBC-RMS roster's June 2025 public availability is a recent policy change — the stability of this policy should be considered a risk factor. The BSO callback is not specified to re-fire on dormancy return or on any periodic cadence; without re-triggering, the original onboarding confirmation stands indefinitely, leaving the dormant-account-takeover branch partially unaddressed. Whether the check adds marginal value over m18+m19 for the populations it does cover needs evaluation in the stage 8 per-measure synthesis.

## Section 3: Open Issues for Human Review

- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** FSAP entity list is not publicly available — the FSAP half of this idea is a manual attestation-and-callback SOP, not an automated check. Structural limitation; not fixable unless the government changes its disclosure policy. The 230-entity roster is small enough that manual verification is operationally feasible.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** IBC roster confirms institutional governance, not order-level authorization. The BSO callback confirms the person is affiliated and within the IBC's purview, but cannot verify that specific sequences in a batch order are authorized. Structural limitation of a roster-based approach.
- **Moderate finding (stage 5):** BSO callback is not specified to re-fire on dormancy return. Without re-triggering, the original onboarding confirmation stands indefinitely. Recommendation: BSO confirmation should have an expiration (e.g., 12–24 months) and be re-triggered on dormancy return.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** IBC-RMS has no documented API — scraper fragility. Implement a freshness check: if the IBC-RMS snapshot is older than N days, flag all IBC-based pre-approvals for manual re-verification.
- **Minor finding (stage 5):** Foreign institutions entirely uncovered. This idea is US-centric; international coverage would require complementary rosters (e.g., UK HSE Approved List, EU Dual-Use Regulation Annex listings).
- **4C flag (minor):** IBC-RMS field names are inferred from the NIH OSP transparency announcement, not from the actual IBC-RMS interface. Actual data format should be verified once publicly available.
