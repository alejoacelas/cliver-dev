## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The GLEIF LEI lookup resolves a customer's claimed institution to a Legal Entity Identifier — a 20-character globally unique code administered by the Global Legal Entity Identifier Foundation. The GLEIF API is free, anonymous, and rate-limited at 60 requests per minute. It returns the entity's registered legal name, jurisdiction, legal form, status (active/inactive/lapsed), registration dates, and — crucially — Level-2 relationship records showing the entity's direct parent and ultimate parent entities with their own LEIs and jurisdictions. The check also surfaces "reporting exceptions" — categories explaining why an entity did not report its parent (e.g., NON_CONSOLIDATING, NON_PUBLIC, BINDING_LEGAL_OBSTACLES). A daily bulk download of the full LEI database (approximately 2.8 million records) is available in XML and CSV for offline matching. The check flags institutions with no LEI, lapsed LEIs, inactive entity status, parent entities in concern jurisdictions (derived from OFAC/FATF/BIS lists), and suspicious reporting exceptions.

### What it catches

The check's highest-value signal is Level-2 parent-chain analysis for entities that have LEIs. When a shell company acquires a going concern that held an LEI, the LEI record may reveal a lapsed status, a parent entity in a secrecy jurisdiction (BVI, Cayman, Seychelles), or a suspicious reporting exception. This directly addresses the beneficial-owner-laundering and shell-company acquisition attacker stories. For the dormant-domain story, a defunct institution's LEI will show LAPSED or RETIRED status, independently confirming the entity is no longer active regardless of domain reanimation. GLEIF also provides globally standardized legitimacy signal for foreign institutions in high-LEI-adoption countries (EU/UK entities are well-represented due to MiFID II and DORA mandates), offering cross-jurisdictional verification that US/UK-centric registry checks cannot provide.

### What it misses

The fundamental limitation is structural: the LEI system was designed for financial-sector entity identification, not for research institutions or small biotechs. An estimated fewer than 5% of US R1 universities have LEIs, and 90–95% of small commercial entities that might order gene synthesis lack them. The `no_lei` flag therefore fires for the vast majority of both legitimate and malicious customers, rendering it non-discriminatory. This means GLEIF provides near-zero signal against the dominant attacker population — small shells, CRO facades, community lab impersonators, and gradual-legitimacy accumulators — none of which have LEIs. The Level-2 parent-chain analysis, while valuable, is defeatable: a shell company can file a `NON_CONSOLIDATING` reporting exception (technically correct for any entity not part of a consolidated accounting group) to avoid disclosing its parent, and the current implementation does not flag this exception type.

### What it costs

The GLEIF API is free with no API key required, making the marginal cost per check $0. The only costs are engineering: an estimated $10K–$20K for initial integration (API client, name-matching pipeline, concern-jurisdiction configuration, reviewer UI) and $2K–$5K/year ongoing for maintaining the concern-jurisdiction list and tuning name matching. A daily ETL pipeline ingesting the bulk concatenated file into a search index costs under $100/month in compute and storage. This makes GLEIF one of the cheapest data sources in the m18 stack — the cost is dominated by the name-matching engineering, not the data access.

### Operational realism

In practice, the `no_lei` flag will fire for the vast majority of customers and must be explicitly treated as informational, not as a negative signal. Reviewers who see `no_lei` should route to alternative legitimacy checks (ROR, Companies House, OpenCorporates) rather than treating the absence as suspicious. The flags that do require active review — `lei_lapsed`, `lei_parent_in_concern_jurisdiction`, `lei_reporting_exception_suspicious`, `lei_entity_inactive` — will fire rarely because so few synthesis customers have LEIs, making the manual review burden light. The check produces a comprehensive audit trail: the full GLEIF API response, parent-chain snapshot, name-match details, concern-jurisdiction match details, and the reviewer's adjudication memo. GLEIF is most useful in combination with other m18 checks: it fills a niche that Companies House/OpenCorporates (legal existence) and ROR (research institutions) do not address — ownership-chain transparency for commercial entities in the financial system.

### Open questions

The form and claim checks both passed. The claim check suggested an empirical spot-check of GLEIF for R1 university names to tighten the "fewer than 5% have LEIs" estimate. The hardening analysis raised a Moderate finding (M2) that the `NON_CONSOLIDATING` reporting exception — the easiest evasion path for a shell company — does not currently trigger the `lei_reporting_exception_suspicious` flag. The implementation should consider treating `NON_CONSOLIDATING` by a small commercial entity as a soft flag when combined with other signals. The coverage research confirmed the structural mismatch: GLEIF is a financial-sector tool being repurposed for a research-sector screening use case, with coverage concentrated in EU/UK financial entities and near-zero coverage of the academic majority (~54% of the gene synthesis market).

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **No surviving Critical hardening findings.**
- **Moderate hardening finding M1 (structural):** LEI coverage gap for research institutions and small biotechs renders the `no_lei` flag non-discriminatory. The check provides near-zero signal for the academic majority of synthesis customers. This is a property of the LEI ecosystem, not fixable by implementation changes. GLEIF should be explicitly framed as a supplementary check for financial-sector entities, not a primary legitimacy signal.
- **Moderate hardening finding M2:** Level-2 parent-chain analysis is defeatable via `NON_CONSOLIDATING` reporting exception, which the current implementation does not flag. A shell company filing this exception is technically correct if it is not a consolidated subsidiary. Consider adding `NON_CONSOLIDATING` by small commercial entities as a soft flag in combination with other signals.
- **`[unknown]` fields affecting policy implications:**
  - LEI coverage of universities/research institutions — `[best guess: <5%]`; no published data found; empirical GLEIF spot-check of R1 universities could tighten this.
  - LEI adoption in Africa, Latin America, Southeast Asia — `[unknown]`; no per-region counts published.
  - Aggregate Level-2 reporting exception rates — `[unknown]`; `[best guess: 20–40%]`; could be derived from the bulk download.
- **`[best guess]` fields needing tighter anchoring:**
  - Name-matching failure rate (10–20% of lookups) — no derivation cited; entity-resolution literature could provide a proxy.
  - Setup cost ($10K–$20K) and ongoing ($2K–$5K/year) — reasonable but unanchored.
- **Minor finding m1:** Name-collision disambiguation not addressed in the GLEIF matching pipeline. The fuzzy match may return the wrong entity when two entities have similar names, and the current `lei_name_fuzzy_match_low_confidence` flag does not distinguish "no match" from "wrong match."
- **Minor finding m2:** Rate limit (60 req/min) may constrain batch onboarding. The bulk download pipeline is the documented mitigation but adds engineering complexity.
- **`no_lei` flag misuse risk:** Must be explicitly documented as "informational only; not usable as a negative signal" to prevent downstream reviewers from treating LEI absence as suspicious.
