## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The composite voucher trust score is an internal scoring module that aggregates five pre-existing verification signals into a single number between 0 and 100. When a voucher (the person attesting to a customer's legitimacy for a synthesis-of-concern order) goes through screening, the system collects results from identity verification (IDV biometrics match), ORCID OAuth (proof of a real researcher profile), DKIM-verified institutional email, seniority indicators (years since PhD, h-index proxy), and institutional legitimacy (ROR-based check on the voucher's employer). Each signal contributes a weighted share to the composite. Two thresholds automate the easy decisions: vouchers scoring 80 or above are auto-approved, those below 40 are auto-declined, and everyone in between is routed to a human reviewer who sees a breakdown of which signals are strong, weak, or missing. Separately, an institutional gate enforces that the voucher's institution must pass the m18 legitimacy check -- this gate cannot be overridden by a high score on other signals. The check requires no external vendor in its build-path configuration; all inputs come from sibling checks already in the screening pipeline. An optional buy path uses Persona Graph or AML risk-scoring engines (Flagright, Alessa) for hosted rule execution and link-analysis.

### What it catches

The composite score is effective against attackers who rely on fabricated or thin identities. Manufactured personas (IT-created identities with institutional email access but no real person behind them) fail IDV and lack ORCID and seniority signals, scoring in the FAIL band. Dormant-domain schemes where an attacker revives an old domain and creates a fake voucher identity are similarly caught -- all individual signals collapse. Gig-platform recruits brought in as vouchers lack scholarly signals and land in REVIEW or FAIL. The institutional gate provides the strongest structural defense against shell entities: any organization that fails m18 legitimacy triggers a hard-decline regardless of how the individual voucher scores. This catches both shell nonprofits and shell companies that have not invested in building ROR-level institutional credibility. Community-bio-lab vouchers without scholarly affiliations also generate low scores, creating meaningful friction.

### What it misses

The most important gap is structural: the composite score measures who the voucher is, not what the voucher does per order. A legitimate PI with a strong publication record, verified identity, institutional email, and a recognized employer will score at or near 100 -- and will continue to score at or near 100 whether they carefully review each synthesis order or rubber-stamp hundreds without reading them. This means the check provides zero friction against the highest-risk insider scenarios: PI rubber-stamping, recruited or complicit researcher vouchers, insider recruitment at legitimate institutions, volume-driven inattention, and gradual-legitimacy accumulation over years of legitimate work. Self-vouching (where the orderer and voucher are the same person) is also undetected because the scoring model includes no orderer-voucher identity deconfliction. Social engineering of provider staff bypasses the check entirely. On the coverage side, an estimated 25-40% of legitimate vouchers will not auto-pass due to structural signal gaps. Industry biotech vouchers are the largest affected group: roughly 15-25% of vouchers lack ORCID and institutional DKIM email, capping their score around 65. Vouchers at institutions without ROR coverage (10-20%) face the harshest outcome -- the institutional gate hard-declines them with no appeal path. Early-career researchers, non-Anglophone institutions with unrecognized domains, and privacy-strict individuals who refuse IDV face compounding score penalties.

### What it costs

The compute cost is negligible (under $0.001 per voucher) since the score is a deterministic combination of pre-computed signals. The dominant cost is human review. The original estimate of a 25% review rate yielding ~$2.50 per voucher may be optimistic given that structural false-positives from coverage gaps could push the true review rate to 30-40%, raising the amortized cost to $3-4 per voucher at $60/hour and 10 minutes per review. Setup cost is estimated at $30-60k one-time, covering two engineering-weeks for the score module, two weeks of compliance and model-governance work (model card, calibration curve, bias review), and threshold calibration against labeled historical data or conservative defaults with a feedback loop. The buy-path vendor add-ons (Persona Graph, AML engines) are vendor-gated on pricing.

### Operational realism

The manual review handoff is well-specified. Reviewers see a contributions panel showing exactly which signals are low or missing, enabling targeted follow-up ("your ORCID failed to OAuth -- please retry, or provide a faculty page URL"). Decisions are recorded with the reviewer's note and a frozen snapshot of the score state. Drift monitoring runs weekly, alerting on shifts greater than two standard deviations in auto-approve or auto-decline rates. Model card updates are required on every weight change, and a SHA-256 hash of each score snapshot provides tamper evidence. The adverse-action explainability path is defined: the contributions breakdown serves as the canonical reasoning artifact. One operational concern is that reviewer fatigue from a high false-positive rate (structural REVIEW-band cases from industry vouchers and non-ROR institutions) could normalize approvals in the adjudication band, weakening the check's ability to catch marginal cases that land there for legitimate risk reasons.

### Open questions

The hardening review identified that score freshness and re-computation cadence are unspecified. If the composite is computed once at voucher onboarding and cached, post-onboarding attacks (credential theft, account hijack, dormant-account takeover) inherit the cached high score. The implementation should specify per-vouching-action re-computation or at minimum a time-based cadence (e.g., every 90 days). Cross-customer voucher-reuse tracking is absent -- the same voucher vouching for many unrelated entities is not flagged. The DKIM-institutional-email allowlist scope is assumed but not confirmed against the sibling check's actual mechanism; if that check uses ROR domains rather than a curated TLD list, the overlap between Gap 2 and Gap 4 in coverage research is larger than estimated. Weight calibration (0.25/0.20/0.15/0.20/0.20) is a best guess requiring production data to validate. The "15-35% review rate is typical for KYC composite-score systems" claim is unsourced and could be strengthened by citing a public vendor benchmark.

## Section 3: Open issues for human review

- **Surviving Critical finding (C1):** High-scoring legitimate vouchers who rubber-stamp or conspire are systematically missed. The composite score measures identity and affiliation, not per-order behavior. A maximum-score voucher is indistinguishable from a maximum-score voucher who exercises no diligence. This gap is structural to any identity/affiliation-based score and cannot be closed within this idea's scope. Addressing it requires a complementary behavioral/per-order component (e.g., voucher approve-all rate, time-to-approve, order-content attestation).

- **Score freshness architecture (M1):** Re-computation cadence is unspecified. Cached scores enable post-onboarding attacks (credential compromise, account hijack, dormant-account takeover). Needs design decision: per-vouching-action freshness vs. time-based cadence vs. security-event trigger.

- **No cross-customer voucher-reuse tracking (M2):** Same voucher vouching for multiple unrelated entities is undetected. Suggested: `voucher_reuse_count` signal with REVIEW elevation at >2 distinct customers in 12 months.

- **Self-vouching not blocked (M3):** No orderer-voucher identity deconfliction. Suggested: compare IDV-verified identities, flag `voucher_orderer_same_person`.

- **Institutional gate severity for non-ROR institutions (Gap 2):** Hard-decline with no override for ~10-20% of vouchers whose legitimate institutions lack ROR coverage. This is a deliberate security-for-coverage tradeoff that should be an explicit policy decision.

- **Industry voucher bias (M5 + Gap 1):** ~15-25% of vouchers structurally cannot auto-pass. Sector-specific signal alternatives or weight adjustments for `company`-type ROR institutions would mitigate this but add model complexity and governance overhead.

- **[vendor-gated] fields:** Persona Graph pricing (sold as add-on to base IDV; exact per-check rate requires sales contact). Flagright/Alessa/Azakaw pricing for hosted rule engines.

- **[best guess] fields affecting policy implications:** (a) Setup cost of $30-60k. (b) 25-40% structural review rate (drives the dominant per-voucher cost). (c) Industry ORCID adoption rate of 20-40%. (d) 10-20% of vouchers from non-ROR institutions.

- **DKIM allowlist scope (6F Flag 2):** The coverage analysis assumes a curated TLD allowlist for the DKIM check; the actual mechanism in the sibling idea (m20-dkim-institutional-email) was not cross-referenced. If that check uses ROR domains, Gap 2 and Gap 4 overlap more than estimated.

- **Drift monitoring limitation (m2):** Weekly 2-sigma alerts may not catch slow adversarial adaptation where an attacker gradually shifts the score distribution over months.

- **Bias amplification:** The composite amplifies upstream biases (FRVT demographic bias in IDV, ORCID adoption skew by region and sector, ROR coverage skew toward OECD). Drift monitoring catches sudden shifts but not slow structural bias creep. Governance approach unresolved.
