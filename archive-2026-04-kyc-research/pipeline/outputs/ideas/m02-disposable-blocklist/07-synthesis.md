## Section 2: Narrative

### What this check is and how it works

The disposable / free-mail blocklist is a first-line filter that matches a customer's email domain against curated lists of throwaway mailbox providers (Mailinator, 10minutemail, Guerrilla Mail, and ~110,000 others) and major free-mail providers (Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook, QQ, etc.). The check uses open-source domain lists maintained on GitHub — the most comprehensive being eramitgupta/disposable-email with 110k+ domains updated daily — or the Kickbox open API, which provides a free hosted lookup returning a simple boolean. Domains matching the disposable list trigger an automated hard reject at signup. Free-mail domains used alongside an institutional affiliation claim trigger a soft flag routed to a reviewer, who escalates to downstream affiliation verification (m07).

### What it catches

The check catches the lowest-effort form of identity concealment: signing up with a throwaway email address designed to be discarded after use. This is a high-certainty signal — a customer using Mailinator or 10minutemail is almost certainly not a legitimate researcher. The free-mail soft flag catches a real but weaker signal: a customer claiming institutional affiliation while using a personal Gmail or Yahoo address, which may indicate either a legitimate researcher without institutional email infrastructure or an attacker using a generic email to avoid domain-level scrutiny. Stage 5 found that none of the 19 modeled wg attacker branches use disposable or free-mail addresses — every modeled attacker has moved past this tier by registering custom domains or compromising real institutional accounts. The check therefore addresses a threat population below the sophistication level of the modeled corpus.

### What it misses

The check has zero leverage on any attacker who registers a custom domain, which is trivial (~$10/year) and defeats the blocklist entirely. It also has zero signal on institutional-email-based attacks (inbox compromise, credential compromise, account hijack, insider threats). The disposable-list approach is inherently reactive: new disposable providers appear at 5-50 domains/day, and community lists lag by 1-14 days. A targeted attacker can create a single-use domain that will never appear on any public blocklist. The free-mail flag produces a high false-positive rate among independent researchers (estimated 10-20% of the customer base) and researchers in developing countries where Gmail is the de facto institutional email.

### What it costs

This is one of the cheapest possible checks: static list lookup is $0 with trivial compute, the Kickbox open API is free, and setup takes approximately one day of engineering. The only ongoing cost is list maintenance (automated via cron) and reviewer time for free-mail soft flags. The cost-benefit ratio is excellent for the disposable hard-reject path (high certainty, zero marginal cost, near-zero false-positive rate on well-maintained lists). The free-mail soft-flag path has a less favorable ratio: it generates a volume of reviewer work proportional to the number of customers using Gmail/Yahoo, most of whom are legitimate.

### Operational realism

The disposable hard-reject path is fully automated: the domain lookup happens at signup, and a match triggers an immediate rejection with a "please use a permanent email address" message. No human is in the loop. The free-mail soft-flag path routes to a reviewer who checks whether the customer can provide institutional affiliation evidence (faculty page, ROR entry, ORCID). If the customer cannot, the reviewer asks for an institutional email round-trip or alternative affiliation evidence within a deadline. This creates a clear escalation path but adds latency for legitimate independent researchers. The audit trail records the domain queried, list version, result, and source per check, plus a rejection log for blocked signups that can be mined for fraud-pattern analysis.

### Open questions

The main open question is how to handle the free-mail soft flag for legitimate independent researchers and researchers in developing countries who lack institutional email. The downstream m07 affiliation check must be designed to accommodate these populations without creating an undue barrier to access. The equity dimension (Gap 3) is real: systematically disadvantaging researchers from countries with poor institutional email infrastructure is a policy concern that needs explicit attention. Additionally, whether privacy-focused providers (Proton Mail, Tutanota) appear on the disposable lists used should be verified before deployment — a hard reject on a Proton Mail user is a severe and alienating error.

- **Privacy-email miscategorization risk:** If Proton Mail, Tutanota, or similar providers appear on the disposable list used, legitimate privacy-conscious users face hard rejection. List curation must be verified before deployment.
- **[unknown] institutional email availability in low-income countries:** No survey quantifies the fraction of researchers using free email professionally in developing countries. This affects the false-positive impact estimate.
- **Free-mail soft-flag resolution workflow:** The cost-benefit of the free-mail flag depends entirely on whether the downstream m07 affiliation check can efficiently resolve flags for independent researchers without institutional backing. If m07 resolution is slow or opaque, the soft flag becomes a de facto barrier.
