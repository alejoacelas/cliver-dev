# Stage 8 — Product Prioritization: Measure 10 (payment-bin-giftcard)

## Selected stack

### 1. Stripe / Adyen funding-source (m10-stripe-funding)

**Selected as the primary and sole recommended implementation.** This idea dominates the other two on nearly every evaluation criterion. It reads the PSP-native `card.funding` field — authoritative data sourced from the card network itself — at zero incremental API cost and with approximately 30 minutes of engineering setup (a single Stripe Radar rule). It is maximally pluggable: the field is already present on every `PaymentMethod` object, requiring no external vendor contracts, no BIN-table subscriptions, and no curation labor. The interface is clean — two flags (`psp_funding_prepaid` as hard block with corporate-prepaid exception path, `psp_funding_unknown` as soft flag for reviewer adjudication). It composes naturally with any future BIN-level check if the threat model ever expands to require gift-card-specific classification. The false-positive burden is manageable: corporate prepaid cards are hard-blocked but mitigable via an exception list, and `unknown` cards receive soft-flag treatment rather than rejection. The marginal cost (2-7 cents/tx bundled with Stripe Radar) is well below the stacked BIN-API approach and requires no ongoing data curation. While the check catches no primary attack paths in the current corpus (prepaid/gift-card payment is not a load-bearing attacker method), it provides proportionate defense-in-depth at minimal cost — the right posture for a control whose threat surface is currently theoretical.

## Dropped ideas

- **m10-binlist-stack (BIN classification stack):** The only incremental value over the PSP funding field is BinDB's gift-vs-prepaid distinction — a single-vendor dependency. The rest of the stack (binlist.net is stale, NeutrinoAPI duplicates PSP data) adds cost and maintenance without meaningful signal gain. The 20-40% corporate-virtual-card FP rate against a near-zero TP rate makes the cost-benefit ratio poor. If gift-card-specific classification becomes necessary, BinDB can be added as a targeted upgrade to the PSP-native check rather than deployed as a standalone three-API stack.

- **m10-prepaid-issuer-denylist (Prepaid issuer / virtual single-use BIN denylist):** Hard-block design amplifies every false positive into a rejected payment — unacceptable given the sponsor-bank ambiguity problem (Sutton Bank alone has 140+ programs including Cash App and Ramp corporate). The BIN-to-program mapping required for safe scoping is not publicly available. Quarterly curation lag creates false-negative windows. The PSP funding field already catches the same population with less operational risk.

## Composition note

The recommended stack is a single check: the PSP-native funding-source field. No multi-idea composition is needed for this measure at the current threat level. The PSP funding field subsumes the detection surface of both dropped ideas (all cards the denylist or BIN-stack would flag as prepaid are also flagged by the PSP's own card-network metadata, with the narrow exception of BinDB's gift-specific classification). If the threat model evolves to include gift-card-specific attacks as a load-bearing path, the recommended upgrade is to add BinDB as a single supplementary data source layered on top of the PSP check — not to adopt the full three-API binlist stack or the hard-block denylist. The `psp_funding_prepaid` and `psp_funding_unknown` flags compose cleanly with other pipeline measures (m07/m18 institutional-context checks) for cross-signal corroboration during reviewer adjudication.
