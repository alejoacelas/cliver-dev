# Measure 17 — Pre-Approval List: Product Prioritization

## Selected stack

### 1. Annual positive-verification SOP (`m17-positive-verification-sop`)

Selected as the backbone. This is the only idea that addresses the fundamental lifecycle problem of pre-approval lists: approvals go stale. The independent-contact mandate (sourcing confirmation contacts from IBC-RMS, institutional switchboards, or public directories rather than from the customer) is a well-designed hardening layer that substantially raises the bar against account-hijack and credential-compromise attacker stories — the attacker controls one inbox but not the BSO or department switchboard. The two-contact requirement for non-established entities adds further friction against purpose-built fronts, though it cannot fully block them. At $75–$150 per entity per year, the cost is meaningful but proportionate to the value of maintaining a trustworthy pre-approval roster. The SOP's 60-day re-verification window with auto-suspend on non-response provides a strong default that prevents indefinite drift. The idea composes cleanly with every other selected idea: event-driven re-evaluation triggers early SOP runs, IBC-RMS serves as the primary independent-contact source, and per-order re-IAL2 provides the identity layer the SOP does not cover.

### 2. Event-driven re-evaluation (`m17-event-driven-reeval`)

Selected as the inter-cycle trigger layer. The annual SOP catches calendar-based drift but cannot detect material changes between cycles — an entity acquired in month 3 of a 12-month cycle operates unchecked for 9 months. Event-driven re-evaluation closes this gap for corporate-ownership changes (OpenCorporates), sanctions additions (OFAC delta), breach events (SpyCloud/Constella), and dormancy. Its independent value is concentrated on the acquisition variant of the shell-company attacker story: when an attacker buys an existing company with a live provider account, the officer-change filing demotes the entity to needs-review before the next SOC order ships. The $1–$5 per entity per year cost is low, and the idea reuses infrastructure from sibling measures (m16-spycloud-breach, m16-dormancy-reidv). The critical structural gap — that patient entity construction generates no events — is precisely what the annual SOP companion covers. Together, the two ideas implement full perpetual-KYC: calendar-based re-verification for stable entities, event-triggered re-verification for changing ones.

### 3. FSAP + IBC roster ingestion (`m17-fsap-ibc-roster`)

Selected as the initial gating layer. The IBC-RMS roster (publicly available as of June 2025) provides the cheapest, most authoritative signal for a high-value population: US academic institutions with active Institutional Biosafety Committees. The check's primary function is exclusion — entities without IBC or FSAP registration are denied pre-approval fast-track status and routed to m18/m19 for alternative verification. This gating function is orthogonal to the re-verification ideas: IBC-RMS gates entry onto the pre-approval list, the SOP and event-driven re-evaluation maintain it. The BSO confirmation email serves double duty as both an onboarding gate and the primary independent-contact source for the annual SOP. Coverage is narrow (US NIH-funded academic institutions only, estimated 50–70% of US academic synthesis customers), but for that population the signal is strong and the marginal cost is $0. The FSAP component degrades to a manual attestation SOP for 230 entities — operationally feasible at that scale.

### 4. Predecessor re-IAL2 + re-bind (`m17-predecessor-reidv`)

Selected as the per-order identity layer. The three ideas above operate at the entity level — they verify that the institution remains legitimate and authorized. None of them verify that the person submitting a specific SOC order is the person originally approved. Predecessor re-IAL2 fills this gap: at SOC order time, the submitter completes a fresh document + selfie + liveness check, and the resulting artifact is bound to the specific order. The `predecessor_rebind_name_mismatch` flag is the primary catching mechanism for account-hijack, credential-compromise, and dormant-account-takeover — attacker stories that the entity-level checks cannot address because the entity itself is unchanged. At $1.50 per verification (Stripe Identity), the per-check cost is low. The friction cost (estimated 10–25% drop rate on first encounter) is real and must be managed through clear customer communication and streamlined UX, but it is justified by the strength of the signal: per-order biometric commitment is the strongest identity evidence in the entire screening stack. For own-identity attackers (gradual-legitimacy-accumulation, shell-company build-from-scratch), the check provides attribution rather than prevention — each SOC order carries a biometric commitment tied to the attacker's real face and document, which strengthens post-incident investigation.

## Dropped ideas

- **IGSC shared customer list + member CRM rollup (`m17-igsc-shared-list`):** All seven mapped attacker stories classified as MISSED. The IGSC channel is "rarely used" per multiple secondary sources, >99% of new customers receive no signal, and the internal CRM rollup is subsumed by the event-driven re-evaluation idea's internal history component. The narrow repeat-offender use case does not justify selection as a standalone idea; the internal CRM history check should be implemented as a trivial lookup within the annual SOP workflow rather than as a separate system.

## Composition note

The four selected ideas compose into a layered pre-approval lifecycle covering entry, maintenance, and per-order identity:

- **Entry gate (IBC roster):** At onboarding, the provider matches the customer's institution against the IBC-RMS roster. On match, a BSO confirmation email is sent. Positive confirmation grants pre-approved status. No match routes to m18/m19 alternative verification. The BSO contact becomes the primary independent contact for future re-verification cycles.
- **Calendar-based maintenance (annual SOP):** Every 12 months (or sooner if event-triggered), the SOP fires: the analyst looks up an independent contact (IBC-RMS first, then switchboard/directory/registry), sends a confirmation request, validates the reply's DMARC alignment, runs a sanctions delta check, and either re-promotes or suspends. Auto-suspend after 30 days past due prevents stale approvals from persisting.
- **Event-triggered maintenance (event-driven re-eval):** Between annual cycles, the event router monitors OpenCorporates, OFAC delta, breach feeds, and dormancy timers. Any matched event demotes the entity to needs-review and freezes SOC orders. The reviewer either re-promotes (triggering an early annual SOP cycle) or revokes. This closes the inter-cycle gap the calendar-based SOP cannot cover.
- **Per-order identity (re-IAL2):** At SOC order time, the submitter completes a fresh IAL2 verification. The artifact is bound to the specific order. Name mismatches against the account holder trigger review. This layer is orthogonal to the entity-level checks — it operates on the individual, not the institution.
- **Shared infrastructure:** IBC-RMS serves both the entry gate and the annual SOP's independent-contact lookup. The OFAC delta feed serves both event-driven re-evaluation and the annual SOP's sanctions delta check. The reviewer queue and audit-trail schema are shared across all four ideas. The IDV vendor integration (Stripe Identity or equivalent) is shared with any m14/m16 identity-proofing ideas in sibling measures.
- **Combined attacker-story coverage:** The stack addresses all seven mapped attacker stories with at least partial coverage. Entity-level checks (IBC roster + SOP + event-driven) catch purpose-built organizations via exclusion (no IBC) or change detection (acquisition). Individual-level checks (re-IAL2) catch identity inheritance (hijack, compromise, dormancy). Own-identity attackers pass all four checks but accumulate per-order biometric commitments that strengthen post-incident attribution. The remaining structural gap — a patient attacker who builds a purpose-built entity with real IBC registration over years — requires complementary measures (m18 institution-legitimacy, m19 individual-legitimacy) and is outside this measure's scope.
